## DFVA REPORT: Master of Psychology (Clinical Neuropsychology)/Doctor of Philosophy (080CN)
**Institution:** University of Melbourne | **Level:** Graduate Research (combined Masters+PhD), AQF Level 10 | **Duration:** 5 years full-time, or equivalent part-time
**Assessment date:** 2026-07-02 | **Source:** https://handbook.unimelb.edu.au/2026/courses/080cn

### 1. PROGRAM PROFILE
The Master of Psychology (Clinical Neuropsychology)/Doctor of Philosophy is a combined Masters-plus-PhD program that provides the required sequence for general psychologist registration in Australia while building specialist competence in clinical neuropsychology — the assessment, diagnosis, and management of cognitive, behavioural, and emotional consequences of brain disease, injury, and developmental disorder. The program is APAC-accredited, meaning its coursework and placement structure satisfies the Psychology Board of Australia's requirements for provisional and general registration, with an additional pathway toward the neuropsychology area of practice endorsement.

Structurally, the degree combines APAC-mandated coursework in psychological assessment, psychopathology, and neuropsychological theory with extensive supervised clinical placements across hospital, rehabilitation, and community neuropsychology settings, and a substantial original research thesis examining a question in clinical neuropsychology or a cognate neuroscience domain. Candidates must satisfy both the professional competency standards of a scientist-practitioner clinical training program and the independent-research standards of a PhD, including ethics approval, primary data collection, and thesis examination by external experts.

Graduates emerge dual-qualified: eligible for general psychologist registration with a pathway to neuropsychology area-of-practice endorsement, and holding a PhD that qualifies them for academic, clinical-research, and senior clinical leadership roles. Typical graduate destinations include clinical neuropsychologist (hospital, rehabilitation, private practice, forensic, paediatric), clinical research fellow, postdoctoral researcher in cognitive neuroscience or clinical psychology, and academic/teaching roles in psychology or neuroscience departments.

**Overall Section Exposure:** LOW

**Durability Assessment:** This is one of the most durable graduate profiles in the portfolio. The task categories most exposed to automation — test scoring, routine documentation — sit at the periphery of clinical neuropsychology practice, not its core. The core tasks (differential diagnosis of brain-behaviour presentations, therapeutic intervention, forensic/medico-legal accountability, and original research) are all structurally protected by a combination of licensure requirements, legal accountability, relational demands, and genuine scientific novelty. The dual Masters+PhD structure compounds this durability: graduates are simultaneously registered clinicians (a licensed, accountable, relational profession) and independent researchers (a knowledge-creation profession), which is a very difficult combination for AI to substitute even partially.

### 4. THREE THRESHOLD QUESTIONS
- **Q1:** NO — a well-prompted AI agent could draft report boilerplate or score standardised tests, but could not conduct a defensible differential diagnosis, deliver therapy, or produce two years of first-author clinical output under registration accountability.
- **Q2:** YES — the program trains graduates to own diagnostic and treatment decisions under professional and legal accountability (clinical registration) and to generate original research insight (PhD thesis) — both core to the curriculum design.
- **Q3:** YES — demand for neuropsychological expertise in ageing populations, TBI/stroke rehabilitation, and forensic contexts is structurally growing, and the dual clinician-researcher qualification positions graduates ahead of any AI-tool substitution trend rather than behind it.


## 5. ANALOGUE GRADUATE PROFILE

The most exposed archetype adjacent to this program is not the clinical neuropsychologist but the **Junior Psychometrist/Test Scorer of 2021** — a role focused on administering standardised batteries, hand-scoring protocols, and producing routine normed summaries with limited clinical interpretation. That role sits at the automatable periphery of this program's training, not its centre.

Specific tools/platforms pressuring that peripheral task set:
- **Q-interactive / Q-global (Pearson)** — digital test administration and automated scoring platforms already displacing manual scoring
- **CNS Vital Signs / Cogstate** — computerised cognitive test batteries with automated norm-referencing
- **AI clinical documentation assistants (e.g., Nuance DAX, Heidi Health)** — ambient/AI-assisted drafting of clinical notes and report sections
- **LLM-based report-drafting tools** — compressing the time needed to turn raw test data into a structured report draft

### 6. VERDICT
This is one of the most future-resilient programs assessable in the DFVA framework. The combined APAC-accredited clinical training and PhD research structure builds durable, legally accountable, deeply specialised, relationally demanding expertise that AI cannot meaningfully substitute — even as it compresses the administrative periphery of the profession (test scoring, report drafting). The principal gap is not structural but curricular currency: there is no evidence in the handbook text of explicit AI-literacy/governance content, which is a real risk given how quickly AI-assisted scoring and drafting tools are entering clinical psychology practice. Addressing that gap would move this program from strong to exemplary rather than changing its fundamental risk category.

## 8. THE REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE

The 2027-ready Master of Psychology (Clinical Neuropsychology)/PhD graduate is not merely a licensed clinician with a research degree — they are a **clinician-scientist who actively directs AI tools rather than being directed by them**. They use AI-assisted scoring and drafting platforms to eliminate the administrative drag of testing and documentation, freeing clinical time for the diagnostic reasoning, therapeutic relationship-building, and multidisciplinary liaison that remain irreducibly human.

They bring explicit AI governance literacy to their registration: they know when an AI-drafted report section requires override, understand the medico-legal exposure of unsupervised AI use in clinical documentation, and can articulate this to supervisees and multidisciplinary teams. Their PhD training means they are equally comfortable generating primary evidence about novel presentations (long-COVID cognitive sequelae, digital-biomarker-based early dementia detection) as they are delivering frontline clinical care — a genuine dual capability that keeps them ahead of any AI substitution curve rather than behind it.

They are positioned to lead, not resist, the incorporation of digital cognitive-monitoring and tele-neuropsychology into service delivery, treating these as extensions of clinical reach into underserved regional and remote populations rather than threats to professional relevance.
