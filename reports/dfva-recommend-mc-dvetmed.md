## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

**1. AI-Assisted Imaging Triage Verification Exercise (DVM3, Diagnostic Imaging rotation).** Students receive an AI-generated radiograph pre-read alongside the actual case history and physical exam findings. They must identify where the AI read agrees with clinical findings, where it is incomplete or wrong, and write a corrected clinical report with justification — assessed on judgment quality, not just final diagnosis accuracy.

**2. Governed AI Documentation Audit (DVM4, clinical rotation).** Students use an AI ambient scribe during a supervised consult, then audit and correct the generated SOAP note against the actual consult before it enters the patient record, documenting every correction and its clinical rationale — building the verification habit the D5 gap analysis identifies as missing.

**3. Evidence-Appraisal Capstone (DVM4).** Students select a real clinical case from their rotation caseload, conduct a structured critical appraisal of the relevant primary veterinary literature, and defend a specific treatment-protocol recommendation in a viva-style assessment — directly targeting the D7 research rigour gap without displacing clinical training time.

---

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

- All AI-generated diagnostic imaging reads, clinical differentials, or documentation entering the official patient record at U-Vet Werribee must be reviewed and countersigned by a supervising clinician or student-under-supervision before finalisation.
- AI tool outputs used in student assessment (imaging triage, scribing) must be clearly labelled as AI-generated in any submitted coursework, with the student's corrections/verification shown as a separate, gradable artifact.
- A designated faculty AI governance lead should maintain a living register of approved AI tools in use across teaching hospital workflows, reviewed at each curriculum advisory cycle.
- Students must be explicitly taught the failure modes of veterinary AI tools (e.g., under-sampled species/breed training data, false-negative risk in rare pathology) as part of the AI literacy module, not just their capabilities.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES

- **Risk: AI governance policy adds administrative burden to already time-pressured clinical faculty at Werribee.** *Mitigation:* Design the verification/countersignature workflow to slot into existing case sign-off processes rather than create a new parallel administrative step.
- **Risk: Graduate destination data partnership with AVBC/Vet Board Victoria may face data-privacy or resourcing delays outside the faculty's direct control.** *Mitigation:* Begin with an internal alumni survey as an interim data source while the formal registry partnership is negotiated.
- **Risk: Adding an evidence-appraisal capstone risks perceived curriculum overload in an already intensive four-year clinical program.** *Mitigation:* Integrate the capstone into existing evidence-based-decision-making case work rather than adding it as a wholly separate assessment block.

---

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027-ready DVM graduate keeps everything that already makes this degree durable — deep procedural and surgical skill, hands-on physical examination competency, and trusted client-facing judgment — while adding a governance layer that lets them work confidently alongside AI diagnostic and documentation tools rather than being caught unprepared by them in practice. They enter general, rural, or specialist practice already knowing how to verify an AI-flagged radiograph read, correct an ambient scribe's note before it becomes a legal record, and defend a treatment recommendation against primary literature — making them not just clinically excellent but professionally accountable in an increasingly AI-enabled veterinary workplace.
