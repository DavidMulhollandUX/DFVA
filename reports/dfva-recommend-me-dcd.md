## 5. 12-MONTH IMPLEMENTATION ROADMAP
* **Month 1-3 — Foundation:** Convene AI-in-dentistry curriculum advisory group with ADA/ADC input; scope the cross-specialty AI Diagnostic Imaging and Governance module content with each of the seven Specialty Field Coordinators.
* **Month 3-6 — Design Sprint:** Draft and pilot the AI module and the case-presentation AI-critique requirement in one or two specialty fields (e.g., Orthodontics, Oral Medicine) where AI tool adoption is most advanced; begin practice-model literacy content development.
* **Month 6-9 — Build and Validate:** Roll AI module out across all seven fields; embed AI-critique requirement into standard case-presentation rubric program-wide; launch regional placement partnership discussions; pilot patient-communication-of-AI module.
* **Month 9-12 — Pre-Launch:** Finalise alumni tracking infrastructure design with ADC/specialist colleges ahead of first outcome-data publication cycle; complete first annual AI-curriculum advisory review.

## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-Assisted Diagnosis Critique Case Presentation:** For each major clinical case presented in supervised practice, the candidate must present the AI diagnostic-imaging or treatment-planning tool's output alongside their own specialist assessment, explicitly identifying where they agreed, where they overrode the AI output, and their clinical reasoning for doing so — assessed as a formal component of the existing case-presentation requirement.
2. **AI Governance Reflective Brief (Oral Medicine field):** Candidates in Oral Medicine complete a structured brief analysing an AI-assisted oral lesion/cancer-screening tool's sensitivity/specificity trade-offs, appropriate referral thresholds, and liability implications, using a real (de-identified) case from clinical placement.
3. **Practice-Model Decision Simulation:** As part of professional-practice teaching, candidates work through a simulated career-decision exercise comparing employed-DSO-specialist, private-practice-ownership, and public-hospital-specialist pathways, including contract-negotiation and regulatory-compliance considerations — building D9/D10-relevant career literacy directly into assessment.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
* Require documented human specialist sign-off on all AI-assisted diagnostic imaging and treatment-planning outputs used in clinical training, mirroring TGA/AHPRA expectations for AI-assisted clinical tools in registered practice.
* Establish a clear escalation and error-review protocol for cases where AI-assisted diagnostic tools produce a false negative/positive later identified by specialist review, feeding lessons back into the AI Diagnostic Imaging and Governance module.
* Maintain field-specific AI-tool currency reviews (e.g., Oral Medicine screening tools, orthodontic planning software) given the pace of change differs materially by specialty.
* Ensure patient consent processes explicitly disclose where AI-assisted tools contributed to diagnosis or treatment planning, consistent with emerging Australian health AI transparency expectations.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
* **Risk: Specialty Field Coordinators (7 separate fields) may resist a shared cross-specialty AI module if it doesn't map cleanly onto field-specific clinical priorities.**
  *Mitigation:* Design the module with a shared core (AI governance principles, liability, documentation) plus field-specific modules co-developed with each Specialty Field Coordinator rather than a one-size-fits-all unit.
* **Risk: Graduate outcome data publication depends on ADC/specialist college cooperation and alumni tracking infrastructure the program does not currently control.**
  *Mitigation:* Start with a voluntary alumni survey pilot within the program while pursuing the longer-lead ADC/college data-sharing partnership in parallel.
* **Risk: Overweighting AI curriculum content risks diluting scarce clinical training time in an already-intensive 36-month specialist program.**
  *Mitigation:* Keep AI content tightly scoped to governance/critique-of-existing-tools (low time cost) rather than technical AI development, preserving clinical training hours as the clear priority.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
The 2027-ready DClinDent graduate remains, first and foremost, a hands-on specialist clinician holding a state-protected title and performing irreducibly physical, high-accountability procedures — that core does not need redesigning. What changes is a thin but important layer on top: explicit, assessed fluency in supervising and critiquing the AI diagnostic-imaging and treatment-planning tools now embedded in specialist workflow, a documented institutional feedback loop keeping that fluency current as the tools evolve, and published outcome data that makes the program's already-strong labour-market position externally legible to prospective candidates, government workforce planners, and the University itself.
