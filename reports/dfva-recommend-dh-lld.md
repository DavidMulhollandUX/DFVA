## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
Not applicable in the standard sense — the LLD has no coursework assessment tasks to redesign. The closest analogue is redesigning the *examination protocol itself*:
1. **AI-provenance statement:** Require candidates to submit a short statement on the role (if any) AI tools played in producing the submitted body of work, examined alongside the scholarship itself.
2. **Originality defence:** Where a submitted corpus includes recent work (last 3-5 years), require the candidate to address, in a short accompanying statement, how their contribution exceeds what current AI legal-research tools could independently produce — testing genuine self-awareness of the credential's differentiating claim.
3. **Examiner AI-literacy briefing:** Require examining committee members to complete a short briefing on current AI legal-research tool capability before each examination cycle, so their judgment of "originality" is calibrated against a current, not historical, AI baseline.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Require AI-disclosure as a standard part of LLD submission, with clear guidance that disclosed AI assistance in drafting/synthesis does not disqualify a submission but must be distinguished from the core original contribution being examined.
- Brief examining committees periodically on the current capability frontier of legal-AI research and drafting tools, so "originality" judgments do not silently drift out of step with what AI can now competently produce.
- Avoid over-correcting into AI-suspicion: the examination should continue to centre human peer judgment about scholarly authority, not become a policing exercise for AI use — the credential's strength is precisely that this judgment cannot itself be automated.
- Periodically benchmark Melbourne's examination criteria against peer higher-doctorate institutions to avoid drifting out of step with how comparable credentials worldwide are adapting to the same pressure.

## 9. MEASUREMENT PLAN
* **Leading indicators (12 months):** AI-disclosure clause adopted; first aggregate conferral data published; advisory panel for periodic review convened.
* **Lagging indicators (12-24 months):** Whether legal academia discourse (citations, appointment committee practice, peer institution benchmarking) shows the LLD retaining or regaining visibility as a differentiated signal; whether examiner feedback indicates the AI-disclosure and briefing process meaningfully sharpens originality judgments.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk: Over-formalising a deliberately informal, honorific process could undermine the flexibility that makes peer judgment of "authoritative standing" work.** *Mitigation:* Keep interventions light-touch (disclosure requirement, periodic review, data publication) rather than introducing rigid rubrics or scoring criteria into what is fundamentally a qualitative peer-review process.
- **Risk: Publishing conferral data could inadvertently highlight how rarely the LLD is awarded, reinforcing a perception of irrelevance rather than prestige.** *Mitigation:* Frame published data around the deliberate rarity and high bar of the credential, consistent with how peer institutions (Oxford DCL, Cambridge LLD) position their own low conferral rates as a feature, not a weakness.
- **Risk: The DFVA numeric score (16/36 HIGH RISK) could be misread by non-expert stakeholders as evidence the LLD is a failing program, when the true diagnosis is that the rubric's training-pathway assumptions do not fit an examination-only higher doctorate.** *Mitigation:* Always attach the interpretive caveat from Report 1 Section 3 when this score is cited in any institutional context.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
There is no graduate to redesign — the 2027-ready version of this credential is not a different kind of graduate but a more actively stewarded examination standard: one that explicitly requires candidates and examiners alike to reckon with what "substantial and original contribution to legal scholarship" means when AI tools have raised the floor of competent legal writing, and one whose scarcity and prestige are protected through visible institutional stewardship (periodic review, published data, recipient profiling) rather than assumed to be self-sustaining.
