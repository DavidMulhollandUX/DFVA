## DFVA REPORT: Doctor of Dental Surgery (MC-DDENSUR)
**Institution:** University of Melbourne | **Level:** Graduate Coursework (Masters Extended), AQF level 9 | **Duration:** 48 months full-time
**Assessment date:** 2026-07-02 | **Source:** https://handbook.unimelb.edu.au/2026/courses/mc-ddensur

### 1. PROGRAM PROFILE
The Doctor of Dental Surgery (DDS) is a four-year, professional graduate-entry degree (400 credit points) that leads directly to registration as a general dental practitioner with Dental Practice Boards across Australia and New Zealand. It is delivered through a mix of lectures, online modules, pre-clinical practicals, and progressively increasing clinical patient treatment, culminating in a private-practice-like final year based at the Royal Dental Hospital of Melbourne and affiliated clinical sites. The program uses year-long extended subjects that run outside the standard university teaching calendar, reflecting the clinical apprenticeship structure of dental training rather than a semester-block coursework model.

Graduates enter the workforce as registered general dental practitioners — a nationally regulated, licensed profession under the Dental Board of Australia (AHPRA) and the Dental Council of New Zealand. The degree is an entry-to-practice qualification: graduates are immediately qualified to diagnose, treatment-plan, and deliver hands-on clinical dental care (restorative, surgical, periodontal, prosthodontic, and preventive) unsupervised, subject to registration and scope-of-practice requirements. The program includes a required research-based project, giving graduates direct exposure to primary evidence generation and methodological defence, in addition to the clinical training.

This is a licensed, physically-delivered clinical profession with an intrinsically high barrier to automation: the core value delivered — hands-on diagnosis and treatment of a physical human patient under a statutory duty of care — cannot currently be performed by AI. The degree combines deep technical/biomedical knowledge, high-stakes clinical judgment under real uncertainty, and irreducible manual/procedural skill, positioning it very differently from information-processing or advisory professions facing direct AI substitution.

**Overall Section Exposure:** LOW

**Durability Assessment:** The DDS trains graduates for a task bundle overwhelmingly dominated by physical, regulated, high-stakes clinical work that AI cannot currently perform, wrapped around a thin administrative layer (documentation, scheduling) that is genuinely automatable and should be treated as capacity freed up rather than a threat. The one area of real and growing AI pressure — diagnostic imaging and treatment-planning support — is a tool-augmentation trend, not a substitution trend, because a licensed practitioner must remain the accountable decision-maker for any invasive intervention on a patient.

### 4. THREE THRESHOLD QUESTIONS
- **Q1:** NO — A well-prompted AI agent cannot deliver hands-on clinical dental treatment, obtain valid informed consent, or hold statutory registration; it can at most assist with note-taking and imaging triage, a small fraction of the graduate's actual first-two-year output.
- **Q2:** YES — Graduates own clinical decisions and diagnoses under a personal duty of care from the start of registration, and the compulsory research project trains genuine methodological ownership and original-insight generation.
- **Q3:** YES — Demand for hands-on, regulated clinical dental care is structurally insulated from AI displacement, and AI diagnostic/administrative tools are more likely to increase throughput and reduce non-clinical burden than to reduce demand for registered practitioners.

## 5. ANALOGUE GRADUATE PROFILE
The most exposed analogue graduate of ~2021-2022 is not the dentist themselves but the **dental practice administrator/receptionist and radiographic triage assistant** — roles centred on scheduling, patient intake documentation, insurance/billing coding, and preliminary image sorting. AI tools now materially threatening that adjacent task bundle include: **Pearl Second Opinion** and **Overjet** (AI-assisted radiographic caries/pathology detection), **Denti.AI** (automated treatment-plan and imaging analysis), **ambient clinical scribing tools** (e.g., generalist medical/dental scribing assistants like Nuance DAX-style products adapted to dental notes), and **AI-driven practice-management scheduling/billing automation** (e.g., features within Dentrix Ascend, Curve Dental). None of these tools threaten the registered dentist's clinical scope; they threaten the surrounding administrative and first-pass-triage layer.

### 6. VERDICT
The Doctor of Dental Surgery is a structurally resilient degree: it trains graduates for a licensed, physically-delivered, high-accountability clinical profession that AI cannot substitute at the point of patient care, and it already builds genuine research rigour and deep domain specialisation. Its MODERATE RISK placement reflects evidentiary and curriculum-governance gaps — no demonstrated formal AI-literacy/governance content, no clear evidence of active curriculum refresh cycles, and no published granular graduate outcome data — rather than any real exposure of the clinical core to displacement; closing these three gaps would very plausibly move the program into the RESILIENT band without changing its clinical training model at all.

## 8. THE REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE
The 2027-ready DDS graduate remains, at core, a registered clinician delivering hands-on diagnosis and treatment — that does not change. What changes is fluency: this graduate confidently interprets and challenges AI-assisted radiographic and treatment-planning outputs rather than either ignoring or blindly trusting them, understands the governance and liability implications of AI-augmented clinical tools, and can articulate why a given AI recommendation should or should not be followed for a specific patient.

This graduate also carries forward the program's compulsory research training into an evidence-evaluation habit — reflexively assessing the quality of evidence behind any new clinical AI tool before adopting it into practice — and enters the workforce backed by transparent, published outcome data that lets prospective students and regulators see exactly where DDS graduates land, in what roles, and how quickly, reinforcing the degree's credibility as a training pathway rather than requiring it to be assumed from registration statistics alone.
