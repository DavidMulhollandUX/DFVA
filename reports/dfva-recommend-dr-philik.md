## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-augmented literature triage with governance defence:** Candidates use an AI tool to conduct an initial literature scan for their research area, then submit a written defence identifying where the AI synthesis was reliable, where it required correction, and — critically — what categories of source or community-held knowledge an AI tool could not have identified or should not be trained on. Assessed for judgment, not tool proficiency.
2. **Community-partnership documentation portfolio:** At confirmation and mid-candidature milestones, candidates submit a structured portfolio documenting the nature of community consultation and consent processes undertaken, including protocols followed and any adaptations made in response to community feedback — building an auditable record of relational research practice as formal assessment evidence.
3. **Dual-methodology defence:** At the confirmation milestone, candidates present and defend both their disciplinary (Western academic) methodology and their Indigenous research methodology as a single integrated design, explicitly addressing where the two frameworks align, where they create tension, and how that tension will be navigated — replacing a single-method proposal defence with an explicitly dual-system one.

---

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Establish an explicit prohibition (or tightly scoped permission regime) on inputting community-consented, ICIP-protected, or culturally sensitive data into third-party generative AI tools, developed and ratified with community advisory input.
- Require disclosure of AI tool use in thesis methodology sections, consistent with University of Melbourne's broader academic integrity AI policy, with specific guidance for the Indigenous knowledge context.
- Build AI governance literacy into the annual progress review checklist so supervisors actively check candidates' AI use decisions, not just their research progress.
- Periodically review emerging AI tools (transcription, translation, cataloguing) used in Indigenous research contexts nationally and internationally for their data handling and sovereignty implications, feeding findings back into the annual curriculum review cycle.

## 9. MEASUREMENT PLAN
* **Leading indicators (12 months):** AI literacy module enrolment/completion rate; ICIP/AI governance policy ratified; first graduate destination register published; community-partnership documentation template adopted at milestone reviews.
* **Lagging indicators (12-24 months):** Year-on-year growth in published graduate destinations across academic, community leadership, and policy roles; uptake of the technical-methods bridging elective; at least one signed government/ACCO secondment partnership; DFVA re-assessment score movement from 27/36 to 28+/36 RESILIENT.

---

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk:** Introducing a formal AI governance policy too restrictively could create friction with candidates' legitimate use of AI as a research-assistance tool, or too permissively could create genuine ICIP/data sovereignty exposure. *Mitigation:* Co-develop the policy with community advisory representatives and pilot it with current candidates before ratifying, rather than imposing a top-down rule.
- **Risk:** Publishing a graduate destination register requires alumni engagement and consent, which may be slow to secure given the relatively small, dispersed cohort typical of this cross-disciplinary pathway. *Mitigation:* Start with a voluntary opt-in register and grow coverage over successive years rather than waiting for complete data.
- **Risk:** The technical-methods bridging elective and government/ACCO secondment pathway both depend on external partner faculties and organisations, introducing timeline risk outside the IKI's direct control. *Mitigation:* Sequence these as later-stage (Months 6-18) interventions behind the higher-certainty, internally-controlled D5/D10 fixes.

---

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
By 2027, the IKI-credentialed graduate is explicitly recognised — inside and outside the university — as someone who can be trusted with the two hardest things AI cannot supply: community consent and cultural authority, and dual-system methodological judgment. They can name precisely where AI tools accelerate their work and where using them would breach community trust or data sovereignty, and they carry a visible, publicly documented track record of community-validated research and destination outcomes that makes their credential legible to funders, employers, and community partners alike.
