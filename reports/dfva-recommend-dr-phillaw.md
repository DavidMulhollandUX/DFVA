## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-Assisted Literature Review Critique Exercise (early candidature milestone):** Candidates generate a first-pass literature review using an AI legal research tool (Lexis+ AI, vLex Vincent AI), then produce a critical annotation identifying every point where the AI tool's synthesis was incomplete, doctrinally wrong, or missed a live tension in the scholarship — assessed as part of the confirmation milestone, directly building D5 competency while reinforcing that literature synthesis is an input, not the contribution.

2. **Mixed-Method Research Design Proposal (optional, for candidates pursuing empirical/comparative tracks):** A structured research-design assessment, supported by the empirical-legal-methods short course, requiring candidates to defend sampling, coding, or comparative-jurisdiction selection choices to a panel — building D3 and D7 jointly and giving candidates a portable, examiner-facing methods credential distinct from the thesis itself.

3. **Public Legal Communication Task (professional development, not examined):** Candidates produce a plain-language explainer or policy brief translating their doctoral research for a non-specialist audience (relevant to law reform, policy, and media engagement career pathways) — building D8 relational/communication capability and directly supporting the law reform and policy pathway formalised in Priority 5.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
* Establish a clear, published candidature policy on acceptable and disclosed use of AI tools in thesis drafting, distinguishing AI-assisted drafting/research support (permitted, disclosed) from AI-generated argumentation presented as original analysis (a research-integrity breach), consistent with University of Melbourne Graduate Research academic integrity policy.
* Require explicit methodology-chapter disclosure of any AI tools used in the research process (literature search, translation, coding assistance), mirroring emerging norms in peer-reviewed legal and social-science publishing.
* Train supervisors, not just candidates, on AI tool capability and limits, so supervisory feedback on AI-assisted work is informed rather than ad hoc.
* Monitor examiner guidance nationally and internationally on AI use in doctoral theses (Australian Council of Graduate Research, international law school consortia) and update Melbourne Law School's policy on a defined annual cycle rather than reactively.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
* **Risk: Academic labour market scarcity is structural and largely outside Melbourne Law School's control — no curriculum intervention will materially increase the number of tenure-track positions available.** *Mitigation:* Explicitly diversify the career-pathway narrative given to candidates from enrolment (judicial, policy, specialist practice, legal-tech/AI governance), rather than implicitly framing the PhD as an academia-only credential.
* **Risk: Adding compulsory AI-literacy and empirical-methods content to a research-only degree risks scope creep against the core value of candidate-led, supervisor-guided original research.** *Mitigation:* Keep new content light-touch and workshop-based (not assessed coursework units) so it supports rather than dilutes the thesis-centred model; empirical methods remain optional/promoted, not mandated.
* **Risk: Graduate destination tracking requires sustained multi-year alumni engagement infrastructure that may be under-resourced relative to competing institutional priorities.** *Mitigation:* Start with a lightweight annual survey of the three most recent graduating cohorts rather than attempting a full historical rebuild, and integrate destination capture into the existing thesis-submission/exit process to reduce ongoing collection burden.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
The 2027-ready Melbourne Law School PhD graduate publishes original, examiner-defended legal scholarship that AI could not have produced, while having spent their candidature building the evaluative literacy to critique and supervise AI-assisted legal research rather than being naively dependent on or dismissive of it. They graduate with a documented, tracked record of teaching, mentoring, and public communication alongside their thesis, and with a clear-eyed understanding — supported by real institutional data, not folklore — of the actual range of career pathways available to them across academia, the judiciary, law reform, specialist practice, and the emerging AI-governance advisory space where their doctoral-level legal judgment is precisely the scarce resource the market needs most.
