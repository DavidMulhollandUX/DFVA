## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **Confirmation milestone AI-disclosure statement:** Candidates submit a short appendix at confirmation explicitly stating which parts of their literature review, data pipeline, and preliminary analysis were AI-assisted, and defending what remains irreducibly their own design judgment — assessed as part of the confirmation panel's decision (targets D5, D2).
2. **Model-risk governance case study (Actuarial Studies / Decision, Risk, and Financial Sciences / Finance streams):** A structured assessment task requiring candidates to identify and defend the governance controls needed around an AI-assisted forecasting or risk model they have built, referencing actual regulatory expectations (e.g. APRA prudential standards) — targets D5, D6.
3. **Stakeholder translation exercise (Management / Marketing / Business Administration and Analytics streams):** Candidates prepare and deliver a board-level or policy-brief translation of a thesis chapter's findings for a non-technical audience, assessed on judgment about what to include/omit and how to communicate uncertainty — targets D4, D8.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Require explicit AI-use disclosure at confirmation and thesis submission across all eight disciplines, distinguishing AI-assisted execution from candidate-originated design and contribution.
- Mandate that any AI-assisted empirical or theoretical work be independently reproducible/verifiable by the candidate without the AI tool present, tested at confirmation.
- For quantitative streams (Actuarial Studies, Decision/Risk/Financial Sciences, Finance, Economics), align AI governance training with existing professional standards bodies (Actuaries Institute, APRA) rather than creating a parallel framework.
- For qualitative/behavioural streams (Management, Marketing), add specific guardrails against AI-generated but unvalidated "insight" being presented as original empirical contribution.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk: Supervisor-level variation undermines consistent AI governance training.** Individual supervisors across eight disciplines have highly variable AI fluency and may not consistently reinforce the new module's content in day-to-day supervision. *Mitigation:* Pair the module with supervisor-facing guidance and make AI-disclosure a formal, panel-assessed confirmation requirement rather than relying on supervisor enforcement alone.
- **Risk: Publishing granular destination data may expose the academic-track contraction more starkly, discouraging prospective candidates.** *Mitigation:* Frame publication as transparency that strengthens informed choice and supports the non-academic pathway build-out, not as a discouragement — pair the report with the new placement pathway messaging.
- **Risk: Discipline heterogeneity (quant vs behavioural) makes a single advisory panel structure ill-fitting for all eight disciplines.** *Mitigation:* Run two panels (quantitative cluster; behavioural/applied cluster) rather than one, as reflected in the roadmap above.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
By 2027, the Doctor of Philosophy - Business and Economics graduate carries not only a defended original contribution to a specialist discipline but also a transparent, disclosed track record of how they used AI throughout their candidature — able to show precisely where AI accelerated their literature synthesis and data pipeline work and where their own defended judgment took over. They enter the labour market with clear, evidence-backed visibility into their discipline's real placement odds across academia, central banking, quant finance, and consulting, supported by formal industry placement experience gained during candidature rather than acquired informally after graduation.
