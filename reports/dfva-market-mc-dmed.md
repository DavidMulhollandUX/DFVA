## 2. RECENT JOB AD SIGNALS
> **Confidence: LOW** — This program does not have a live job-ad scrape available (DFVA_MOCK constraint in this environment); the signals below reflect structural market knowledge of the AHPRA-regulated Australian medical workforce pipeline (national intern allocation processes, AMC/AHPRA public reporting, and known specialist college training-place dynamics) rather than a real-time scrape of job boards such as Seek or LinkedIn. Treat directionally, not as literal ad counts.

**Signal 1 — Structural, not open-market, hiring.** Unlike almost every other DFVA-assessed program, MD graduates do not compete in an open job-ad market for their first role. Internship placement is allocated centrally by state health departments via a matched-preference process tied to medical school completion and AHPRA provisional registration — meaning "job ad volume" is close to irrelevant as a market signal for this cohort's first 1-2 years.

**Signal 2 — Persistent national workforce shortages in GP and rural generalist medicine.** Structural undersupply in general practice and rural/regional medicine has been a stated Commonwealth policy priority (Rural Generalist program, GP incentive payments), creating durable demand pull that is not disrupted by AI in the assessment horizon.

**Signal 3 — Rising demand for "AI-literate clinician" framing in specialist college curricula.** Multiple Australian medical colleges (e.g. RACP, RANZCR) have begun publishing position statements and CPD content on AI in clinical practice, signalling that AI fluency is becoming an expected competency layered on top of, not instead of, core clinical training.

**Signal 4 — Radiology and pathology training-place competitiveness increasingly discussed alongside AI.** Public commentary (AMA, specialist college forums) increasingly frames radiology/pathology training choice decisions in the context of AI-assisted imaging tools — not as a threat to the specialty, but as a skill graduates are expected to be conversant in by the time they apply.

**Signal 5 — Ambient documentation AI rollout across Australian public hospitals.** Several state health services (e.g. Victoria, NSW) have run or announced pilots of ambient clinical documentation/scribe AI tools aimed specifically at reducing junior doctor administrative burden — directly corroborating the D5/D9 gap identified in Report 1.

**Signal 6 — Continued strong domestic and international demand for UoM MD graduates.** University of Melbourne's MD is one of Australia's most established and internationally recognised medical degrees; graduate destination outcomes remain consistently strong across public reporting on medical graduate employment (near-universal internship placement, low unemployment).

**Signal 7 — Growing employer/hospital expectation of "digital health" competency.** Hospital recruitment and orientation materials increasingly reference digital health systems, AI-assisted decision support, and electronic medical record fluency as baseline expectations for incoming interns, independent of specialty.

## 5. CURRICULUM IMPLICATIONS
| # | Implication | Dimension | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| CI-1 | Ambient documentation AI is already displacing time currently spent by interns on discharge summaries/referrals — curriculum should treat this as freed capacity to redirect toward supervision and judgment training | D5, D9 | Embed AI-documentation-tool competency and verification practice into clinical placement onboarding |
| CI-2 | Diagnostic/imaging decision-support AI adoption is outpacing formal training — graduates risk encountering these tools first in practice rather than in training | D5 | Add mandatory AI-diagnostic-tool module with simulated error-catching scenarios |
| CI-3 | Specialist colleges (RACP, RANZCR) are moving faster on AI position statements than the underlying MD curriculum appears to — risk of graduates arriving under-prepared relative to evolving college expectations | D9 | Establish formal liaison with specialist colleges' AI/digital-health working groups to keep curriculum current |
| CI-4 | Research pathway depth is currently uneven (Discovery-pathway-dependent) while AI is simultaneously accelerating literature synthesis — risk of the general cohort under-developing critical appraisal skill just as it becomes more, not less, important | D7 | Extend rigorous primary-research engagement beyond the Discovery research-scholar pathway to the full cohort |
| CI-5 | Persistent GP/rural workforce shortage is a durable demand tailwind independent of AI — curriculum and careers messaging should lean into this as a stable, high-need destination | D6, D10 | Strengthen rural/regional and GP-pathway exposure and outcome tracking |
| CI-6 | Radiology/pathology is the most AI-exposed specialist destination — students self-selecting into these pathways need explicit AI-workflow literacy before college applications | D5, D1 | Offer targeted AI-in-imaging/pathology elective ahead of specialist college application windows |
| CI-7 | No granular public destination data at the program level makes it hard to demonstrate outcome durability relative to peer medical schools | D10 | Publish specialty-matching and time-to-fellowship data annually |
