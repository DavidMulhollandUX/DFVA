## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-Assisted Literature Review Audit (confirmation milestone):** Candidates submit a literature review produced with AI assistance alongside a critical audit document identifying every AI-introduced error, hallucinated citation, or interpretive overreach — directly assessing D5 AI literacy while reinforcing that human verification judgment is the graded skill, not the AI output itself.
2. **Non-Academic Translation Brief (mid-candidature milestone):** In addition to the standard academic progress report, candidates produce a 1,500-word policy brief or public-facing article translating their research findings for a non-specialist audience, assessed by both the supervisory panel and an external non-academic reader — building and evidencing D8 and D1 transferable capability.
3. **Digital Method Capstone Exercise (optional, within the new D3 track):** Candidates apply a digital-humanities or corpus-analysis method relevant to their discipline to a bounded sub-question of their thesis, producing a short methods appendix — building genuine technical depth without displacing the core interpretive thesis work.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Establish a clear, discipline-agnostic AI-use disclosure policy for thesis submission (what AI assistance is permitted, how it must be disclosed, and how examiners should treat AI-assisted drafting versus AI-generated original argument).
- Require supervisory panels to complete AI-and-research-integrity training as part of the formalised 2-year refresh cycle, ensuring consistent examiner expectations across disciplines.
- Build AI-output verification into the confirmation and mid-candidature milestones explicitly (per Assessment Redesign Example 1), rather than leaving AI governance as an unassessed, informal expectation.
- Monitor examiner reports for AI-related integrity concerns as a standing agenda item in Graduate Research School quality assurance, feeding back into the curriculum currency review cycle.

## 9. MEASUREMENT PLAN
* **Leading indicators (12 months):** AI literacy module completion rate at confirmation; destination survey response rate and first-wave publication; number of employer partnerships signed for the non-academic pathway program; supervisory training refresh completion rate.
* **Lagging indicators (12-24 months):** Year-over-year change in non-academic placement rate and time-to-placement from the destination survey; AI-verification-editor placement cohort size and post-placement retention; digital-methods track uptake rate across sub-disciplines; qualitative feedback from examiners on AI-integrity module effectiveness.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk:** Non-academic pathway program requires sustained employer relationship investment that may not show placement results within the 24-month window given long AI PhD-to-hire cycles. *Mitigation:* Set leading-indicator KPIs (partnerships signed, program engagement) rather than lagging placement-rate KPIs for the first 18 months.
- **Risk:** Mandatory AI literacy module could be perceived as bureaucratic overhead disconnected from disciplinary research practice if designed generically. *Mitigation:* Co-design module content with representatives from each of the five constituent schools/disciplines to ensure discipline-relevant framing.
- **Risk:** Destination survey response rates for humanities PhD alumni are historically low, especially for those who left academia, risking a biased sample that over-represents continuing academics. *Mitigation:* Use multiple contact channels (LinkedIn, alumni networks, supervisor contact) and report response-rate transparency alongside results.
