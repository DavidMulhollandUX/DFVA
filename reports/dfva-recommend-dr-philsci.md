## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-Validation Confirmation Exercise (all schools):** At confirmation review, candidates must submit a short "AI-assisted vs. independently verified" comparison — using an AI tool to draft part of their literature review or run a standard analysis, then documenting where the AI output was wrong, incomplete, or required domain judgment to correct. Assessed on critical validation quality, not AI-avoidance.
2. **Novel-Methodology Statement (computational sub-disciplines — Maths & Stats, Physics, Earth Sciences modelling):** A required confirmation-review section explicitly identifying which parts of the proposed research programme go beyond what a current AI/automated pipeline could produce, and why that novel contribution matters — reviewed by the confirmation panel as a pass/fail gate.
3. **Public Science Communication Milestone (all schools):** Candidates deliver a 10-minute non-specialist briefing (policy-style or public-facing) on their research at the mid-candidature review, assessed by a panel including a non-academic stakeholder (industry, government, or science communication professional) where feasible.

---

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Require explicit disclosure of AI tool use (which tools, for what tasks) in thesis methodology sections and confirmation/milestone documentation, consistent with emerging journal and funder AI-disclosure norms.
- Establish a school-level (or faculty-level) AI-in-research-integrity policy addendum covering authorship, data privacy (especially for field/human-subject-adjacent data in some Geography/Agriculture sub-disciplines), and acceptable-use boundaries for AI-assisted analysis.
- Mandate that any AI-generated or AI-assisted statistical/computational output used in a thesis chapter be independently validated and the validation method documented — not merely asserted.
- Provide supervisors with guidance/training on assessing AI-assisted work product, since supervisors currently have no formal framework for distinguishing genuine candidate skill development from AI-outsourced labour.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk:** A mandatory AI-in-Research module, if poorly designed, could be seen as generic compliance training disconnected from discipline-specific research practice, reducing candidate buy-in.
  *Mitigation:* Co-design module content with each school's advisory panel so AI examples are drawn from that discipline's actual research workflows (e.g., AI-assisted spectral analysis for Chemistry vs. AI-assisted climate model diagnostics for Earth Sciences).
- **Risk:** Publishing granular destination data may reveal genuinely weaker outcomes in some schools or sub-disciplines, creating reputational or recruitment pressure.
  *Mitigation:* Frame publication as a transparency and continuous-improvement initiative tied to the advisory panel process, with context (labour market conditions, discipline norms) alongside raw figures.
- **Risk:** Adding assessed milestones (AI-validation exercise, communication milestone) to an already four-year candidature risks perceived scope creep and supervisor/candidate workload concerns.
  *Mitigation:* Integrate new requirements into existing milestone review points (confirmation, mid-candidature) rather than creating new standalone assessment events.
