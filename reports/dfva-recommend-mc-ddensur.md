## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-Assisted Diagnostic Case Review (D5, D2):** Students are given a set of radiographs pre-screened by an AI caries/pathology-detection tool and must independently diagnose, then compare against and critique the AI output — justifying agreement or disagreement with reference to clinical evidence, and identifying the tool's likely failure mode in that case.
2. **Treatment-Plan Failure-Mode Defence (D2, D4):** In a clinical case-conference format, students present a treatment plan for a complex patient (e.g., medically compromised, multiple restorative needs) and must defend it against a structured failure-mode challenge from supervising clinicians ("what happens if this restoration fails in 18 months, and how does your plan account for that risk?").
3. **AI Tool Evidence Audit (D7, D5):** As an optional research-project stream, students select a commercially available dental AI tool (diagnostic or treatment-planning) and produce a structured critical appraisal of its published evidence base, methodology, and limitations, defended in the same format as the standard required research project.

---

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Require that any AI diagnostic or treatment-planning tool used in student clinical placements come with documented sensitivity/specificity data and a clear clinician-override protocol taught explicitly to students before use.
- Establish a standing clinical-AI oversight item on the Dental School's curriculum/academic board agenda, reviewed at minimum every two years, to track new tool adoption at placement sites.
- Ensure all AI-assisted diagnostic outputs used in student training or assessment are clearly labelled as decision-support only, with the registered/supervising clinician retaining and demonstrating full accountability for the final diagnosis or treatment decision.
- Build basic AI-literacy content (how these tools are trained, common failure modes, bias risks in imaging datasets) into the AI-in-dentistry module rather than treating it as a pure tool-operation tutorial.

## 9. MEASUREMENT PLAN
* **Leading indicators (12 months):** AI-in-dentistry module launched and completed by ≥90% of eligible cohort; first graduate outcome report published; curriculum review charter signed; failure-mode-analysis rubric embedded in at least two clinical units.
* **Lagging indicators (12-24 months):** Measurable increase in D5/D9/D10 scores on DFVA re-assessment; documented biennial review cycle completed at least once; two consecutive years of published graduate outcome data showing registration rate and practice-setting distribution trends.

---

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk: Clinical curriculum is already at capacity (400 credit points across 4 extended-calendar years), leaving little room for new content.** *Mitigation:* Embed AI-literacy and failure-mode-analysis content within existing clinical and research units rather than adding standalone credit-point load.
- **Risk: Outcome-data publication depends on external careers/QILT reporting infrastructure outside the Dental School's direct control.** *Mitigation:* Start with an internal, Dental-School-led outcome survey of recent graduates as an interim measure while formal institutional reporting is established.
- **Risk: Naming specific commercial AI tools (e.g., Overjet, Pearl) in curriculum risks rapid obsolescence as the vendor landscape shifts.** *Mitigation:* Teach transferable AI-literacy principles (how diagnostic-support AI works, how to evaluate its evidence base) rather than tool-specific operation, with vendor-specific hands-on exposure kept to clinical placement sites' actual tool stack.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
The 2027-ready DDS graduate remains a registered, hands-on clinical practitioner, but now enters the workforce already fluent in critically evaluating AI-assisted diagnostic and treatment-planning tools, backed by a Dental School that can show, with published data, exactly where its graduates land and how the curriculum has kept pace with clinical AI adoption — converting an already-resilient clinical training pathway into one that is demonstrably, evidentially future-proofed rather than resilient by inference alone.
