## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES
1. **AI-disclosure appendix in thesis submission.** Require candidates to submit a structured appendix documenting where and how AI tools were used across the thesis (literature review, coding, drafting), with a brief critical reflection on tool limitations encountered — assessed as part of the confirmation and final thesis milestones (targets D5).
2. **Cross-sector case study defence at mid-candidature review.** Add a structured component to the mid-candidature review requiring candidates to frame their research question within a One Health / cross-sector (human-animal-environment) context and defend its relevance to at least one non-academic stakeholder (government, industry, or community) audience (targets D2, D8).
3. **AI-assisted diagnostic triage exercise with expert override.** For diagnostics/pathology research candidates, add a practical exercise using an AI image-triage tool on a real or synthetic case set, followed by a written critique of the tool's errors and a defended expert override — assessed within the relevant research cluster's methods training (targets D3, D5).

---

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS
- Require explicit AI-tool disclosure in all thesis chapters and milestone documents, consistent with emerging Australian research-integrity norms for AI use in scholarly work.
- Mandate that no primary research finding (data generation, diagnostic interpretation, statistical conclusion) may rely on AI output without documented human verification and sign-off by the candidate and supervisor.
- Establish a standing Graduate Research Committee review point (tied to the annual advisory panel) specifically to reassess AI tool capability in veterinary research contexts (e.g., diagnostic image analysis, genomic annotation) and update permitted-use guidance accordingly.
- Ensure animal ethics and biosecurity approval processes explicitly address any AI-assisted experimental design or monitoring tools used in candidate research, keeping governance aligned with existing welfare/biosecurity frameworks rather than treating AI as a separate silo.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES
- **Risk: Destination tracking system requires alumni contact and voluntary response, risking incomplete/biased data.** *Mitigation:* Partner with University alumni relations and professional networks (e.g., AVA, veterinary research associations) to improve response rates, and supplement self-report with LinkedIn/public-record verification where ethically appropriate.
- **Risk: AI-in-research-methods module content risks becoming quickly outdated given the pace of AI tool change.** *Mitigation:* Tie the module's content review directly to the annual advisory panel cycle (Intervention 3) rather than treating it as a one-off build, ensuring it stays current by design.
- **Risk: Industry/government placement pathways depend on external partner capacity and may not scale evenly across all research clusters (e.g., bench-heavy vs field-heavy specialisations).** *Mitigation:* Prioritise placement partnerships in clusters with clearest translational demand (biosecurity, biotech R&D) first, and use those as templates before expanding to less obviously placement-ready clusters.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)
By 2027, the Melbourne Veterinary Science PhD graduate exits candidature not only with a rigorously examined original research contribution but with a documented, disclosed track record of critically evaluated AI-tool use across their research process, a formal placement or secondment experience linking their research to industry or government application, and the benefit of a program that can point to transparent, granular data on where its recent graduates have gone. The degree's underlying research training — physically grounded, regulator-accountable, and technically deep — remains its structural strength; the redesign closes the visibility and governance gap around that strength rather than altering its substance.
