# COMPASS — Brand Strategy

**A complete brand strategy for the curriculum-durability rating venture**

**Prepared by:** David Mulholland, Associate Director, Service Experience & Design, University of Melbourne
**Date:** 2026-06-24
**Builds on:** [Vision](compass-vision.md) · [Problem Tree](compass-problem-tree.md) · [Stakeholder Analysis](compass-stakeholder-analysis.md) · [Competitive & Strategic Intelligence](compass-competitive-strategic-intelligence.md) · [Market Sizing](compass-market-sizing.md) · [Deeper Perspective](compass-deeper-perspective.md)

---

> **The one reframe that governs everything below.** COMPASS has been built and described as an *edtech tool*. It is not. It is a **rating agency** — the Moody's, the Morningstar, the Health Star Rating for the economic durability of degrees. Every strategic decision that follows flows from that single recategorization. Rating brands do not win on features; they win on **trust, independence, and legibility**, and when they win they don't take market share — they *become the thing the market is required to show*. That is the prize, and it changes how the brand must be built.

---

## 1. Category & Market Analysis

**How the category is perceived today.** There are really three adjacent categories the market currently sees, and COMPASS is mentally filed into the wrong one:

- **Curriculum workflow software** (Coursedog, CourseLeaf, Modern Campus) — perceived as plumbing. Registrars buy it. It manages *how a curriculum change gets approved*. Nobody loves it; everybody needs it. Low emotion, high switching cost.
- **Labour-market data** (Lightcast, Faethm, QS Intelligence) — perceived as a feed. Analysts buy it. It's a commoditising input, sold by the seat, increasingly undifferentiated.
- **Rankings & accreditation** (QS, Times Higher Ed, AACSB, TEQSA) — perceived as prestige and permission. This is the category COMPASS is *psychologically* closest to and the one it should colonise — but with a crucial inversion (below).

The tragedy of the current framing is that COMPASS keeps getting compared to category one (workflow), which is a knife-fight against PE-backed incumbents with $100M+ war chests, in a fight COMPASS doesn't need to be in.

**Key tensions, frustrations, and unmet emotional needs.** The category's defining emotion is **anxiety without an instrument**. Everyone in the system privately fears the same thing and none of them can see it clearly enough to act:

- *The DVC* knows AI is restructuring job families on 12–18 month cycles while their curriculum governance runs on 18–84 month cycles — a 5–10× mismatch — and has no instrument to manage the exposure they're accountable for.
- *The program director* is asked to defend a program's future on anecdote, reputation, and graduate-outcomes data that is structurally 5–7 years stale by the time it clears the loop.
- *The student and their family* are committing $40,000–$200,000+ on backward-looking proxies — ATAR, brand, a cousin's advice — into a market where domestic-graduate full-time employment just fell from 79% to 74% **in a single year**.

The unmet need is not *more data*. It is **sight at the moment of decision** — and the relief, conviction, and defensibility that come with it. The emotional job COMPASS does is converting *dread-in-the-dark* into *clarity-with-conviction*.

**White space.** The defensible white space is not a better tool — it is an **empty seat of authority**. No independent, trusted, comparable durability *signal* exists, and no one owns the standard that would define it. Australia is the rare ideal beachhead: a regulator (TEQSA) already naming AI as a curriculum-integrity problem, 41 universities under acute financial stress (sector margins down to 5.3%), and — critically — *zero* domestic competitor in the LMI-to-curriculum niche and an ~18–36 month window before the global incumbents notice the gap. The white space is a category waiting for its rating agency.

## 2. Core Brand Strategy

**Positioning Statement**
> COMPASS is the independent durability rating for university degrees — the trusted, comparable signal of whether a program will still be economically worth choosing in an AI-shaped labour market, delivered at the moment the decision is actually made.

**Brand Promise**
> A degree's economic future — made visible, and decision-ready, before anyone commits to it.

What we uniquely deliver is not analysis; it's **resolution at the point of commitment**. The same way a credit rating resolves "is this bond safe?" into a single act-on-able grade, COMPASS resolves "will this degree hold its value?" into a signal a dean can govern by, a regulator can reference, and a student can trust.

**Core Proposition — the one big idea**
> **Durability, made visible.**

Every degree carries an invisible economic clock. COMPASS is the instrument that reads it. The big idea is the *act of revelation at the moment of choice* — the exact mechanism behind every precedent this venture rests on: **Carfax** made a used car's hidden history visible at the point of purchase and became the thing buyers *demand* before they commit ("show me the Carfax"); the **Health Star Rating** made nutrition legible on-pack and reshaped what manufacturers produced; **Moody's** made a bond's durability comparable and became something markets *require* before deploying capital. None of them made the underlying product. They made the **signal** — and the signal became mandatory. That is COMPASS's one big idea and its entire endgame: *the signal the sector comes to expect before it commits.*

## 3. Brand Archetype & Personality

**Primary: The Sage. Secondary: The Magician.**

The Sage is non-negotiable — a rating is worthless the instant it is not believed, so the brand's foundational job is to manufacture *credible, independent truth*. But a pure Sage is passive; it merely knows things. The Magician supplies the active mechanism this venture actually runs on: **making the invisible visible at the moment of decision, and transforming the decision as a result.** Sage gives COMPASS its authority; Magician gives it its verb.

**The trap I am explicitly rejecting: the Ruler.** The obvious instinct for a rating agency is the Ruler — *we sit above you and grade you.* In this market that is a fatal misread. COMPASS sells into faculties who guard their autonomy and operates under an NTEU line that sharply distinguishes acceptable *decision-support* from disputed *decision-replacement*. A Ruler brand reads as a new compliance overlord and triggers the sector's immune system. The discipline is this: **derive authority from Sage-credibility (being right, being independent, being citable), never from Ruler-position (sitting in judgement).** The archetype choice is not cosmetic — it *is* the political survival strategy. Moody's authority comes from being correct and independent, not from ruling anyone.

**Personality traits (the seven adjectives):**
- **Rigorous** — every claim traces to evidence or the published rubric.
- **Independent** — beholden to no institution it rates; this is the whole ethic.
- **Unflinching** — names the risk band even when it's uncomfortable; no euphemism.
- **Anticipatory** — reads where the system is heading, not where it's been.
- **Precise** — speaks in scores, bands, and specifics, never vibes.
- **Legible** — turns complexity into a single act-on-able signal; allergic to the black box.
- **Empowering** — hands the instrument to the human who decides; never decides for them.

**Tone of voice.** Picture the love-child of three voices: **The Economist** (authoritative, opinionated, evidence-led), **Stripe** (calm, exact, respects the reader's intelligence, never hypes), and **a credit-rating note** (sober, comparable, conclusive). It states the conclusion, then shows the working.

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| **Lead with the verdict.** *"B-Design: 22/36, Moderate Risk. The single biggest gap is AI literacy and governance (1.1/3). Three changes move the score most — here they are."* | **Bury it in futurism.** *"In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape, institutions must embrace transformative innovation to future-proof the learning journey."* (This is the exact slop to delete on sight.) |
| Use real numbers and name the source. *"74%, down from 79% in one year (QILT 2024)."* | Use unsourced superlatives. *"AI is changing everything at unprecedented speed."* |
| Be calm about hard truths. *"This program is structurally exposed. That's manageable — but only if you start now."* | Fear-monger. *"Your degree is about to be obsolete."* COMPASS is an instrument, not a doom-merchant. |
| Respect the reader's expertise. Frame as *evidence for your decision*. | Condescend or replace them. *"COMPASS decides which programs to cut."* Never. |
| Say "we don't know yet" when true. Candour builds the trust the rating runs on. | Hedge to avoid a conclusion. Academic throat-clearing is the enemy of a usable signal. |

## 4. Core Values & Beliefs

**Core Values (5):**

1. **Independence above all.** The rating is only worth what its impartiality is worth. We will not be captured by the institutions we rate — not by ownership, not by revenue concentration, not by flattery. This is simultaneously our ethic and our moat. (It is also our hardest near-term problem — see §6/§7.)
2. **Evidence over assertion.** Nothing is scored that can't be traced to a cited source or the published rubric. We'd rather show a defensible 22 than a flattering 30.
3. **Legibility.** A signal no one can act on isn't a signal. We compress genuine complexity into a clear, comparable mark — and we publish how it's calculated. Authority through transparency, not mystery.
4. **Candour.** We name the band plainly, including CRITICAL. The value of a rating that only ever says "fine" is zero. We are kind in tone and unflinching in substance.
5. **In service of the human decision.** We are the instrument, never the verdict. COMPASS arms the dean, the director, the student — it does not overrule them. (Brand value *and* NTEU survival line, by design.)

**Foundational beliefs — what we hold true about the world:**

- **A degree is an economic instrument, and like any instrument its future value can — and should — be rated.** The discomfort the sector feels about saying this out loud is precisely the gap we exist to close.
- **Compliance is a floor, not a signal.** A degree can be fully accredited and quietly economically obsolete. Accreditation tells you a program is *allowed* to exist; it is silent on whether it *should*.
- **Rigour and relevance are different axes.** A program can be intellectually excellent and economically irrelevant. *Rigorous-but-irrelevant* is a real failure mode the current system can't even see.
- **The signal already exists — it just arrives too late and at the wrong moment.** Employer dissatisfaction, graduate outcomes, skills shortages: the market is shouting. It arrives 5–7 years stale, after graduation, when nothing can be changed. **Information at the wrong time is the same as no information.**
- **You cannot predict the future, but you can read where a system is heading from the beliefs holding it in place.** This is the venture's intellectual spine and a genuinely differentiated point of view in a market drowning in confident forecasts.

## 5. Brand Story & Narrative

**Origin story angle — "built from inside the building."** COMPASS was not born in a Silicon Valley lab pitching disruption at universities from the outside. It was born *inside* one — at the University of Melbourne, out of service design and CX practice, by people who watched the broken feedback loop from within the system they're trying to fix. That insider provenance is a strategic asset most edtech entrants would kill for: it converts "another vendor selling to academia" into "one of us, who finally built the instrument we all needed." The founding posture is captured in the workbook's own line — *"We are not asking for permission to build. We are asking for help to build the right thing."* That is the brand's origin voice: humble about the answer, certain about the question.

**Customer transformation (before → after).**

*Before:* A program director is told to defend a degree's future in a review meeting. She has reputation, anecdote, and graduate data half a decade old. She suspects the program is exposed but can't prove it, can't size it, and can't say what to do about it. The dean above her has the same problem across forty programs and no portfolio view. The student choosing that degree has even less — a brochure and an ATAR cutoff. Everyone is committing real money and real years on faith.

*After:* The same program arrives at the meeting with a COMPASS rating — a 22/36, Moderate Risk, with the weakest dimension named (AI literacy, 1.1/3) and the three highest-leverage interventions ranked. The conversation shifts from *"is this a problem?"* (anecdotal, defensive, political) to *"here's the exposure, here's the fix, here's the timeline"* (evidenced, forward, governable). The dean has a portfolio heat-map. The student has a signal they can trust before they sign. **The unanswerable question became an act-on-able one.**

**Emotional through-line.** From **dread-in-the-dark to clarity-with-conviction.** The deepest human truth in this market is that everyone — dean, director, student, regulator — quietly fears the degree is becoming obsolete, and the fear has nowhere to go because no one can *see* it well enough to act. COMPASS's emotional gift is **sight**, and the courage that sight unlocks. (Note the brand boundary: the emotion we trade in is *clarity and agency*, never *fear*. The moment COMPASS becomes a fear-merchant — "AI is coming for your degree!" — it forfeits the sober authority a rating depends on. We sell the flashlight, not the monster.)

## 6. Differentiation & Moat

**Visually.** Reject the entire edtech visual vocabulary — the friendly blobs, the purple gradients, the rounded-everything, the stock photo of three students laughing at one laptop. That language signals "software vendor" and undercuts authority. Instead, adopt **instrument-grade trust**: the visual world of ratings, indices, and finance, but warmer and more legible than a Bloomberg terminal — closer to Morningstar's clarity or Stripe's restraint. The **rating itself is the hero visual asset** — a single, instantly legible mark (the band + score) engineered to be recognised, displayed, and eventually *demanded*, the way a Health Star or a credit grade is. If institutions one day put "COMPASS-rated" on a program page, that mark is the brand.

**Verbally.** Own the **band vocabulary** — RESILIENT / MODERATE / HIGH / CRITICAL is COMPASS's equivalent of AAA→junk, and it's already half-built. It is a proprietary, repeatable, ownable verbal system. Speak in verdicts-then-evidence. The phrase to plant and own is **"durability."** Nobody else in the category owns it.

**Strategically.** The moat is not the software — a workflow tool can be cloned by a PE-backed incumbent in two quarters. The moat is the **standard and the benchmark dataset**, which compound into a flywheel no feature-clone can catch:

- The **durability standard** itself, defended (like NABERS, B Corp, the Health Star Rating) by *legitimacy and citation*, not data scale — the single most defensible asset a small Australian team can actually win.
- The **cross-institutional benchmark dataset** — the *output* data (durability scores across the sector), which is path-dependent, first-mover-sensitive, and cannot be retroactively manufactured. This is the only true network-effect moat, and it couples with the standard into a flywheel: more ratings → more authority → more demand to be rated.
- **ANZ-regulatory nativeness** — every competitor is US-centric; TEQSA/AQF/QILT-native positioning is uncopyable from the outside.

**What we are deliberately "anti":**

- **Anti-doom.** We are not selling AI panic. We are the calm instrument in a noisy, anxious room.
- **Anti-vanity-ranking.** We are not QS or THE. We do not measure prestige; we measure durability. (A sandstone brand can score CRITICAL; a regional program can score RESILIENT. That inversion *is* the differentiation.)
- **Anti-black-box.** Our authority comes from being *legible and citable*, not from a proprietary mystery score. We publish the rubric.
- **Anti-workflow.** We are not Coursedog. We are not the plumbing; we are the signal that runs through it. *"Coursedog automates how curriculum gets approved; COMPASS rates whether it should exist."*
- **Anti-replacement.** We never decide. We inform the human who decides. (The line that keeps us on the right side of the NTEU — and on the right side of our own ethics.)

**The honest brand risk to resolve (because a rating agency lives or dies on it):** COMPASS's entire moat is independence, yet it is incubated inside one university. *Can a UoM-born tool credibly rate UoM's competitors?* Until this is structurally answered — through governance, an independent advisory board, eventual spin-out, or a peak-body partnership — independence is a claim, not a fact. This is the single most important brand-equity decision the venture faces, and it should be treated as a P0, not a footnote.

## 7. Strategic Recommendations

**Naming direction.** Three decisive calls:

1. **Separate the brand from the rating — and make the rating the hero, the way Morningstar owns "the star rating" and Moody's owns "Aaa."** COMPASS = the platform/company. The *signal* needs a sayable public name. **"DFVA" must be retired as an external term** — "what's your DFVA score?" is unsayable and technical; keep it as the internal engine name only. Elevate **"Durability Rating"** (and the band names) as the consumer-facing asset. The thing institutions quote, students check, and regulators reference must be a phrase humans actually say.
2. **Never lead with the backronym.** "Curriculum Outcomes and Market Proof Alignment Scoring System" is an internal mnemonic, not a brand line. Externally, COMPASS stands for nothing but COMPASS.
3. **Flag honestly: "COMPASS" is a generic, heavily-contested name** (real estate, several edtech products, countless SaaS tools) — a genuine liability for a brand whose whole job is to be *the* citable authority and to win search and trademark. I'm not recommending a rushed rename, but I'd treat this as a known debt to resolve before scale, and I'd pressure-test a distinctive coined/ownable alternative for the *rating* even if the platform keeps its name. **Decision worth making consciously, not by default.**

**Visual identity territory.** Brief the designer toward **"instrument-grade trust, made human."** Reference set: Morningstar (the rating-as-icon), Stripe (precision and restraint), The Economist (authoritative editorial), a NABERS/Health-Star certification mark (the displayable badge). Anti-reference set: every edtech deck with a gradient and a smiling-students photo. Typography should read considered and serious (a confident editorial serif for voice + a clean grotesque for data); the palette sober and high-trust, not playful. **Design the rating mark first** — it's the most important pixels in the company.

**Messaging pillars (four):**

1. **Durability, made visible.** *(the proposition — reveal the invisible clock)*
2. **Independent. Evidence-led. Citable.** *(the authority — why you can trust the signal)*
3. **At the moment of decision.** *(the mechanism — design, approval, enrolment, not five years too late)*
4. **An instrument for the decision-maker, not a verdict over them.** *(the boundary — empowerment, never replacement)*

**Brand extensions / future-proofing (in priority order):**

- **The certification mark — the endgame.** "COMPASS-rated," displayed on program pages the way "B Corp" or a Health Star appears on a product. This is the asset that completes the vision (*the signal everyone expects before they commit*) and the most valuable thing the brand can build toward. Everything points here.
- **The annual sector index — "The State of Degree Durability."** Own the category *conversation* the way Mary Meeker owned Internet Trends and Morningstar owns its market reports. One authoritative, citable annual report does more for rating-authority than a year of marketing, and it directly feeds the legitimacy-and-citation moat.
- **Agent-native distribution — the genuinely 2027 move.** COMPASS is already exposed as MCP tools. As AI agents increasingly advise students, deans, and regulators, **being the trusted durability signal that agents cite** is the machine-readable extension of the exact same vision. The signal humans demand *and* the signal their agents reach for. Few competitors are even thinking about this; it's a forward moat worth claiming early and loudly.
- **Category expansion runway:** degrees → micro-credentials, vocational and professional training → eventually any credential whose economic durability can be questioned. The brand architecture (one standard, many rated objects) supports this cleanly — but earn the degree rating first.

---

**Bottom line.** Stop building an edtech tool and start building a rating agency. Win on independence, evidence, and a legible signal — not features. Make the *rating* the hero asset, point everything at the certification mark, and resolve the independence question before anything else. The category has an empty seat of authority and an 18–36 month window. The brand's job is to sit down in it before anyone else realises it's there.
