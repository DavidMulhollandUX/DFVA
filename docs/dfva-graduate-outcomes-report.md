## Executive Summary

The DFVA assessments are analytically strong but rest on one acknowledged weakness: their labour-market evidence is **synthetic**. The market-intelligence sections state it plainly in their own headers — signals "constructed from documented labour market trends", "Pattern-based from known discourse", sourced from global aggregates (WEF Future of Jobs, LinkedIn Workforce Reports, Stack Overflow). This is defensible as a forward-looking judgement, but it is not evidence a faculty board can audit, and it is the part of every report most exposed to the challenge "how do you actually know?".

The University already holds the answer. Its **Job Insights Reports** — published on Careers Online and built from the LiveAlumni employment-data platform — record where graduates of **141 UoM programs actually went between 2015 and 2025**: real job titles by career stage, named employers, and skills in use, with sample sizes. Across the set this is **29,859 alumni** (median sample n=90; 61 reports with n≥100). This report consolidates that data, maps it onto the 41-program DFVA portfolio, and quantifies what it changes.

The headline is specific and governance-relevant. The Portfolio Intelligence Report identifies **D10 Outcome Evidence** as "the most governance-diagnostic dimension in the portfolio" (16 programs score 1/3; the entire Science master's cluster uniformly so) and its **Recommendation 3** calls for building a "Centralised Graduate Outcome Intelligence System" to fix it. **That system's core data already exists** — these Job Insights Reports are exactly the role-title, employer, and destination evidence the recommendation asks for. Folding it in:

- **12 programs' D10 scores have a documented basis to rise**, and **2 reclassify risk band on outcome evidence alone**: **Master of Computer Science 27→29 (MODERATE RISK → RESILIENT)** and **Master of Information Systems 18→20 (HIGH RISK → MODERATE RISK)** — the latter the program the Portfolio Report singles out as the institution's "greatest embarrassment".
- The data also reveals **portfolio-level destination structure invisible to per-program synthetic signals**: a Big-Four consulting funnel, a research-retention counter-pole, and a set of cross-faculty skills that are the real connective tissue of UoM employability.

The recommendation of this report is not to recompute scores automatically. It is to treat the Job Insights Reports as the **evidence base** that converts DFVA's market layer from *asserted* to *cited*, and to surface — for human re-review — the specific scores and bands that the real data now supports.

## 1. The problem: synthetic market evidence

Every DFVA market-intelligence report carries a confidence note that, read closely, is a disclaimer. The Master of Computer Science market report is representative:

> "Job-family profiles, hiring signals, and discussion themes below are constructed from documented labour market trends… Sources: WEF Future of Jobs 2025, LinkedIn Workforce Reports Q1 2026, Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025."

The reports then present precise-sounding figures — "ML Engineer roles +35% YoY ANZ", "Quantum computing roles… 47 postings Q1 2026 (Seek)", "AI Governance Analyst — 34+ new ANZ postings in Q1 2026". None of these are verifiable, and the reports do not claim they are. The risk is twofold: the precision implies an evidence base that does not exist, and the **synthetic signal misses real structure**.

The Master of Computer Science is the clean illustration. Its synthetic market report fabricates a quantum-computing statistic but **never mentions the most distinctive real fact about where its graduates go**: proprietary quantitative trading. Optiver, IMC Trading and Akuna Capital — three of the world's largest trading firms — are top real employers of MC-CS alumni, and "Quantitative Trader" is a real early-career title. The synthetic report manufactured a number and missed an entire destination cluster sitting in the University's own data.

This is the pattern across the portfolio: synthetic evidence trades real signal for false precision. The fix is not better synthetic data — it is the real data the University already publishes.

## 4. Beyond scoring: what the real data reveals

Synthetic, per-program signals cannot see structure that only emerges across the whole portfolio. Analysing the Job Insights destinations as a network (employers and skills shared across programs) surfaces four findings.

### 4.1 The Big-Four consulting funnel
Nineteen programs across five faculties list **Deloitte** as a top employer; the same holds for KPMG, EY and PwC. These wildly different degrees converge on a narrow band of roles, with **"Business Analyst" the single most common entry title** across the cohort, alongside Data Analyst, Graduate Analyst and Management Consultant. Big-Four consulting is a **role-convergence funnel** — and it funnels graduates into precisely the junior-analyst tier that DFVA's D1 Automation Exposure dimension flags as most exposed to AI substitution.

### 4.2 The research-retention counter-pole
At the opposite end, the **University of Melbourne itself is a top employer for 30 programs across eight faculties**, with **"Research Assistant" the dominant role** (plus Research Officer, Laboratory Technician). This is the academic on-ramp — Science and Health graduates staying on as research staff. These roles are the **least** automatable (irreducible research work) but the most precarious and PhD-dependent. The two hubs are the two poles of DFVA's exposure axis: Big-Four → high-exposure / well-paid; UoM-research → low-exposure / precarious.

### 4.3 Cross-faculty skill bridges
The skills that span the **most** programs are not technical specialisms but **Data Analysis (21 programs), Project Management (17), Research (19), Leadership and Stakeholder Management**. Python spans only seven programs; Data Analysis spans 21. The connective tissue of UoM employability is exactly the durable, cross-disciplinary, least-automatable capability the DFVA rubric prizes — and the Portfolio Report's §5 finds the same skills rising on the synthetic side. Real and synthetic evidence converge here, which strengthens both.

### 4.4 Destination as an independent exposure signal
The deepest implication: **which hub a program feeds is itself an exposure datapoint, independent of curriculum.** A program whose modal destination is "Business Analyst at a Big-Four firm" carries real exposure its syllabus alone may not reveal; one whose graduates become Research Scientists carries less. DFVA currently scores the curriculum; the Job Insights data argues a program's **actual destination roles** should pull on the D1 and D10 scores — and the Big-Four funnel is a portfolio-wide signal, not a per-program one.

## 5. Coverage and matching

The 141 Job Insights Reports map onto the 41 DFVA programs as follows:

| Match tier | DFVA programs | Meaning |
|---|---:|---|
| Exact same-name | **16** | A Job Insights Report exists for the identical degree — drop-in real data |
| Cognate | 7 | Same field, different level/specialisation (e.g. Master of Science (Physics) ↔ Bachelor of Science (Physics); Master of Physiotherapy ↔ Doctor of Physiotherapy) |
| Partial | 8 | A related-field proxy exists (e.g. Master of Business Analytics ↔ Master of Management) |
| None | 10 | No good UoM proxy (e.g. Master of Screenwriting, Master of Climate Science) |

**16 programs have exact real data today; 23 have an exact or cognate match; 31 of 41 (76%) have at least a related proxy.** The exact-16 list is reliable and can be wired in immediately. Cognate and partial matches require a human glance per row before use — the field is right but the level or specialisation differs, and that caveat must travel with the data. The 10 unmatched programs should say so explicitly rather than fall back on synthetic signals dressed as evidence.

## 6. Caveats and limitations

- **LiveAlumni coverage is partial.** Each report states it is based on data of *N* alumni "who studied this degree… (not all alumni)". *n* is a floor, and skews toward alumni with discoverable professional profiles.
- **AI-processed, human-curated.** Titles, employers and skills were extracted by a tool then curated by Careers staff — high quality, but not an audited dataset.
- **Backward-looking.** These are where graduates *went* (2015–2025), partly before AI disruption. DFVA is forward-looking to 2027+. The correct use is **Job Insights as the evidence base, DFVA's analysis as the projection** — ground the forward judgement in the real destinations; do not let historical outcomes override the automation-exposure call.
- **Matching is heuristic** for cognate/partial tiers — verify per row before publishing a score change.
- **"Research Assistant" straddles the exposure line** — irreducible if it launches a research career, automatable if it is a holding pattern of literature triage and data entry. The data alone cannot distinguish the two.
- **Provenance.** The reports sit behind UoM SSO and are internal careers material; retain the provenance line and confirm redistribution norms before any external publication.
