# Evidura — Deep-Dive: Benchmark Dataset Never Reaches Critical Mass

> Failure mode analysis: the benchmark dataset flywheel stalls and international expansion collapses.
> **Date:** 2026-06-30 · **Based on:** [Business model](evidura-business-model.md) · [Market sizing](compass-market-sizing.md) · [Competitive intelligence](compass-competitive-strategic-intelligence.md)

---

## 1. The Failure Story

**Year 1–2 plays out roughly to plan.** Melbourne pilot validates the DFVA methodology. Five Go8 design partners come aboard. The *State of Degree Durability* index launches to polite sector attention. By mid-Year 3, Evidura has 24 Australian institutions on paid subscriptions — slightly under the 26-institution target, but credible. The Australian revenue base is real: ~$600K ARR from institutional subscriptions, accreditation conversations are progressing, and the team has just spun out as an independent entity. Momentum feels genuine.

Then international expansion stalls, and it stalls because of something nobody said out loud during the Go8 pilot: **24 Australian institutions do not constitute a benchmark dataset.** They constitute 24 data points. A benchmark dataset — the kind a UK pro-vice-chancellor pays $49,000 to access — needs hundreds of rated programs across multiple national systems, disciplines, and regulatory contexts before it produces comparisons worth paying for.

**The New Zealand conversation dies first.** NZ has eight universities. An NZ vice-chancellor asks the obvious question: "What am I benchmarking against? Twenty-four Australian institutions operating under TEQSA, the Australian Qualifications Framework, and Australian labour market conditions? How does that tell me whether my commerce degree is future-viable in a Wellington labour market?"

Evidura's answer — "the methodology is jurisdiction-agnostic, it's the same 11 dimensions" — is technically true and commercially irrelevant. The buyer isn't paying for the methodology. She's paying for the *signal*, and the signal's value is entirely relative: *how does my program compare against comparable programs in comparable institutions in comparable regulatory environments?* A dataset composed entirely of Australian universities answers a question no UK or Canadian buyer is asking.

**The UK pipeline goes cold.** Two UK universities expressed interest at a UA conference. Both ask for a benchmark comparison against the Russell Group before committing. Evidura has zero Russell Group data points. The team tries the "seed the dataset" play from the business model's Immediate Next Steps (#7) — rating 10–20 UK programs from public handbooks without institutional consent. But public-handbook ratings are shallow: they can score the visible curriculum design dimensions (structure, assessment patterns, visible technology integration) but cannot score the dimensions that require institutional access — industry partnership depth, employer advisory board quality, work-integrated learning placement rates, graduate outcome data, or faculty digital capability. The public ratings give UK institutions scores that are systematically lower than the Australian scores (because the AU ratings benefit from rich institutional data access), making the benchmark comparison misleading and, worse, legally vulnerable. A UK university rated CRITICAL from public handbooks alone has every incentive to challenge the methodology — and Evidura has no UK legal presence to defend it.

**The benchmark access licence product collapses.** This was supposed to contribute $112K–$150K in Year 3 revenue (5 subscribers at $15K–$30K/yr). But the dataset is Australian-only, and the peak bodies, government policy units, and sector analysts who would buy benchmark access need cross-jurisdictional comparisons — that's the entire point of a benchmark for an internationalised higher education sector. The dataset is too thin to support the annual index's premium data tier, too. The *State of Degree Durability* report can say interesting things about Australian programs, but it cannot say anything about UK, Canadian, or US programs, which makes it a parochial document, not the international authority the brand strategy requires.

**The flywheel stalls at exactly the point the financial model depends on it spinning.** Year 3 international revenue was projected at $375K (10–15 institutions). Actual: $0 from NZ, $0 from UK, $0 from Canada. The Year 5 projection of $7.2M from 400 institutions across four countries becomes fiction. Evidura is a successful Australian business — $600K–$900K ARR, profitable, respected — but it is not a $10M international rating agency. The benchmark dataset, the thing the business model called a "path-dependent, compounding moat," never compounded. It accumulated. 24 data points is not a flywheel; it's a filing cabinet.

**The ultimate irony:** Lightcast enters Australia in Year 4. Lightcast doesn't need an Australian benchmark dataset — it already has 18+ billion labour market data points spanning 30 countries. It can enter the Australian market and *immediately* provide cross-jurisdictional program-to-occupation alignment comparisons, because its dataset was built from labour market data, not from institutional relationships. Evidura's "first-mover" window closes because the first mover advantage was in the wrong asset — methodology and relationships, not data scale. And data scale is what international buyers actually pay for.

## 2. The Underlying Assumption

> **"The Australian benchmark dataset will be valuable enough to sell internationally."**

This assumption appears nowhere explicitly in the business model document, but it is the invisible beam holding up the entire international revenue projection. It rests on three nested sub-assumptions, all of which are false or fragile:

### Sub-assumption 2a: 26 institutions = a benchmark dataset

The business model treats "26 AU institutions" as if it constitutes a sellable benchmark. It does not. A benchmark dataset requires:
- **Sufficient N per cell:** At least 8–12 data points per discipline × institution-type × regulatory-context cell before any comparison is statistically meaningful. With 26 institutions, most cells have N=1 or N=2.
- **Cross-jurisdictional composition:** A UK buyer needs UK comparators. A Canadian buyer needs Canadian comparators. The methodology may be universal, but the *signal value* is jurisdiction-specific.
- **Temporal depth:** A single year of ratings (which is what Year 3 provides) is a snapshot, not a trend. Buyers pay for trends.

### Sub-assumption 2b: International customers will buy before the international dataset exists

The financial model assumes 10–15 international institutions in Year 3 — the same year the business model's Immediate Next Steps (#7) proposes to "seed the benchmark dataset with international programs." This is temporally incoherent: you cannot sell a product whose key input you are only beginning to build in the same year you expect revenue. The seed-the-dataset strategy is a *prerequisite* to international sales, not a *parallel* activity.

### Sub-assumption 2c: The methodology is the product, the dataset is the delivery mechanism

The business model's pricing philosophy states: "the rating is the product, not the software." It should also state: "the benchmark comparison is the product, not the individual rating." A standalone durability score for a single program has limited commercial value — it's an opinion. The *comparative* score — "your commerce degree ranks in the bottom quartile of Go8 commerce programs" — is what drives urgency, procurement, and renewal. That comparative value requires a dataset. When the dataset is thin, the signal is weak, and weak signals don't command $49,000 subscriptions.

### Why the assumption was easy to miss

It's a classic category error: confusing *having a dataset* with *having a valuable dataset*. The business model was built by people who deeply understand rating agencies (Moody's, NABERS, Health Star Rating) and correctly identified that the benchmark dataset is a path-dependent, first-mover moat. What they missed is that **those rating agencies built their datasets in single, large, contiguous markets** (US corporate bonds, Australian commercial buildings, Australian packaged food) where the dataset was comprehensive within a single jurisdiction from day one. Evidura's market is inherently fragmented — 41 institutions in Australia, 8 in NZ, 169 in the UK, ~2,500 in the US — and "first mover in Australia" does not translate to "first mover in the UK." Each jurisdiction requires its own dataset bootstrapping exercise, and each one faces the same chicken-and-egg problem. There is no single contiguous market; there are five separate markets, and the dataset moat resets to zero in each one.

## 3. Early Warning Signs

These are the signals that should have prompted a strategic rethink before the international sales push began. They are drawn from the business model document itself — the plan contains its own critique, if you know where to look.

### Warning Sign 1: The plan's own "seed the dataset" item is a confession

**Source:** Business model §10, Immediate Next Step #7: "Seed the benchmark dataset with international programs — Rate 10–20 UK/CA/US programs using public handbooks." Scheduled for Q2 2027 — the same year international revenue is projected at $375K.

**What it tells you:** The plan acknowledges, in its own action items, that the international dataset does not exist yet — and that the proposed solution (public-handbook ratings) is a weak proxy for the real thing. If this is the plan to build the asset you need to sell, and the plan's own timetable puts it *after* the sales target, the revenue projection is a hope, not a forecast.

### Warning Sign 2: The Australian-only unit economics don't support the international scaling narrative

**Source:** Business model §5: "The total addressable revenue from Australian institutional subscriptions alone is roughly $1.0M–$1.5M ARR." The business model itself states the Australian market caps at ~$1.5M. The entire financial case for Evidura rests on international expansion. But if the asset required for international expansion (the benchmark dataset) can only be built through international expansion, the financial case is circular.

### Warning Sign 3: No line item for international dataset bootstrapping costs

**Source:** Business model §4.3 (P&L) and §4.2 (non-people costs). There is no budget for rating programs that aren't paying customers — the "seed the dataset" activity has no associated cost. Rating 10–20 international programs from public handbooks requires analyst time, methodology adaptation for different qualification frameworks, and legal review for publishing ratings of institutions that never consented to be rated. This is real work, and the P&L doesn't fund it.

### Warning Sign 4: The "benchmark comparisons against the sector" value proposition is Australian-tethered

**Source:** Business model §1, Value Proposition: "benchmark comparisons against the sector." The customer segments list international universities as "Tertiary (Year 3+)." But the value proposition doesn't specify *which* sector — and for an international customer, "the sector" means *their* sector (Russell Group, U15, etc.), not the Australian sector. The value proposition was written for Australian buyers and never re-examined for international ones.

### Warning Sign 5: The competitive landscape has a data-scale asymmetry no one modelled

**Source:** Competitive intelligence doc confirms Lightcast operates in US, Canada, and UK with "18+ billion labour market data points" and a "Program-to-Occupation Alignment Score (0–100) benchmarked against" that dataset. Lightcast's benchmark is built on labour market data — it doesn't need institutional relationships to scale internationally. Lightcast can enter any English-speaking market tomorrow with a richer benchmark than Evidura will have in that market for years. Evidura's moat is relationship-dependent; Lightcast's moat is data-dependent. In a race to build cross-jurisdictional benchmarks, data-scale beats relationship-depth every time.

### Warning Sign 6: The Year 3–5 hockey stick assumes the hardest problem is already solved

**Source:** Business model §8 (the J-curve chart) and §5: "the constraint is institutional sales velocity, not market size."

**What it tells you:** The document correctly identifies that the Australian market is small and the sales cycle is slow. But it assumes the *product* — the benchmark comparison — works internationally as soon as there are customers to sell to. It doesn't. The product is the dataset, and the dataset doesn't exist internationally. The constraint isn't sales velocity; it's *dataset velocity* — the rate at which rated programs accumulate in each new jurisdiction. And that rate is zero until someone pays, which no one will do until the rate is non-zero.

### Warning Sign 7: The "path-dependent, compounding moat" language smuggles in an assumption about compounding rate

**Source:** Business model §1, Moat: "The benchmark dataset — path-dependent, first-mover, cannot be retroactively manufactured."

**What it tells you:** The language is correct — the dataset is genuinely path-dependent. But path-dependence doesn't guarantee compounding; it only guarantees that latecomers can't skip the line. If the line moves at 26 institutions in three years, the compounding rate is too slow to produce a valuable asset before competitors arrive or before the business runs out of Australian runway. The moat is real, but it's one metre wide and filling at a rate of centimetres per year.

## 4. What a Mitigated Strategy Would Look Like

If this failure mode is identified early enough (i.e., before Year 3 international sales begin), there are mitigations that change the shape of the problem:

1. **Price the Australian business for what it is** — a profitable $1.0M–$1.5M ARR rating agency for the Australian higher education sector. This is a real business. It's just not a venture-scale one. Drop the $10M Year 5 aspiration and build a sustainable, respected institution that owns the Australian market.

2. **International expansion through accreditation bodies, not direct sales.** AACSB, AMC, and Engineers Australia are international bodies. If AACSB embeds the Durability Rating as an evidence input for Standard 4.1, every AACSB-accredited business school globally (950+ institutions across 60 countries) has a reason to get rated — and the benchmark dataset builds itself through compliance demand, not sales conversations. This is the "become mandatory" play (business model Option D), and it's the only path that solves the chicken-and-egg problem at scale.

3. **Partnership with an existing international data holder.** QILT, HESA (UK), or NCES (US) could provide the data backbone for international program comparison without requiring Evidura to build institutional relationships in every market from scratch. A partnership with JISC (UK) or EDUCAUSE (US) — both mentioned as data sources in the market sizing document — could shortcut dataset bootstrapping.

4. **Radical reframe of the international value proposition.** Instead of "benchmark comparisons against the sector," sell "methodology licensing" to international partners who already have local datasets. Evidura provides the scoring engine; the local partner provides the benchmark context. This is a franchise model, not a centralised rating agency, and it produces a different (smaller) revenue profile — but it's achievable.
