## 1. The Trigger — When Commercialisation Gets Involved

**The trigger is automatic.** It fires the moment UoM-developed IP is transferred to an external entity. David doesn't choose to involve Commercialisation — the act of incorporating Evidura Pty Ltd and licensing the DFVA methodology to it triggers their involvement by default.

The IP ownership chain is unambiguous:

| Fact | Implication |
|---|---|
| David is a UoM Associate Director (employee) | IP developed during employment defaults to UoM ownership under standard employment contracts |
| The 41-program pilot was run on UoM programs | University data, university systems, university time |
| Infrastructure was built using UoM resources | University servers, university-funded development |
| The methodology was refined through UoM-funded service design work | University investment in the asset |

Commercialisation's position — *"the IP belongs to the university, and the university is entitled to fair value"* — is legally and ethically defensible. There is no loophole. The methodology was not developed on personal time with personal resources. It was developed in the course of employment.

**What does NOT trigger Commercialisation (yet):**

- Filing a conflict-of-interest disclosure with HR (this is separate — HR manages employment conflicts, not IP)
- Publishing the methodology as an academic paper (this is normal scholarly activity)
- Running pilot assessments on other universities' programs at no charge (this is research collaboration)
- Conversations with potential design partners (this is business development — no IP transfer)

**What DOES trigger Commercialisation:**

- Incorporating an external entity (Evidura Pty Ltd) with the intent to transfer UoM-developed IP to it
- Signing a licence agreement that moves the DFVA methodology from UoM ownership to external ownership
- Taking payment from an external entity for use of UoM-developed IP
- Issuing equity in an external entity to anyone, including the founder, when that entity uses UoM-developed IP

## 2. The Policy — Standard Spin-Out Terms

UoM Commercialisation is not a venture investor. It is a **policy-driven administrative unit** with a fiduciary duty to protect university assets. Its framework is designed for conventional IP commercialisation — patents, licences, research tools, drug discoveries — where the university's interest is straightforward: extract maximum value for university-owned IP.

### 2.1 Standard term sheet (documented standard practice)

| Term | Standard Range |
|---|---|
| **Equity** | 30–50% of the spin-out entity, calibrated to the value of the IP transferred and the university resources consumed during development |
| **Board seat** | One UoM-nominated director, full voting rights |
| **First-refusal rights** | On all equity issuances, asset sales, and change of control |
| **IP licence** | Royalty-bearing (typically 3–5% of gross revenue), revocable if the spin-out breaches licence terms |
| **Non-compete** | The spin-out must not develop or commercialise any product that competes with UoM's core teaching or research activities |
| **Decision timeline** | 6–12 months minimum (IP valuation → Commercialisation Committee review → legal drafting → DVC-R approval → University Council notification) |

### 2.2 The Commercialisation Committee

The Committee that approves spin-out terms meets **quarterly**. If you miss a quarterly window, the next one is 3 months away. The Committee requires:

- IP valuation (internal or external assessment)
- Conflict-of-interest review (founder is a UoM employee — this gets extra scrutiny)
- Business case assessment (standard template — not designed for independence-dependent ventures)
- DVC-R briefing note

### 2.3 What Commercialisation Optimises For

| Commercialisation optimises for | The venture needs |
|---|---|
| **Asset protection** — maximising the university's return on its IP | **Independence** — minimising the university's stake and control |
| **Policy compliance** — applying standard terms consistently | **Non-standard terms** — the standard terms kill the product |
| **Precedent management** — avoiding terms that weaken future negotiations | **Venture viability** — terms the market will accept |
| **Due process** — deliberate, multi-stage review | **Speed** — independence needed in months, not years |

These are in direct conflict. The conflict is structural, not personal. The individuals in Commercialisation are competent and acting in good faith. The problem is that their remit cannot accommodate what the venture needs.

## 3. The Structural Mismatch

| What Evidura needs | What Commercialisation's mandate requires | Why they can't meet |
|---|---|---|
| **Equity ≤ 20%** (above this, competitors won't pay) | **Equity 30–50%** (standard policy for IP developed on university time) | The ranges *are* the problem. 25% instead of 30% is "significant movement" in their framework — but it's still above the 20% ceiling. |
| **No board seat** (a UoM director seeing competitor data destroys trust) | **Board representation** (fiduciary duty to monitor the university's equity investment) | An observer seat is the maximum concession — but even an observer sees board papers. |
| **No first-refusal rights** (they block venture capital) | **First-refusal rights** (prevents IP being sold or diluted without university consent) | Narrowing from 3 categories to 1 is a concession — but one category is still a blockade. |
| **Fast resolution** (the independence clock is ticking) | **Due process** (committee review, legal assessment, DVC-R approval — 6–12 months) | The process is designed to be deliberative. It cannot be accelerated without DVC-R intervention. |

## 4. Why the Negotiating Position Fails

The independence structure document proposes: *"Negotiating position: the venture's entire value depends on independence. UoM equity >20% or a board seat destroys the product. UoM's choice is between 10-20% of a viable venture and 100% of an unsellable one."*

This argument fails for three reasons:

### 4.1 The IP ownership is unambiguous

*"The IP is worthless without independence"* is strategically correct but legally irrelevant. The university owns the asset. The asset's market value is a problem for the spin-out, not the owner. If the asset can't be commercialised on standard terms, the university's policy response is *"then it shouldn't be commercialised"* — not *"then we should accept non-standard terms."*

### 4.2 Your argument is indistinguishable from a bluff

To convince Commercialisation to accept 10%, the founder must argue that 30% kills the venture. But the founder *is* the venture — the person making the argument is also the person who would be responsible for the venture's success. Commercialisation hears *"we'll fail if you take 30%"* the way a bank hears *"we can't repay the loan if the interest rate is too high."* It may be true. It is also indistinguishable from a bluff.

### 4.3 The timelines are structurally incompatible

The venture needs Stage 1 (incorporation, IP licence, advisory board) by December 2026 — six months from the first meeting. Commercialisation's standard process takes 6–12 months *before any negotiation begins.* Their committee meets quarterly. Legal drafting takes weeks. DVC-R approval takes weeks. Council notification takes weeks. You cannot accelerate a process designed to be deliberative.

## 6. What to Do Instead — Before the First Meeting

### 6.1 Secure DVC-R (or higher) sponsorship

The independence requirement must be framed as a *structural constraint*, not a negotiation preference. Someone with authority over Commercialisation needs to direct them: this venture requires non-standard terms because its entire value proposition is independence. Standard terms kill it. Find a path.

Without that pre-commitment: Commercialisation applies standard policy (their job). With it: they have cover to deviate (they were directed to).

**Who to approach:** Brad (Director) escalates to the DVC-R or Provost. Frame it as: *"We have a venture whose commercial viability depends on independence from the university. The standard Commercialisation terms — 30%+ equity, board seat, first-refusal — destroy the product. We need a directive to negotiate within viability-preserving parameters, or we need to know now that it's not possible, so we can pivot to academic research only before burning relationships with design partners."*

### 6.2 Talk to a UoM spin-out survivor

A single two-hour conversation with a founder who has taken a venture through UoM Commercialisation would reveal: the standard term sheet ranges, the real timeline (not the published one), the committee calendar and quarterly windows, the escalation path when negotiations stall, and which arguments have worked before (and which haven't).

This is the highest-ROI research available. Do it this week.

### 6.3 Prepare the fallback

If Commercialisation won't budge — and the most likely outcome is they won't without DVC-R intervention — have Option C ready:

> **Stay internal. Academic research only.** Publish the methodology. Build the benchmark dataset for research purposes. Rate UoM programs only. Let the methodology gain academic credibility and citation. Commercial spin-out deferred until the independence answer becomes available (new leadership, policy change, or external partnership).

This is better than the failure path: burning 18 months negotiating unworkable terms, losing design partners, and watching Lightcast enter while you're still arguing about equity percentages.

### 6.4 The pre-meeting checklist

Before the first Commercialisation conversation:

- [ ] DVC-R or Provost briefed and pre-committed to non-standard terms — OR — Option C accepted as the fallback
- [ ] One conversation completed with a UoM spin-out survivor
- [ ] External opinion obtained (university IP attorney or commercialisation consultant) confirming standard terms are incompatible with an independence-dependent venture
- [ ] Brad aligned as sponsor — understands the binary: standard terms = dead venture, non-standard terms = viable venture, or no venture at all
- [ ] Timeline adjusted: Stage 1 target moved from December 2026 to June 2027 (12 months minimum from first meeting)

> **Commercialisation's job is to protect university assets. Your job is to explain why protecting this asset destroys its only source of value. But you cannot make that argument credibly without DVC-R sponsorship — because you are the beneficiary of the terms you're asking for.**
