# Deep-Dive: Unit Economics Don't Close — The Capital Gap, Fragility, and Talent Trap

> A failure analysis of Evidura's financial model: why Year 3 EBITDA of $218K cannot fund the ambition, cannot survive a shock, and cannot attract the team needed to execute the plan.
> **Date:** 2026-06-30 · **Based on:** [Business model](evidura-business-model.md) · [Market sizing](compass-market-sizing.md) · [Procurement velocity deep-dive](evidura-procurement-velocity-deep-dive.md) · [Benchmark flywheel deep-dive](evidura-deepdive-benchmark-flywheel.md)

## 1. The Failure Story

### Year 3 Arrives. The Plan Says "Profitable."

Evidura closes Year 3 with $1.64M in revenue against $1.42M in costs. EBITDA is +$218,000 — a 13% margin. The Australian base is established: 26 institutions on paid subscriptions, accreditation partnerships generating $240K, benchmark licences contributing $112K, the annual index bringing in $100K. The first 10–15 international institutions in NZ and the UK have signed, contributing $375K. On paper, the J-curve is working. The plan calls this the inflection point — the moment the business funds its own growth.

### Year 4 Arrives. The Maths Breaks.

The Year 5 target is $10M ARR across 400 institutions in AU, NZ, UK, and Canada. Evidura currently has ~40 institutions. To reach 400 in 24 months, the business must add **360 institutions** — roughly 15 per month, every month, for two years. This is not a sales target. This is a *capital deployment target*.

At the plan's own optimistic CAC of $18,000 per institution, acquiring 360 institutions costs **$6.48 million**. The business has $218,000 in retained earnings from Year 3. If every dollar of Year 4's projected $2.1M EBITDA is reinvested into customer acquisition — zero margin, zero buffer, zero investment in methodology R&D, zero legal defence fund — the business can generate $2.32M in acquisition capital across Years 3–4. That is **$4.16 million short** of what's needed just for customer acquisition costs, before a single dollar is spent on the actual scaling infrastructure: international sales teams, local legal entities in three new countries, cross-jurisdictional marketing, and the benchmark dataset bootstrapping that the business model itself admits doesn't exist yet.

The shortfall gets worse when you examine what CAC actually means outside Australia. The plan's $18,000 CAC is built on the Australian sales motion: Go8 reference network, conference appearances, relationship-driven procurement. This sales motion does not exist in the UK, Canada, or New Zealand. There is no Go8 reference network in London. There is no five-year relationship with Universities UK. Every international institution must be acquired from a cold start, in a procurement environment that is — if anything — slower and more political than Australia's. Real international CAC is conservatively **$40,000–$60,000 per institution**. At $50K average, acquiring 360 institutions costs **$18 million** — more than Year 5's total projected revenue.

The business model contains an unstated, mathematically impossible assumption: that the Year 4–5 growth can be funded from operations. It cannot. The plan requires venture-scale capital deployment ($6M–$18M in acquisition costs alone) but projects lifestyle-business retained earnings ($218K–$2.1M). The gap is not marginal. It is existential.

### The First Shock Arrives. The Business Dies.

While the leadership team is stretched across three time zones trying to build international pipelines with Australian-sized margins, something goes wrong at home:

**Scenario A — Budget crisis:** The Australian university sector's margins have collapsed to 5.3%. Two mid-tier universities ($32K each) decline to renew, citing budget pressure. A third Go8 institution ($49K) puts renewal "under review" pending the outcome of a faculty restructure. Revenue loss: $113,000 — more than half of Year 3's total EBITDA.

**Scenario B — Competitor entry:** Lightcast or Coursedog launches an Australian office. Two institutions pause their Evidura renewal to evaluate the competitor. One switches. Revenue loss: $50,000–$75,000.

**Scenario C — Legal challenge:** A program rated CRITICAL disputes the rating. The university's general counsel sends a letter threatening action. Evidura has no legal budget for this — the Year 3 legal line item is $30,000, and it was spent on trademark and spin-out documentation. Defending the methodology costs $80,000–$150,000 in legal fees — consuming 37–69% of the entire Year 3 EBITDA on a single incident.

**In all three scenarios, the business flips from marginally profitable to cash-negative in a single quarter.** At 87% fixed costs (10 FTE of people), there is no cost base to cut quickly. The people who produce the ratings, maintain the dataset, and run the sales process *are* the cost base. Cutting them cuts revenue-generation capacity. The business enters a death spiral: shock → cash-negative → cost-cutting → reduced capacity → slower sales → revenue decline → deeper cash-negative.

The plan's risk register rates "competitor entry" as MEDIUM and "legal challenge" as MEDIUM. Neither has a financial buffer modelled against it. The risk mitigation for competitor entry is "18–36 month first-mover window" — which is not a financial buffer, it's a hope. When a 22% revenue hit from churn can eliminate all profitability, the risk register's severity ratings are disconnected from the P&L's fragility.

### The Talent Never Arrives.

To reach $10M ARR, Evidura needs to hire across four countries. The plan requires:

- **UK Country Lead:** Someone who can sell into Russell Group universities, navigate HESA and Office for Students regulatory environments, and build a UK benchmark dataset from zero. Market rate: £120,000–£150,000 (AUD $230,000–$290,000).

- **North American Sales Lead:** Someone who can penetrate the US and Canadian markets, understand NCES data structures, and compete against Lightcast on their home turf. Market rate: USD $180,000–$250,000 (AUD $275,000–$380,000).

- **International Legal Counsel:** Someone who understands higher education regulation, defamation law, and commercial contracting in Australia, the UK, Canada, and New Zealand. Market rate: AUD $200,000–$280,000.

- **Marketing / Brand Lead:** Someone who can position the *State of Degree Durability* index as an international authority, not an Australian curiosity. Market rate: AUD $150,000–$200,000.

These four hires alone cost **$855,000–$1,150,000 per year**. That is 3.9–5.3× the entire Year 3 EBITDA. The plan's Year 3 people cost of $1.1M for 10 FTE (average $110K) is already below-market for the roles listed. The plan cannot afford these hires at Year 3 profitability levels, and it cannot reach Year 5 without them.

But the problem is deeper than affordability. These people won't join. A $218K EBITDA company with a 13% margin, 10 employees, and a market cap of ~$2M (at a generous 10× EBITDA multiple) cannot offer competitive compensation packages. It cannot offer equity that compensates for below-market salary — the equity in a $2M company is worth approximately nothing to a senior hire who could earn $250K+ at a funded SaaS company. It cannot offer career growth — there are 10 people, and the next level above "UK Country Lead" is "CEO," who isn't leaving. It cannot offer stability — the business is one bad quarter from cash-negative.

The talent the plan requires to execute international expansion is the talent that will only join *after* international expansion is already successful. This is the talent chicken-and-egg problem: you need world-class international sales leadership to reach 400 institutions, but world-class international sales leaders only join companies that can already demonstrate international traction, competitive compensation, and a credible equity story. Evidura at Year 3 has none of these.

### Year 5 Arrives. The Business Is a Successful Australian Rating Agency. It Is a Failure by Its Own Ambition.

Without the capital to fund international expansion, without the talent to execute it, and without the financial buffer to survive the shocks that every B2B company experiences in its first five years, Evidura does not fail — it plateaus. It reaches 30–35 Australian institutions, perhaps a handful in New Zealand through personal relationships, and settles at $1.2M–$1.6M ARR. EBITDA stabilises around $250,000–$350,000. The CEO earns $180,000. The team stays at 10–12 people. The business generates enough to be self-sustaining and respected in the Australian higher education sector. It is not a $10M international rating agency. It is a lifestyle business.

The cruel irony: **the plan's own ambition kills it**. The business model is built around a growth trajectory that requires venture-scale capital deployment, but it's structured as a bootstrapped spin-out producing lifestyle-business margins. It aims for Moody's scale with a NABERS budget. If the plan had accepted from the start that Evidura was an Australian business — $1.5M ARR, 15 people, profitable, respected — it would be a success story. Instead, it's a failure story because it set a target ($10M, 400 institutions) that its own unit economics could never fund, and the gap between ambition and resources produced a business that was neither a viable startup (not enough growth to attract venture capital) nor a viable small business (not enough margin to fund growth from operations). It fell into the dead zone.

## 2. The Underlying Assumption

> **"The business can self-fund international expansion from Australian operating profits."**

This is the invisible assumption that connects Year 3 to Year 5 in the financial model. It is never stated. But it must be true for the projections to hold, because the plan does not include a Series A raise, a major grant, or any external capital beyond the $500K–$800K seed round earmarked for the spin-out independence structure.

This assumption fails under examination from every angle:

### The Growth Capital Gap (Quantified)

| | Year 3 | Year 4 | Year 5 | Total (Yrs 3–5) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Institutions (cumulative)** | ~40 | ~120 (implied) | 400 | — |
| **New institutions to acquire** | — | ~80 | ~280 | 360 |
| **Acquisition cost @ $18K CAC (optimistic)** | — | $1.44M | $5.04M | **$6.48M** |
| **Acquisition cost @ $50K CAC (realistic intl)** | — | $4.00M | $14.00M | **$18.00M** |
| **Projected EBITDA** | $218K | $2.1M | $3.8M | **$6.12M** |
| **Capital shortfall @ optimistic CAC** | — | — | — | **$0.36M** |
| **Capital shortfall @ realistic CAC** | — | — | — | **$11.88M** |

At the plan's own optimistic CAC, the growth is *barely* self-funding — it requires reinvesting 106% of projected EBITDA in Years 3–4 into acquisition, leaving zero buffer for anything else. At realistic international CAC, the shortfall is catastrophic: $11.88M.

But even the "barely self-funding" scenario at optimistic CAC is misleading. It assumes:
- Every dollar of EBITDA is available for customer acquisition (no methodology R&D, no legal, no governance, no infrastructure investment)
- The 360 new institutions all convert at $18,000 CAC (despite being international, cold-start, in markets with no reference network)
- No churn during the growth period (an 8–12% churn rate on the existing base would subtract $130K–$200K from available capital annually)
- No salary inflation as you hire the 30+ additional people needed to service 400 institutions

Remove any one of these assumptions and the gap widens considerably.

### The Margin Trap

A 13% EBITDA margin is too thin to fund growth and too fat to attract venture capital. The business falls into the dead zone of startup finance:

- **Too thin to self-fund growth:** At 13%, retained earnings compound too slowly to finance a 10× revenue expansion in 24 months. At this margin, the business would need 7–8 years of retained earnings to fund the acquisition costs of reaching 400 institutions — but the plan calls for 2 years.

- **Too unremarkable to attract venture capital:** Venture investors don't fund 13% margin businesses. They fund growth — 50%, 100%, 200% year-over-year — and they accept negative margins as the cost of scaling. But Evidura's growth rate is structurally capped by university procurement cycles at 30–50% annually (optimistically), which is below the threshold that interests venture capital. The business is too slow-growing for VC and too capital-intensive for bootstrapping. It has no natural funding source.

- **Too small to attract debt financing:** A $1.64M revenue business with $218K EBITDA cannot secure meaningful debt. Banks and venture debt providers want $2M+ in revenue and clear growth trajectories. Evidura at Year 3 qualifies for neither.

The plan's seed raise ($500K–$800K) is described as funding "the independence structure, independent advisory board setup, and initial sales push beyond the Go8 reference network." It is *not* described as funding international expansion — because it can't. $800K might fund one international sales hire and 12 months of market development in one country. It cannot fund simultaneous expansion into NZ, the UK, and Canada. And once that $800K is spent, the business is back to self-funding from a 13% margin — the same math that got it here.

### The Australian Ceiling Is Real and Low

The business model itself states: "The total addressable revenue from Australian institutional subscriptions alone is roughly $1.0M–$1.5M ARR" (line 166). This is not an estimate to be validated; it's arithmetic. 41 universities at a blended $25K–$36K each = $1.0M–$1.5M. Secondary revenue streams (accreditation, benchmark, index, seal licensing) are also capped by the number of Australian institutions, accreditation bodies, and peak bodies — there are only so many buyers in a market of 41 institutions.

The plan acknowledges this ("meaningful but not venture-scale") and correctly identifies international expansion as the only path to $10M. But it never models the cost of the bridge from Australia to the world. Building that bridge requires capital the business cannot generate and talent it cannot attract. The bridge doesn't exist, and the plan treats it as if it's already built.

### The Hidden Funding Requirement

If we ask what the plan *actually* needs to reach 400 institutions — not what it claims it needs, but what the unit economics demand — the answer is stark:

| Funding Requirement | Amount | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| International customer acquisition (360 institutions @ $50K CAC) | $18.0M | Sales teams, travel, local marketing, procurement support in 3 new countries |
| International team build (4 country leads, legal, marketing) | $3.5M–$5.0M | Salaries for 2–3 years until international operations break even |
| Benchmark dataset bootstrapping (UK, CA) | $500K–$1.0M | Analyst time to rate 100+ programs from public handbooks in new jurisdictions |
| Legal entity setup (UK, CA, NZ) | $200K–$400K | Incorporation, regulatory compliance, local counsel, insurance |
| Methodology localisation (QF adaptation) | $250K–$500K | Adapting DFVA for different qualification frameworks and labour market data |
| Buffer for bad years / shocks | $1.0M–$2.0M | Operating reserve for the 87% fixed cost base during procurement cycles |
| **Total realistic funding requirement** | **$23.5M–$26.9M** | — |

The plan allocates $500K–$800K. The gap is roughly **30–50×**.

This is not a modest miscalculation. It is a category error: mistaking a business model designed for a single-country, relationship-driven professional services firm for a multi-country, capital-intensive B2B SaaS company. The unit economics of the former cannot fund the growth trajectory of the latter.

## 3. Early Warning Signs

These were visible at the time of planning. They are drawn from the business model document itself and the surrounding market intelligence. Taken together, they constitute a pre-mortem that the plan's own numbers could have generated.

### Warning Sign 1: The Plan's Own P&L Shows the Problem

**Source:** Business model §4.3 Summary P&L.

| | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $280K | $920K | $1,640K |
| EBITDA | –$270K | +$30K | +$218K |

Year 3 EBITDA is **$218,000**. That is the pool of retained earnings available to fund Year 4 growth. Year 4 revenue is projected at approximately $4.5M (interpolated from the Year 3–5 hockey stick). That implies adding roughly 80 institutions in a single year (from ~40 to ~120). At the plan's own $18,000 CAC, that's **$1.44M in acquisition costs** — against $218K in retained earnings. The plan's own numbers show a 6.6× gap between available capital and required investment for the very next year. No funding source is identified to close it.

### Warning Sign 2: The Plan Treats "International Expansion" as an Item, Not a Capital Project

**Source:** Business model §3.2, Year 3 international revenue line: "$250K–$500K."

International expansion appears in the revenue model as a line item generating $375K in Year 3, sitting alongside accreditation partnerships, benchmark licences, and seal licensing as if it were equivalent to them. It is not. Selling to UK and NZ universities is not a secondary revenue stream — it is a second business. It requires local presence, local legal, local brand, local dataset, local regulatory navigation, and local competitive positioning. Treating it as a line item that delivers revenue without modelling its cost is the financial equivalent of assuming a bridge you haven't built will hold traffic.

The plan's cost structure (§4.2) includes no line item for international expansion costs — no international legal, no international travel (the $40K travel line is Australian sales), no international marketing, no local incorporation. The $375K of Year 3 international revenue appears in the P&L as if it dropped from the sky. The costs to generate it are invisible.

### Warning Sign 3: The "One Chart That Matters" Is a Fantasy

**Source:** Business model §8.

```
Year 3:  +$218K  (profitable — Australian base established, international begins)
Year 4:  +$2.1M  (scale — UK/Canada expansion, seal licensing active)
Year 5:  +$3.8M  (mature — 400 institutions, certification flywheel, agent-native channel)
```

This chart implies that profitability compounds geometrically from Year 3 onward, funding its own acceleration. The Year 3→4 EBITDA jump is **9.6×** ($218K → $2.1M). The Year 4→5 jump is a more modest 1.8×. Nothing in the plan's cost structure, unit economics, or market context explains the 9.6× jump between Year 3 and Year 4. It is not modelled; it is asserted. A 9.6× EBITDA increase in a single year, in a business with 9–18 month sales cycles and a 13% starting margin, has no precedent in B2B SaaS or professional services. The chart is not a forecast. It is a wish.

### Warning Sign 4: The Talent Numbers Don't Work

**Source:** Business model §4.1 People costs.

The Year 3 people cost of $1.1M for 10 FTE averages $110,000 per person. This is already below market for several of the listed roles: full-stack engineers at $140K (Australian market rate for mid-level is $130K–$180K; senior is $160K–$220K), data engineers at $130K (market rate $140K–$200K), and a CEO at $180K (significantly below market for a spin-out CEO with the domain expertise described). These are salary numbers for a bootstrapped startup offering meaningful equity — but as analysed above, the equity in a $2M EBITDA business is not meaningful.

To reach 400 institutions, the plan requires adding 20–30+ people in Years 4–5, many at senior levels and in expensive international markets. The Year 3 people budget of $1.1M must scale to roughly $3.5M–$5M — a 3–4.5× increase — while EBITDA is only growing from $218K to a projected $2.1M. Even if the revenue materialises, the people costs to generate it consume the margin. The people model is calibrated for a 15-person Australian consulting firm, not a multi-country rating agency with 400 institutional clients.

### Warning Sign 5: The Australian Revenue Concentration Problem Has No International Analogue

**Source:** Business model §2, independence constraint: "no single institution >15% of total revenue."

This constraint is prudent and necessary for credibility. But it creates a structural problem for international expansion that the plan never addresses: the more institutions you add, the more the revenue concentration rule forces you to diversify *before* you can concentrate revenue from any single source. At 26 Australian institutions, the maximum any one can contribute is $246,000 (15% of $1.64M). A Go8 institution paying $49,000 is safely at 3% of revenue. But when you're entering the UK market and you have *one* UK customer, that customer is 100% of your UK revenue. Under a strict 15% rule applied to total company revenue, the first UK customer can contribute at most $246,000 — which is fine, because they'll pay $49,000. But as you grow, the percentage limits mean you need *more* customers to grow total revenue, which means you need *more* capital to acquire them. The independence constraint mathematically increases the capital required for growth, because it forces diversification faster than an unconstrained business would need it. The plan never quantifies this effect.

### Warning Sign 6: The 400-Institution Target Exceeds the English-Speaking University Count for the Named Markets

**Source:** Market sizing doc: Australia 41, NZ 8, UK 169, Canada ~100. Total: **~318 universities.**

The Year 5 target is 400 institutions across AU, NZ, UK, and CA. But there are only ~318 universities in those four countries combined. To reach 400, Evidura would need to either:
- Penetrate the US market (2,500+ institutions), which the plan does not commit to for Year 5
- Include non-university higher education providers (RTOs, polytechnics, colleges), which the pricing model was not designed for and which have different procurement processes and willingness to pay
- Achieve >100% market penetration in the four named markets (impossible)

The plan's own market sizing data shows that the 400-institution target requires either expanding into markets the plan hasn't costed for, or penetrating markets at rates that are commercially implausible. Either way, the target number doesn't survive contact with the institution counts.

### Warning Sign 7: The Seed Raise Is Mislabelled

**Source:** Business model §4.4: "Seed raise: $500K–$800K to fund the independence structure, independent advisory board setup, and initial sales push beyond the Go8 reference network."

The seed raise is described as funding corporate governance and an "initial sales push." But the plan's own projections show the business reaching break-even in Month 18–22 and profitability by Year 3 — suggesting the seed raise is a governance requirement, not a survival requirement. However, as this analysis shows, the business cannot fund its own growth trajectory from Year 3 profitability. The seed raise should be described as what it actually is: **the first capital call in a funding requirement of $23M–$27M to reach the stated Year 5 target.** Labelling it as a one-time $500K–$800K governance fund masks the true capital need and creates the false impression that the business is capital-efficient when it is, in fact, profoundly capital-intensive at the growth stage.

### Warning Sign 8: The Plan's "Conservative" Framing Is Risk Camouflage

**Source:** Business model line 277: "The financial projections are deliberately conservative."

The word "conservative" appears to inoculate the projections against scrutiny. But conservative relative to what? A conservative projection for a business selling a new category into a sector with 5.3% margins, 12–18 month procurement cycles, and no regulatory mandate would model the base case at the low end and the downside at near-zero. This plan models 63% Australian market penetration in 3 years (26 of 41 universities) as *conservative*. That is not conservative by any standard applied to university procurement. The framing is not a reflection of the numbers; it's a rhetorical device that discourages interrogation of the unit economics beneath.

## 4. What a Corrected Picture Looks Like

If we accept that the unit economics don't close — that the business cannot self-fund international expansion from Australian operating profits — then the strategic options narrow to three:

### Path A: Accept the Lifestyle Business

Price Evidura for what it actually is: a profitable Australian rating agency. Target 30–35 Australian institutions, plus NZ through personal relationships. Revenue ceiling: $1.5M–$2.0M. EBITDA: $300K–$500K. Team: 10–15 people. No international expansion beyond opportunistic NZ contracts. No venture capital. No $10M Year 5 target. This is a real, viable, respected business — it's just not the business the plan describes. It generates enough to sustain itself, pay its people reasonably, and serve the Australian higher education sector with an independent, credible durability rating. It is a success on its own terms.

### Path B: Raise the Capital the Plan Actually Requires

Accept that reaching 400 institutions requires $20M–$27M in growth capital (not $500K–$800K). Build a venture-scale fundraising narrative around the accreditation-body channel as the wedge into international markets. Target a Series A of $8M–$12M in Year 2–3, with a clear path to Series B of $15M–$20M for scaling. This requires:
- Demonstrating 100%+ year-over-year growth in the Australian market (impossible if procurement velocity caps at 3–5 institutions/year, which the procurement deep-dive establishes)
- Showing a clear path to venture-scale returns (difficult given the $80M SAM)
- Competing for venture dollars against SaaS companies with larger TAMs and faster growth rates

This path is theoretically possible but practically very difficult. The business's structural growth constraints (procurement cycles, thin market, new category) make it a poor fit for venture capital expectations.

### Path C: The Accreditation-Body Wedge (Long Game)

Abandon direct international institutional sales entirely. Focus all international expansion energy on embedding the Durability Rating in accreditation body requirements (AACSB, AMC, Engineers Australia). If AACSB requires the rating for Standard 4.1, every AACSB-accredited business school globally — 950+ institutions — has a compliance reason to get rated. This solves the chicken-and-egg problem at scale: institutions come to Evidura because they *must*, not because they *choose to*. The benchmark dataset builds itself through compliance demand. Revenue becomes structural, not discretionary.

This path is the only one that closes the unit economics gap without requiring $20M+ in growth capital — but it takes years, not quarters, and it requires patience, policy influence, and a CEO who can sustain a multi-year partnership cycle with slow-moving accreditation bodies. The plan is not currently resourced or structured for this path.

## 5. Recommendations

### For the Financial Model

1. **Model the growth capital requirement explicitly.** Don't let "international expansion" sit in the P&L as a revenue line item without corresponding costs. At minimum, add:
   - International CAC of $40,000–$60,000 per institution
   - Country launch costs of $300,000–$500,000 per new market (legal, local hire, marketing, travel)
   - Benchmark dataset bootstrapping costs of $250,000–$500,000 per new jurisdiction

2. **Build a capitalisation table for the full growth trajectory.** The seed raise of $500K–$800K is Year 1–2 money for governance and initial sales. What funds Year 3–5? Either answer that question or remove the Year 3–5 projections.

3. **Stress-test the P&L against single-shock scenarios.** Model what happens when:
   - 2–3 institutions churn (8–12% of base)
   - A competitor enters the Australian market
   - A legal challenge costs $100,000+
   - Procurement cycles extend to 24 months (a realistic downside, per the procurement deep-dive)

   If any single scenario flips the business from profitable to cash-negative, the EBITDA margin is too thin for the stated growth ambition.

4. **Revise the "One Chart That Matters" to show realistic growth rates.** A 9.6× EBITDA jump between Year 3 and Year 4 is not a forecast. Show what growth looks like at 30–50% annually (the realistic ceiling for university procurement-constrained sales) and let that determine the Year 5 number — not the other way around.

### For the Strategy

1. **Choose a path: lifestyle business, venture-funded scale-up, or accreditation-body long game.** The current plan tries to be all three and achieves none. Each path requires a fundamentally different cost structure, funding strategy, and timeline.

2. **If the accreditation-body path is chosen, resource it properly.** This means a dedicated partnerships lead (not 0.5 FTE of the CEO's time), a multi-year timeline (3–5 years to embed in AACSB standards), and realistic interim revenue projections that don't depend on international institutional sales.

3. **If the lifestyle business path is chosen, own it.** Drop the $10M Year 5 target. Build a sustainable, respected Australian rating agency. This is a genuinely good outcome — it's just not the outcome the plan currently describes.

### For the Risk Framework

1. **Add "growth capital gap" as a CRITICAL risk.** The plan currently has no risk item for the fundamental mismatch between growth ambition and available capital. This is the risk that makes all other risks consequential — because without capital to absorb them, every MEDIUM risk becomes a CRITICAL one.

2. **Add a specific KPI: "Growth capital runway"** — the number of months the business can sustain its current cost base and acquisition spend at current cash levels. When this number drops below 6 months, trigger a strategic review.

3. **Add a hard gate to international expansion:** do not hire international staff until either (a) $2M+ in growth capital is secured, or (b) the Australian business generates $500K+ in annual free cash flow. The current plan calls for international hiring at Year 3 when neither condition is met.
