# COMPASS — Alignment with University Council Strategic Questions

**Purpose:** Map the four strategic questions raised in University Council against the COMPASS research and capability, honestly distinguishing what COMPASS answers, what it partially informs, and what sits outside its scope. Companion to [compass-problem-tree.md](compass-problem-tree.md), [compass-stakeholder-analysis.md](compass-stakeholder-analysis.md), and [compass-competitive-strategic-intelligence.md](compass-competitive-strategic-intelligence.md).

---

## Why This Matters

Council has independently articulated the problem COMPASS was built to address. The COMPASS problem tree's core problem statement — *"university degrees failing to maintain economic value in an AI-augmented labour market, and institutions lacking the mechanisms to detect or respond before it compounds"* — is almost exactly Council's questions 2 and 3.

Three implications:

1. **Validation.** The problem has reached the institution's highest governance level. This is no longer a hypothesis the team is testing in isolation — it is a stated institutional priority.
2. **A sponsorship pathway.** The research repeatedly identified that any curriculum-market alignment tool requires a DVC-level or executive champion because no regulatory mandate forces adoption (TEQSA regulates assessment integrity, not curriculum-market durability). Council attention is the highest-possible expression of that executive demand.
3. **A primary-research opportunity.** Council members *are* the senior stakeholders the research plan set out to interview. The questions they have raised are, in effect, their own articulation of the interview agenda.

But there is also a caution embedded in the questions, addressed below: Council's Q1 deliberately pushes beyond economic value — and COMPASS, as currently scoped, measures economic durability. That boundary must be named, not blurred.

## The Four Questions, Mapped

### Q2 — The value of a degree *(COMPASS's home ground — directly addressed)*

> *Do students still value a degree? What is a Melbourne degree worth, what does it now signal, and how must the credential change?*

COMPASS produces exactly this: a structured viability score and risk band for any degree, measuring whether its content retains labour-market value. This is the most direct mapping of all four questions.

**Evidence already gathered that speaks to this:**
- **The employer-institution perception gap is the central, repeated finding.** Pearson/AWS (April 2026): 78% of HE leaders believe they meet employer expectations; only **28% of employers agree**. Lumina-Gallup (March 2026): only 54% of employers believe colleges graduate students with needed skills; 69% of recent hires require significant additional training.
- **The signal is bifurcating, not simply collapsing.** Cengage (2025): degree requirements *rose* from 55% to 71% of postings — the credential as a screening signal is intensifying in some segments. PwC (June 2025): in the most AI-augmented roles, degree requirements are *declining* (71%→67%). The degree's value as content and its value as signal are diverging.
- **75% of employers still expect degrees to be as or more important in five years** (Lumina-Gallup) — the credential is not disappearing, but the gap between what it signals and what it delivers is widening.

**The distinction COMPASS must hold:** COMPASS measures whether a degree's *content* prepares graduates for the market. The *signal* value of a Melbourne credential — brand, network, screening function — is partly independent of content and partly outside what COMPASS measures. The honest framing: COMPASS answers "is the economic substance behind the signal eroding?" — which is the part of Q2 most amenable to evidence.

---

### Q3 — The jobs that motivate study *(COMPASS's home ground — directly addressed, richest evidence)*

> *Will AI remove the graduate/entry-level jobs that motivate people to enrol? What are we now preparing students for?*

COMPASS Dimension 1 (*Automation Exposure of Roles*) maps the first five years of graduate work against current AI automation capability. Its Market Intelligence capability profiles job families by AI substitution pressure. This is the question COMPASS is most heavily evidenced to address.

**Evidence already gathered — this is the sharpest and most urgent of the four:**
- **WEF Future of Jobs 2025:** 92M roles displaced, 170M created (net +78M) by 2030; 40% of skills change within five years.
- **McKinsey (Nov 2025):** 57% of US work hours are technically automatable; demand for AI fluency grew sevenfold in two years.
- **The entry-level compression is already measurable.** Cengage (2025): only 30% of 2025 graduates secured jobs in their field — down from 41% the prior year; **46% of employers cite AI as a reason for reduced entry-level hiring**; 76% are hiring the same or fewer entry-level workers.
- **The "human middleware" thesis.** The COMPASS proof-of-concept (Master of Information Systems assessment) found the program "anchors early-career outcomes in human middleware tasks — documentation, requirements analysis, coordination, and reporting — that are the current front line of AI-driven role compression." This is precisely the mechanism behind Council's Q3.
- **JSA Emerging Roles (Nov 2024):** 37 emerging roles identified; the pipeline is shifting, not vanishing — but toward roles most degrees were not designed to produce.

**This is where COMPASS's value is clearest:** it turns Q3 from a board-table anxiety into a structured, evidence-based read on which specific programs feed graduates into the roles most exposed to automation.

### Q1 — Relevance of the university *(partially addressed — COMPASS measures one dimension of relevance)*

> *Does AI erode our relevance? What do we uniquely offer that AI cannot (community, credentialing, research, mentorship, formation)?*

This is the deepest and broadest question, and COMPASS answers only part of it.

**What COMPASS does address:**
- **Dimension B — Irreplaceability Premium** is, almost word-for-word, "what do we uniquely offer that AI cannot": the rare integration of technical depth, domain expertise, and human judgment.
- **Dimension 8 — Human and Relational Capability** captures the clinical, care, and stakeholder-accountability skills that remain strongly human.
- **The systems-thinking framing** ([compass-systems-thinking.md](compass-systems-thinking.md)) already operates at the level Q1 demands: the *mental models* layer of the iceberg — the unexamined beliefs that accreditation equals quality, and that academic rigour is valuable independent of economic relevance. Q1 is Council beginning to examine exactly these mental models.
- **The complementarity evidence:** McKinsey finds 70%+ of employer-sought skills are used in *both* automatable and non-automatable work — supporting a "human + AI" framing over wholesale replacement.

**Where COMPASS stops — and this matters:** COMPASS measures *economic/labour-market relevance*. Council's Q1 explicitly names **community, mentorship, and formation** — sources of value that are not primarily economic and that COMPASS does not measure. A tool that scores degrees only on labour-market durability could, if framed carelessly, appear to endorse a purely instrumental view of the university — the exact reductive framing the COMPASS proposal itself flagged as a risk ("how to frame outputs so they are genuinely useful rather than reductive or damaging").

**The honest position:** COMPASS answers "*is our economic relevance eroding, and where?*" — a necessary but not sufficient component of Q1. The broader relevance question (formation, belonging, the civic and developmental role of a university) is one COMPASS should be explicitly positioned *alongside*, not as answering. Treating COMPASS as a full answer to Q1 would overclaim and invite the reductiveness critique.

---

### Q4 — Research credibility & integrity *(outside COMPASS's scope — a sibling problem)*

> *Is AI undermining research? How do we preserve credibility, integrity and trustworthiness (provenance, reproducibility, authorship, peer review)?*

**COMPASS does not address this.** COMPASS is a curriculum and labour-market durability tool — it concerns teaching, programs, and graduate outcomes, not the research enterprise. Provenance, reproducibility, authorship, and peer review are a distinct domain.

**What the research gathered touched, tangentially:**
- Macquarie University and QUT published *A university framework for the responsible use of generative AI in research* (Journal of Higher Education Policy and Management, 2025) — a governance framework grounded in honesty, transparency, and accountability.
- The ACSES *Australian Framework for AI in Higher Education* (December 2025) includes "research applications" as one of its nine implementation areas, but at a principles level.

**The honest position:** Q4 is a genuinely separate workstream. It shares only the *meta-framing* with COMPASS — both are events on the same systems-thinking iceberg (visible symptoms of AI restructuring the university), and both stem from the same structural mismatch between the speed of AI change and the speed of institutional response. But the mechanisms, stakeholders, and tooling are different. Stretching COMPASS to cover research integrity would weaken its credibility on the questions it genuinely answers.

## How to Incorporate

**1. Reframe COMPASS as decision-support for Council's questions — not as the answer.**
The COMPASS proposal's defining promise is "evidence, not opinion." Council is currently asking Q2 and Q3 in the register of board-table discussion. COMPASS's positioning is as the evidence engine that lets the institution answer those two questions rigorously — moving from anecdote and peer benchmarking to a structured, repeatable read across the program portfolio. This is the strongest and most honest framing: COMPASS answers *two of the four* questions well, and it answers them with evidence.

**2. Lead with Q3, anchor with Q2, position carefully on Q1, defer Q4.**
- **Q3** is where the evidence is richest and the urgency clearest — lead here.
- **Q2** is the natural companion — the viability score is the artefact.
- **Q1** should be presented as *partially* addressed, with the explicit acknowledgement that COMPASS measures economic relevance and that the university's formative and civic value is a parallel conversation COMPASS supports but does not resolve. This honesty is a strength in a Council context, not a weakness.
- **Q4** should be named as out of scope and flagged as a sibling workstream — ideally with a pointer to the Macquarie/QUT and ACSES frameworks as starting points for whoever does own it.

**3. Use the framing caution as a credibility asset.**
The risk that a labour-market-durability tool reads as reductive is real, and Council's Q1 surfaces it. Naming this risk proactively — "COMPASS measures one dimension of value deliberately, and is not a claim that economic durability is all that matters" — pre-empts the most likely critique and demonstrates the kind of judgment that earns executive trust.

**4. Treat Council's questions as the live interview agenda.**
The research plan's primary-research phase sought access to DVC-level stakeholders. Council having raised these questions is the opening. The eight falsifiable interview questions in [research-plan.md](research-plan.md) — particularly willingness to pay, curriculum-change authority, and faculty attitudes to external skills data — can be pursued directly with the people now asking Q2 and Q3.

---

## The Honest Boundary

COMPASS answers two of Council's four questions well (Q2, Q3), informs a third in part (Q1, its economic dimension only), and does not address the fourth (Q4). That is a strong and defensible position — provided it is stated that way. The temptation in a moment of institutional attention is to position COMPASS as answering all four. It does not, and claiming otherwise would forfeit the evidentiary credibility that is COMPASS's entire reason for existing.
