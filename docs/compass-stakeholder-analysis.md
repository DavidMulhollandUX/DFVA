# Stakeholder Analysis: COMPASS and the Curriculum–Labour Market Intelligence Market

**Team expertise:** Service Design, Customer Experience, Digital Transformation, AI — Higher Education sector.

**COMPASS context:** COMPASS (Curriculum Outcomes and Market Proof Alignment Scoring System) is a digital assessment tool evaluating whether university degree programs will retain economic value in an AI-augmented labour market. It produces structured viability assessments across ten dimensions, real-time market intelligence by job family, and curriculum redesign roadmaps. The venture sits at the intersection of labour market intelligence platforms, curriculum management systems, faculty professional development, and higher education quality assurance — four markets undergoing concurrent disruption from generative AI.

## 2. Who Are the Essential Players?

### Group A: Direct Participants

#### A1. Labour Market Intelligence Platform Vendors

**Key companies:**
- **Lightcast** (US, KKR-backed since 2019; formed from 2021 merger of Burning Glass Technologies and EMSI; estimated revenue USD $250–500M; ~700 employees)
- **Faethm by Pearson** (Sydney-founded 2017; acquired by Pearson September 2021 for £65M total consideration; now part of Pearson's Enterprise Learning and Skills division; ~23 employees globally as of 2026)
- **QS Quacquarelli Symonds** (UK; £50.5M total 2023 turnover across all products including QS LMI)
- **LinkedIn Talent Insights** (Microsoft; bundled into enterprise Microsoft agreements; confirmed NSW government contract AUD $565,352 for Recruiter + Talent Insights, 2024–26)
- **Revelio Labs** and **Textkernel** (specialist niche vendors)

**What they care about most:** Expanding into new buyer segments while protecting the proprietary value of their skills taxonomy and data aggregation infrastructure; sustaining high retention rates (Lightcast's broader group churn is approximately 1%); establishing data partnerships with employers and governments that deepen competitive moats.

**What frustrates them:** The higher education segment is structurally fragmented and individually low-value compared to corporate clients — a single Australian university contract delivers significantly less revenue than a single large corporate client, yet requires comparable implementation and relationship management. No existing curriculum management system has an integration pathway into their platforms. Their primary university buyer (careers offices and IR/IE) does not hold curriculum decision-making authority, creating a structural disconnect between data consumption and the decisions that would generate their largest value.

**Power and influence: HIGH** in the broader workforce intelligence market; **LOW–MEDIUM** in directly influencing curriculum decisions at Australian universities. Lightcast has a confirmed AUD $255,763 contract with the Victorian Department of Jobs, Skills, Industry and Regions (2024) and a documented multi-year relationship with the University of Melbourne's Careers and Employability team since 2017, producing 115 custom Study Area Reports annually. Faethm/Pearson is effectively absent from Australian HE — Pearson Australia operates as a traditional publisher, and the only confirmed Australian Faethm engagement is with the Australian Digital Health Agency.

*Sources: TechnologyOne ASX announcement (2024); Lightcast University of Melbourne case study (lightcast.io); MarketScreener, Pearson acquisition of Faethm (2021); GovMarket.com.au (DJSIR Victoria–Lightcast contract, 2024); buy.nsw.gov.au (LinkedIn NSW contract, 2024); Tracxn Faethm profile (2026).*

#### A2. Curriculum Management System Vendors

**Key companies:**
- **CourseLoop** (acquired by TechnologyOne for AUD $60M in November 2024; AUD $9.1M ARR at acquisition; ~6.6x ARR multiple; confirmed Australian clients: Monash, UTS, QUT, Newcastle, Wollongong, UNSW, Murdoch, Flinders, UniSQ, Charles Sturt, James Cook University)
- **Akari Software** (Cork, Ireland; UK G-Cloud base annual licence £22,500/year; confirmed Australian clients: University of Sydney, University of Tasmania, Swinburne, Bond, Curtin, University of the Sunshine Coast)
- **TechnologyOne OneEducation** (broader ERP suite encompassing SIS, CMS, and timetabling; claims 60%+ of Australian and NZ HE institutions for student management; FY2024 total revenue AUD $515.4M, 30% PBT margin; education vertical grew 24% in FY2024; group churn ~1%)
- **Coursedog** (US-only; no Australian presence confirmed)

**What they care about most:** Replacing manual curriculum governance (Word, Excel, email approval chains) with structured digital workflows; retaining clients through deep integration with SIS and LMS environments; expanding platform scope from curriculum approval into broader academic planning and graduate outcome tracking.

**What frustrates them:** Academic governance processes are inherently idiosyncratic — each institution has unique approval hierarchies, committee structures, and accreditation requirements requiring bespoke configuration. No commercial incentive currently exists to integrate LMI data into CMS platforms — neither the vendors nor their university clients have yet demanded it, and the technical complexity of an LMI data partnership would require unpriced investment.

**Power and influence: HIGH** as infrastructure owners. Once CourseLoop or Akari is embedded in a university's governance workflow, replacement is operationally and politically costly. Any LMI-to-curriculum integration must route through these systems, making TechnologyOne and Akari structural gatekeepers for any product attempting to embed labour market intelligence into curriculum governance in Australia. TechnologyOne's acquisition of CourseLoop makes it the single most strategically significant infrastructure entity in the Australian HE curriculum technology market.

*Sources: TechnologyOne ASX announcement (November 2024); TechnologyOne FY2024 full-year results; Vista Point Advisors (CourseLoop acquisition, 2024); CourseLoop G-Cloud 14 listing, UK Digital Marketplace (£40,600/year base); Akari Software G-Cloud 14 listing (£22,500/year base).*

#### A3. Quality Assurance and Accreditation Bodies

**Key organisations:**
- **TEQSA** (registers and regulates all 203 Australian HE providers under the HESF 2021)
- **Engineers Australia**, **AACSB** (business), **AHPRA** (health professions), **Australian Psychology Accreditation Council**, **Australian Medical Council**, legal profession admitting bodies

**What they care about most:** TEQSA's stated focus across 2023–2025 publications has been assessment integrity in the context of generative AI — specifically ensuring assessment processes remain tamper-proof and that awarded qualifications reflect genuine learning. TEQSA's September 2025 publication *Enacting Assessment Reform in a Time of Artificial Intelligence* describes three reform pathways all framed around learning assurance, not labour market durability. Professional accreditors focus on ensuring graduates meet the entry requirements for licensed professional practice.

**What frustrates them:** TEQSA must balance regulatory proportionality with quality assurance across a diverse provider population. Curriculum revision processes are described by practitioners as "cumbersome, inflexible, and lengthy," consuming approximately 200 hours of senior academic time per change (HERDSA, 2024). TEQSA's guidance to date has no mechanism to assess whether a compliant, accredited degree produces graduates prepared for AI-augmented workplaces — a gap explicitly identified in the ACSES framework (December 2025) and unaddressed by any Australian regulatory body.

**Power and influence: VERY HIGH** — TEQSA can impose conditions on institutional registration and revoke registration. Professional accreditors can withdraw accreditation for specific programs, removing graduates' eligibility for licensed professional practice. However, universities self-accredit their courses without government approval (Centre for Independent Studies, *Job-Ready Graduates 2.0*, 2024), meaning significant curriculum design authority rests with institutions, not the regulator.

*Sources: TEQSA, Enacting Assessment Reform (September 2025); ACSES, Australian Framework for AI in Higher Education (December 2025); HERDSA 2024 Annual Conference paper (Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education, July 2024); Centre for Independent Studies (2024).*

### Group B: Influencers

#### B1. Government Bodies and Peak Organisations

**Key organisations:**
- **Universities Australia** (peak body; coordinates DVC-Academic representatives in twice-yearly sector meetings)
- **Australian Department of Education / DESE** (administers CGS funding, HECS-HELP, QILT data collection, and Universities Accord implementation)
- **Jobs and Skills Australia (JSA)** (publishes Internet Vacancy Index, Emerging Roles Report, Australian Skills Classification; confirmed Lightcast data user for its AI skills analysis)
- **ATEC** (established April 2026; focused on access, equity, and system stewardship)
- **Group of Eight (Go8)** (disproportionate influence given their concentration of enrolments)

**What they care about most:** The Australian Universities Accord Final Report (February 2024, 47 recommendations) established a sector-wide expectation that universities align academic offerings with Australia's workforce needs — creating formal accountability for labour market alignment that was previously absent. JSA's 2024 Emerging Roles Report identifies 37 emerging roles with 35% of occupations showing higher AI skills job ad growth.

**What frustrates them:** Accord implementation mechanisms are underfunded and rely on industry co-design processes that are structurally slow. The abolition of the OLT in 2016 eliminated cross-institutional knowledge exchange infrastructure for sector-wide T&L innovation with no equivalent established. Universities Australia publicly pushed back on TEQSA's scope in 2023, arguing "it remains unclear what, as a sector, needs to change when individual providers adapt their course and program offerings to include AI technologies."

**Power and influence: VERY HIGH** (government, through funding mechanisms and regulatory authority); **HIGH** (Universities Australia, through collective advocacy); **MEDIUM** (JSA, through data publication that shapes the narrative without direct curriculum authority).

*Sources: Australian Universities Accord Final Report (February 2024); JSA Emerging Roles Report (November 2024); Universities Australia response to TEQSA consultation (October 2023).*

### Group C: End Users (Segmented by Type)

#### C1. DVC Academic / DVC Education

**Profile:** The most senior executive with direct accountability for educational quality, program design, and student outcomes. Accountable to the Vice-Chancellor for "the academic performance of the University through effective leadership" and "the quality and overall design of academic programs" (RMIT DVC Education position description, 2024). Sits on the Vice-Chancellor's Executive; holds budget oversight of the education portfolio; typically chairs or holds senior membership of the Academic Board. In 2024, Australia had 142 DVCs across the sector, up from 113 in 2001 (Future Campus, March 2026, citing HEIMS data).

**What they care about most:** Academic program quality; student access and experience; teaching innovation and digital enablement; industry partnerships; compliance with TEQSA Threshold Standards; demonstrating labour market alignment following the Accord's recommendations.

**What frustrates them:** The pace of AI change outstripping the speed of institutional policy response — documented explicitly in the University of Sydney's DVC(E) submission to TEQSA's assessment reform consultation (authored by then-DVC(E) Professor Joanne Wright, 2023). The "traditional university culture characterized by change aversion, complex bureaucratic processes, and a research-dominated academic workload allocation and promotion system poses significant obstacles to teaching quality enhancement" (HERDSA, 2024). The Accord's workforce alignment mandate requires co-design, microcredentials, WIL, and industry partnership that must be orchestrated across multiple faculties simultaneously.

**Power and influence: VERY HIGH** — the DVC Academic is the decision-maker for curriculum technology investment within institutional budget delegations (typically authority to approve contracts up to AUD $100,000–$250,000 without escalation) and the executive champion required for any new curriculum tool to achieve adoption. This is the most critical "yes" in the purchasing process.

*Sources: RMIT DVC Education position description (2024); UNSW DVC Education recruitment advertisement (2024); Universities Australia DVC Academic documentation (2021); TEQSA Guidance Note: Academic Governance (Version 3.1, November 2023); Future Campus (March 2026).*

---

#### C2. Curriculum Coordinators and Program Directors

**Profile:** Mid-level academic staff — variously titled Program Director, Course Coordinator, Honours Coordinator, or Unit Coordinator — responsible for "the practical and everyday process of supporting, managing, developing and inspiring academic colleagues" (HERDSA, Vol. 39, 2016). They implement curriculum decisions, manage renewal and accreditation cycles, and support student success across large cohorts, often while carrying full teaching loads.

**What they care about most:** Ensuring learning outcomes are met and assessment standards are consistent; managing curriculum renewal in line with AQF and TEQSA Threshold Standards; protecting the quality of the student experience within their programs. When labour market signals reach them, it is typically through informal channels — industry contacts, anecdotal employer feedback, QILT data reviewed during accreditation cycles — rather than structured intelligence.

**What frustrates them:** Administrative overload combined with full teaching duties — one documented Program Director accumulated approximately 200 hours of curriculum revision work over four weeks on top of normal teaching duties, with the curriculum revision system described as "cumbersome, inflexible, and lengthy" (HERDSA 2024 Annual Conference paper, *Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education*, July 2024, DOI: 10.1080/09650792.2024.2385410). Coordinators have "little if any formal power" despite their work directly affecting large student populations (HERDSA, Vol. 39, 2016). Research-dominated promotion culture deprioritises curriculum coordination work.

**Power and influence: MEDIUM** operationally (they implement curriculum decisions); **LOW** structurally (they cannot initiate major curriculum change without Academic Board processes and lack budget authority to procure new tools). They are the primary users of any curriculum intelligence tool, but are rarely its buyers.

*Sources: HERDSA 2024 Annual Conference paper (Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education, July 2024); HERDSA Research and Development in Higher Education, Vol. 39 (2016); TEQSA compliance documentation, Flinders University (2023).*

---

#### C3. Institutional Research / Institutional Effectiveness (IR/IE) Offices

**Profile:** The office responsible for data collection, analysis, and interpretation to support institutional planning, policy formation, and decision-making. Defined by AAIR (est. 1988) as providing information that "assists institutional leaders by informing their planning and decision-making." Functions include enrolment tracking, attrition and retention analysis, academic program performance, financial analytics, and government compliance reporting (QILT, HEIMS).

**What they care about most:** Data accuracy and accessibility; connecting disparate institutional data sets; providing analytics that executives will act on; meeting government reporting obligations efficiently; moving from a compliance-reporting function toward a strategic advisory role (EAB, *Insights from IR Leaders*, 2023).

**What frustrates them:** Data silos are the dominant structural pain point: "every institution spoke about the existence of data silos across its campus, which act as a strong impediment to enhancing the use of data for decision-making" (APLU, *Data Analytics: Uses, Challenges, and Best Practices at Public Research Universities*). Poor data quality, access restrictions between organisational units, and the conflict between reporting obligations and strategic analytical work are consistently documented barriers. 82% of VPs of Institutional Effectiveness were the first to hold the title at their institution, reflecting an emerging function globally that has not yet developed mature workflows or authority structures (EAB, 2023).

**Power and influence: MEDIUM** — IR offices hold the data most directly relevant to curriculum performance (SES, GOS, ESS, attrition) but operate in a consultative, advisory capacity. Their influence depends entirely on whether DVC executives choose to act on their analysis. In US universities, IR/IE is the documented primary buyer of LMI subscriptions (confirmed at Cal State LA and University of Minnesota). The Australian pattern is partially confirmed by the University of Melbourne Careers and Employability team using Lightcast since 2017.

*Sources: AAIR About page (2024); AAIR Academic Research Institutional Analytics SIG (Chair Eva Seidel, 2024); APLU Data Analytics report; EAB Insights from IR Leaders (2023); Lightcast University of Melbourne case study.*

---

#### C4. Employers and Industry Partners

**Profile:** The range of organisations that hire graduates, participate in industry advisory boards, and fund industry partnerships with universities. Includes large professional services firms, technology companies, healthcare systems, government departments, and SMEs.

**What they care about most:** Job-specific technical skills are ranked by employers as their top hiring priority, while educators rank them last — instead prioritising soft skills (Cengage Group, *2025 Graduate Employability Report*, September 2025, n=865 employers). Only 54% of employers believe colleges are graduating students with the skills their organisations need; 69% of recent hires require moderate or significant additional training (Lumina-Gallup, *Aligning Education and Work*, March 2026). The QILT Employer Satisfaction Survey 2024 (published September 2025) found that 42.7% of supervisors nominated employability and enterprise skills as the top area needing improvement, even as overall satisfaction reached 85.5% — the highest since 2016. AI readiness is an emerging priority: 46% of employers cite AI as a reason for reduced entry-level hiring; 53% report they cannot find graduates with the right AI skills (Pearson/AWS, *AI Readiness*, April 2026).

**What frustrates them:** Time commitment is the dominant barrier to advisory board participation — over 80% of industry respondents agreed their engagement might negatively impact colleagues' workload, with time (median = 5/5 on importance scale) and professional workload management (median = 4.5/5) as the primary barriers (PMC, *Engaging Industry in Health Professions' Education*, 2023, PMC10298709). Technology access barriers (university firewalls preventing practitioners from accessing LMS) and difficulty translating professional expertise into academic contexts create structural friction.

**Power and influence: HIGH** indirectly (through professional accreditation requirements, QILT ESS data shaping institutional reputation, and enrolment demand for employer-preferred credentials); **LOW** directly (advisory board participation is non-binding and structurally infrequent). The Universities Accord (February 2024) elevated the formal expectation of employer influence through industry co-design provisions, but the mechanisms for translating employer input into curriculum change remain structurally slow.

*Sources: Cengage Group 2025 Graduate Employability Report; QILT ESS 2024 National Report (September 2025); Lumina-Gallup Aligning Education and Work (March 2026); Pearson/AWS AI Readiness (April 2026); PMC10298709 (2023).*

#### C5. Prospective Students and Families

**Profile:** Individuals making degree selection decisions — predominantly school-leavers aged 17–18, plus mature-age, career-change, and international students. Primary information proxies used are ATAR entry scores, institutional reputation, and peer/family advice.

**What they care about most:** Career and employment outcomes — but this aspiration does not translate into systematic use of available outcomes data at the point of decision. Many applicants draw on "chance encounters and questionable sources" for course selection (James, R., *Journal of Institutional Research in Australasia*, Vol. 9, 2000). A third of first-year students were "not ready to choose a university course" during their final year of school. Information asymmetry is compounded for disadvantaged students: students from low SES backgrounds "are commonly directed towards VET and TAFE pathways and are also more likely to receive little to no advice at all" (ACSES, *The great social divide*).

**What frustrates them:** The QILT GOS-L 2024 finding that 30–40% of employed Australian graduates are not fully utilising their skills and education in their current job is not communicated in university marketing or government information tools. 48% of 2025 graduates felt unprepared to apply for entry-level roles; 56% of those cite job-specific skills as their primary gap (Cengage 2025) — information prospective students cannot access before committing to a degree.

**Power and influence: HIGH** in aggregate (enrolment choices determine revenue under demand-driven funding); **LOW** individually (curriculum governance structures offer students minimal formal decision-making power; GOS and SES create retrospective accountability but operate after the fact).

*Sources: QILT GOS-L 2024 National Report; Cengage 2025 Graduate Employability Report; James, R. (2000); ACSES, The great social divide; Pires et al., Journal of Community Psychology (2022, PMC9543643); Arnup, Edwards & Baker, Australian Journal of Education (2025).*

## 3. How Does Value Flow?

### Money

**Primary flows in the Australian HE sector (FY2024):**

Australia's 42 universities reported total revenue of AUD $45.17 billion in FY2024 (Department of Education Finance 2024), with a sector surplus of AUD $2.14 billion (4.7% net margin).

| Flow | Amount | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Commonwealth Grant Scheme (CGS) to universities | AUD $7.8B (2024) | Per-EFTSL; not per-graduate or per-employment outcome |
| HECS-HELP (government lending on behalf of students) | AUD $5.3B | Per-enrolment; counted as university income |
| Upfront student contributions | AUD $646M | Per-enrolment |

The Job-Ready Graduates reforms (2021) created extreme discipline-level distortion: humanities, law, and business students now pay more than 90% of total course funding; nursing and teaching students pay less than 20%. Universities are still financially rewarded for headcount, not outcomes.

**Within the curriculum-LMI market:**

- **Universities → CMS vendors:** Approximately £40,600/year (CourseLoop UK G-Cloud base) to £22,500/year (Akari UK G-Cloud base) per institution, tiered by student numbers. Australian pricing not publicly disclosed.
- **Universities → LMI vendors:** The only verified Australian HE LMI relationship in the public record is the University of Melbourne–Lightcast subscription (since 2017, financial terms not disclosed). US secondary market sources suggest USD $20,000–$60,000/year for comparable institutional LMI subscriptions.
- **Government → Lightcast (confirmed):** AUD $255,763 contract with Victorian DJSIR, commencing November 2024, for provision of job advertisement data (GovMarket.com.au).

**Where margin concentrates:** In the CMS segment, TechnologyOne's 30% PBT margin on AUD $515M FY2024 revenue demonstrates the economics of a high-retention, low-churn (~1%) SaaS ERP. In the LMI segment, margins concentrate at the data aggregation and normalisation layer — Lightcast's proprietary dataset (2.5 billion job postings, 800 million career profiles, 100+ government data sources) creates switching costs that sustain pricing once institutional workflows are built around its outputs.

*Sources: Department of Education Finance 2024; TechnologyOne FY2024 full-year results; RBA Bulletin July 2025; CourseLoop G-Cloud 14 listing; Akari G-Cloud 14 listing; GovMarket.com.au.*

### Products and Services (Origin to End User)

The value chain from raw labour market signal to curriculum decision passes through five discrete stages, with a structural break between stages 2 and 3:

**Stage 1 — Raw signal generation:** Employers post job advertisements on SEEK, LinkedIn, Indeed, and sector boards. Governments collect labour force statistics (ABS), occupational classification data (ANZSCO), and earnings data.

**Stage 2 — Data aggregation and intelligence production:** LMI vendors (Lightcast, Faethm, JSA, NCVER) aggregate, deduplicate, classify, and transform raw postings into structured intelligence — skills taxonomies, occupational demand trends, automation risk scores, salary benchmarks. JSA's Emerging Roles Report uses Lightcast job posting data (7 million+ Australian postings, 2018–2023) as its primary source.

**Stage 3 — Institutional access and interpretation:** LMI data reaches institutions through IR/IE offices, careers and employability teams, or strategy offices. Translated into institutional reports, strategic presentations, or careers advisory materials. The University of Melbourne's 115 annual Study Area Reports using Lightcast data represent the most documented Australian example.

**→ Critical structural break:** Data rarely crosses from Stage 3 into Stage 4. No major Australian CMS has an LMI data integration. No LMI vendor has a named Australian CMS integration partner. The gap is organisational and structural, not technical.

**Stage 4 — Curriculum decision-making:** Academic boards, program directors, and DVC offices make curriculum investment decisions. These are informed primarily by TEQSA compliance requirements, professional accreditation standards, peer benchmarking, and faculty expertise. Where labour market data enters the room, it is typically carried in manually by a career services representative or a consultant commissioned for the purpose.

**Stage 5 — Curriculum publication and student choice:** Approved changes are published through CMS platforms (CourseLoop, Akari) to student-facing handbooks. Students select courses from a catalogue that may reflect labour market conditions from 2–7 years prior to their enrolment.

### Poorly Understood or Hidden Areas of the Value Chain

**1. Actual LMI penetration into Australian curriculum decisions.** Australian universities are not required to publish contract registers. The University of Melbourne's Lightcast relationship (since 2017) is the only publicly documented Australian HE LMI subscription. Whether this pattern replicates at 5, 10, or 30 other institutions is unknowable from public sources. Lightcast's Labour Insight ANZ infrastructure implies more subscribers than the one named university client — but the number, contract value, and churn are invisible.

**2. The informal intermediary layer between LMI data and curriculum decisions.** Consultancies (Nous Group via DIMA, Deloitte Access Economics, PwC) use LMI data in institutional reports, acting as informal intermediaries. These relationships are commercial-in-confidence. The margin between LMI subscription cost and consulting report fee — captured by consultancies — is entirely undocumented.

**3. The cost of curriculum misalignment per graduate.** No published Australian research quantifies the economic cost per graduate of being placed in a role misaligned with their credential. The QILT GOS-L 2024 finding that 30–40% of employed graduates are not fully utilising their education is a proxy for this cost; its dollar value is uncalculated.

**4. TechnologyOne's undisclosed LMI integration roadmap.** TechnologyOne's November 2024 acquisition announcement referenced "data-driven insights" and "graduate outcomes" as strategic goals for the combined entity. Whether this includes an LMI data partnership or a graduate outcomes module is not publicly disclosed. This undisclosed roadmap is potentially the most consequential variable in the Australian curriculum technology market.

**5. The economics of informal employer-curriculum flows.** The most influential employer signals often travel through personal relationships — informal conversations between faculty and industry contacts — rather than through formal advisory board mechanisms. These flows are entirely undocumented but may carry more curriculum change influence than the formal governance structures that appear in institutional documentation.

**6. Negative externalities of curriculum misalignment.** The value destruction caused by misaligned curricula — concentrated on students who carry the financial and career cost of credentials that do not perform in the labour market — does not appear in any actor's profit-and-loss statement. It is an externality borne by individuals and the broader economy, not by the institutions that designed the misaligned programs.

**1. The Future of Jobs Report 2025**
*World Economic Forum, January 2025*
Survey of 1,000+ employers representing 14 million workers across 55 economies. Projects 170 million new roles created and 92 million displaced by 2030 (net +78 million). AI and big data top the fastest-growing skills list. 63% of employers cite the skills gap as their primary barrier to business transformation.
🔗 https://reports.weforum.org/docs/WEF_Future_of_Jobs_Report_2025.pdf

**2. Agents, Robots, and Us: Skill Partnerships in the Age of AI**
*McKinsey Global Institute, November 2025*
Current technologies can technically automate 57% of U.S. work hours. Demand for AI fluency grew sevenfold in two years. More than 70% of employer-sought skills are used in both automatable and non-automatable work — framing the case for adaptive capability over technical skill alone.
🔗 https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/our-research/agents-robots-and-us-skill-partnerships-in-the-age-of-ai

**3. AI Readiness: Building the Bridge from Higher Education to Work**
*Pearson and Amazon Web Services, April 2026* (n=2,700+, six countries)
Only 14% of graduates report high AI tool proficiency; 53% of employers cannot find AI-ready graduates. The perception gap: 78% of HE leaders believe they meet employer expectations; only 28% of employers agree.
🔗 https://www.pearson.com/content/dam/global-store/global/resources/ai-readiness/AI-Readiness-Report-2026.pdf

**4. Aligning Education and Work: What Employers Say Higher Education Must Deliver**
*Lumina Foundation and Gallup, March 2026* (n=2,000 U.S. employers)
Only 54% of employers believe colleges graduate students with the right skills; 69% of recent hires require significant additional training. Workforce misalignment is the second leading driver of declining public confidence in higher education (after politicisation, before cost).
🔗 https://www.luminafoundation.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Lumina-Foundation-Gallup-SOHE_Employer-Report.pdf

**5. 2025 Graduate Employability Report**
*Cengage Group, September 2025* (n=865 employers, 971 graduates, 698 instructors, US)
Only 30% of 2025 graduates secured jobs in their field; 48% felt unprepared for entry-level roles. Critical finding: 89% of educators believe students are workforce-ready while nearly half of graduates disagree. Employers rank job-specific technical skills first; educators rank them last.
🔗 https://www.cengagegroup.com/news/press-releases/2025/cengage-group-2025-employability-report/

*TEQSA, September 2025*
Australia's primary regulatory guidance document on AI in higher education. The authoritative document for understanding the gap TEQSA leaves in curriculum-market alignment.
🔗 https://www.teqsa.gov.au/sites/default/files/2025-09/enacting-assessment-reform-in-a-time-of-artificial-intelligence.pdf
