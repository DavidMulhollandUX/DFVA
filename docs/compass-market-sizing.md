## 2. Market Context: The Opportunity

Universities globally face a structural inflection point. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025 projects that nearly 50% of all workforce skills will need updating by 2027, and that 92 million jobs will be displaced by AI by 2030 — even as 170 million new roles emerge requiring fundamentally different capabilities. This mismatch between what universities are teaching and what the labour market requires is not a marginal risk: it is an institutional governance problem. The question of whether a degree program produces employable graduates in an AI-augmented economy is now a quality assurance, accreditation, and reputational risk question that curriculum committees cannot answer with existing tools. COMPASS is purpose-built to fill this gap.

The EdTech sector provides the macro market context. GlobalData's primary databook (*EdTech: Global Industry Data 2021–2030*, February 2026) sizes the global EdTech market at $333.4 billion in 2025, growing to $513.7 billion by 2030 at a sustained CAGR of approximately 9%. Higher Education is the single largest EdTech sub-segment at 39.8% of the global market — approximately $132.8 billion in 2025, forecast to reach $199 billion by 2030. Within that, the curriculum management software vertical — COMPASS's closest peer category — is sized at $2.1–3.0 billion globally in 2023–2024 with CAGR estimates of 12.5%–15.5% across multiple research firms, making it among the fastest-growing EdTech sub-segments. The broader EdTech SaaS market reached approximately $43 billion in 2024 and is growing at 13.5–13.7% CAGR through 2034, with higher education representing an estimated $17–22 billion of that total. COMPASS targets the institutional analytics and curriculum intelligence layer of this SaaS market — a niche that, at current market development, has no dominant global incumbent.

Australia represents an exceptionally favourable launch market. IBISWorld's June 2026 report on University and Other Higher Education in Australia (P8102) confirms 41 institutions generating AUD $45.4 billion in revenue in 2025–26, with the industry under acute structural pressure: international student revenue has collapsed under federal visa policy changes and National Planning Level caps, profit margins have fallen to 5.3% (down 9.6 percentage points over five years), and institutions are actively eliminating courses and restructuring workforce at scale. This is precisely the environment in which evidence-based curriculum investment decisions become a board-level priority. The GlobalData Australia EdTech profile (March 2026) separately confirms a USD $1.17 billion Higher Education EdTech sub-market in Australia growing at approximately 8.8% CAGR to USD $1.78 billion by 2030. Critically, the IBISWorld Online Education report (X0008, May 2026) explicitly identifies AI as "creating a tension between accessibility and integrity" and notes that institutions are actively redesigning assessments and curriculum in response — confirming institutional readiness for a tool like COMPASS.

The demand signal from adjacent workforce analytics markets reinforces the thesis. The global workforce analytics market is sized at $1.6–3.5 billion in 2024–2025 and growing at 12–16% CAGR. A GlobalData case study of HR analytics adoption found that 113 of 114 surveyed enterprises planned to expand analytics investment, yet only 29% of HR executives rated themselves capable of acting on analytics outputs — an implementation gap that signals strong unmet demand for decision-support tools that translate data into action. Universities face the same analytics capability gap at the curriculum level. COMPASS addresses this by converting publicly available program data into an actionable viability score and redesign roadmap, removing the analytical burden from curriculum teams who currently lack the tools and methodology to assess AI-era relevance systematically.

## 4. Competitive Market Context

No dominant global incumbent in the specific niche of AI-era curriculum viability analytics has been identified across any of the five research sources consulted. The curriculum management software market ($2.1–3.0B) is populated primarily by workflow and compliance tools — curriculum mapping, accreditation documentation, program review management — rather than labour-market-oriented scoring platforms.

**Adjacent market players:**
- **Workforce/people analytics SaaS:** Visier Solutions, Claro Workforce Analytics, Linear B, GattiHR *(GlobalData HR Analytics Case Study, Feb 2022)*. These platforms target HR departments and corporate employers, not universities.
- **Australian EdTech market:** Described by GlobalData as **highly fragmented**, dominated by US-headquartered content delivery platforms (Coursera, Udemy) and local school-level tools (ClickView, Cluey Learning). No curriculum analytics platform for higher education is identified as a domestic leader.
- **University IT spend:** Australian mid-sized universities are estimated to spend AUD $2–7M per year on software (derived from 3–5% IT/opex ratio; no published benchmark exists). Group of Eight universities at AUD $10–25M total annual IT spend. At a $15K–$35K subscription price point, COMPASS represents a sub-1% share of any institution's software budget — a low barrier to procurement approval.
- **SaaS adoption trajectory:** IBISWorld's Software Publishing report (J5420, Aug 2025) confirms Australian enterprises are migrating to SaaS at an 11% CAGR; the SaaS model is described as "driving resilience and growth" — supporting COMPASS's pricing and procurement model.
- **Analytics maturity gap:** Only 36% of workforce analytics platforms were reported to deliver actionable insights *(GlobalData, 2022)*, and only 29% of HR executives felt capable of acting on analytics data. The equivalent gap in higher education curriculum analytics is an unaddressed white space.

### Impact on TAM/SAM/SOM Estimates

**TAM (~$388M) — Validated; upward pressure noted.** Euromonitor confirms ~102,000 higher education institutions globally, with world higher education production growing at 5–8% CAGR depending on region. The prior TAM was conservatively derived bottom-up at $25K/institution across ~15,500 qualifying institutions. The Euromonitor institution count (broader definition including RTOs) does not challenge this — the addressable universe remains well above COMPASS's realistic penetration ceiling.

**SAM (~$80M) — Modest upward revision to ~$85–90M warranted.** North American higher education growing at 7.7% CAGR and Australian higher education at 5.2% CAGR supports expanding institutional technology budgets. The declining Australasia completion rate (−6.9pp) is a new, Euromonitor-sourced demand signal not captured in v2.0.

**SOM (Year 3: ~$4.5M ARR; Year 5: ~$10M ARR) — Unchanged.** Execution question, not a market-size question. The AUD 40B+ Australian higher education sector provides ample institutional scale.

- Intergenerational education persistence: 72% of adults with a tertiary-educated parent also attain tertiary; only 19% of those whose parents lacked upper secondary do *(OECD Education at a Glance 2024, Chapter A1, Box A1.1)*

**IMF — AI and Labour Market (Tier-1 Multilateral Source)**

- Approximately **40% of global employment is exposed to AI**; **60% in advanced economies**; 40% in emerging markets; 26% in low-income countries *(IMF Staff Discussion Note SDN/2024/001, January 2024)* — **the single most important new citation for the COMPASS pitch**
- Roughly half of AI-exposed jobs face downward wage/demand pressure; half face productivity augmentation upside *(IMF SDN/2024/001)*
- **44% of workers' skills will be disrupted** within 5 years; 6-in-10 need retraining before 2027, but only 50% have adequate access to training *(IMF SDN/2024/001)*
- **AI disproportionately impacts high-skilled and cognitive roles** — college-educated workers are more exposed than manual workers in advanced economies. Automation risk runs directly through university graduates and the programs that trained them *(IMF SDN/2024/001)* — this reverses the prior assumption that automation primarily threatened low-skilled work
- IMF explicitly recommends modernised education systems and lifelong learning as policy responses *(IMF SDN/2024/001; confirmed in Australia Article IV consultations, December 2024 and February 2026)*
- IMF 2025 Article IV Consultation — Australia: weak productivity growth flagged as the central structural problem; skills and human capital embedded in reform agenda *(IMF, February 2026)*

**QILT 2024 — Australian Graduate Outcomes**

- Australian undergraduate **median salary: AUD 71,000 (2023**, +4.4% YoY); postgraduate coursework: AUD 96,600; postgraduate research: AUD 100,000 *(QILT 2023 Graduate Outcomes Survey, Australian Government Dept of Education, May 2024)*
- **Full-time employment rate for domestic undergraduates fell to 74.0% in 2024** (from 79.0% in 2023); overall employment (including part-time) was 86.9% *(QILT 2024 Graduate Outcomes Survey National Report)*
- Australian graduates earn **AUD 12,500–14,000/year more** than non-graduates and are **2.5 times less likely to be unemployed**; salary grows >30% within 3 years of graduation *(Universities Australia / QILT longitudinal data, 2024)*
- Grattan Institute: **Go8 prestige confers only ~6% career earnings advantage** after controlling for discipline and background — **field of study drives ROI far more than institutional brand** *(Grattan Institute, 2024)* — directly legitimises COMPASS's curriculum-level (not institution-level) unit of analysis

**NBER Academic Evidence**

- Causal evidence that tasks with higher AI exposure see **reduced labour demand**; occupations where AI exposure is concentrated face the greatest net labour demand decline; overall employment effects modest due to productivity offsets at AI-adopting firms *(NBER Working Paper 33509, Hampole, Papanikolaou, Schmidt, Seegmiller, February 2025)*

### Why the IMF Finding Reframes the COMPASS Value Proposition

The prior market narrative positioned AI risk as primarily affecting low-skilled or routine roles. IMF SDN/2024/001 establishes the opposite: in advanced economies, **60% of jobs are AI-exposed, and it is college-educated knowledge workers — precisely the graduates universities produce — who face the highest exposure**. Combined with the QILT finding that full-time graduate employment fell from 79% to 74% in a single year, and the Grattan finding that field of study (not institutional brand) drives lifetime earnings, the data now supports a sharper COMPASS pitch:

> The credential premium is real but fragile. A degree from a prestigious institution guarantees nothing if the curriculum was designed for job families that AI is now restructuring. COMPASS makes curriculum-level AI exposure visible before it becomes a graduate employment crisis.
