## Executive Summary

The University of Melbourne's 41-program DFVA assessment reveals a portfolio that is structurally sound in traditional academic dimensions but systemically unprepared for the AI-augmented labour market its graduates are entering. The overall risk posture is one of moderate-but-fragile: the portfolio average sits at 23.07/36, with 33 programs (80%) in MODERATE RISK, 5 (12%) in HIGH RISK, 2 (5%) RESILIENT, and 1 (2%) CRITICAL. There are no programs that have comprehensively failed, but there are also very few that could be described as genuinely future-ready. The institution is not in crisis — it is in a slow drift toward misalignment that will become acutely visible within three to five years if the structural curriculum gap identified here is not addressed.

The single biggest systemic gap is D5 AI Literacy: 34 of 41 programs (83%) score 1 or below on a 0-3 scale, meaning that across the overwhelming majority of the portfolio, AI tools and concepts are either absent from or peripheral to core curriculum. This is not a technology problem or a resource problem — it is a curriculum governance problem. The four programs that score D5=3 (Master of Business Analytics, Master of Computer Science, Master of Business Administration, Master of Applied Business Analytics) demonstrate that AI literacy integration is achievable with existing institutional capacity. The remaining 37 programs have simply not been required to make it a priority. A 36-point total gap exists between the portfolio's current state and a minimum functional AI literacy threshold of D5 >= 2 across all programs — equivalent to substantive curriculum redesign in 34 programs.

The most urgent individual programs are the Master of Screenwriting (score 11/36, CRITICAL, three zero-dimension scores, existential threat requiring structural redesign rather than incremental improvement) and the three HIGH RISK programs in the Faculty of Business and Economics (Master of Business Administration Marketing 17/36, Master of Information Systems 18/36, Master of Advanced Social Enterprise 18/36). Of these, the Master of Information Systems presents the greatest institutional embarrassment: a technology-management program in 2026 with D5 AI Literacy at 1/3 sits below the nursing science degree, below urban horticulture, and below journalism in overall DFVA score. The Master of Actuarial Science (20/36), while technically in MODERATE RISK, represents the single most dangerous market-curriculum mismatch in the portfolio: the actuarial market has already bifurcated into declining traditional roles and surging data science roles, and the program is credentialling graduates for the compressing tier.

The key strategic opportunity is the portfolio's concentration of shared gaps across all 41 programs, which makes university-wide initiatives substantially more efficient than program-by-program fixes. Five shared solutions — a cross-disciplinary case-study bank, a centralised graduate outcome tracking platform, a modular AI literacy unit, a university-wide industry capstone programme, and a federated curriculum advisory panel — would collectively address 66% of all documented recommendation rows. Building each once at institutional level replaces 87 separate program-level projects. The quick-win potential from the 68 LOW-effort recommendations alone amounts to approximately 60 DFVA points distributed across 25-30 programs, potentially lifting the majority of mid-band MODERATE RISK programs toward the RESILIENT threshold at Program Coordinator level within six months.

**1. Modular AI Literacy Unit** (addresses 23+ programs, MEDIUM to HIGH effort per program, but built once centrally): A shared governance core covering NIST AI Risk Management Framework, AI ethics, and tool evaluation methodology, with discipline-specific case modules for healthcare, law, science, and design contexts. ## 7. Strategic Surprises and Anomalies

**The portfolio's most dangerous illusion: Master of Clinical Dentistry.** It scores 23/36 MODERATE RISK while carrying D5 AI Literacy = 0 — the identical raw AI literacy score as the CRITICAL-rated Master of Screenwriting. It achieves MODERATE RISK because its physical-layer moat (D1=3, D8=3, D6=3, B=3) is genuine: hands-on dental procedures cannot be performed remotely or algorithmically. But AI diagnostic tools — Pearl AI and Denti.AI specifically — are now transforming the diagnostic core of dental practice. A graduate with D5=0 has no framework for governing, evaluating, or contextualising these tools. The MODERATE RISK band provides a false comfort signal in precisely the specialty where AI clinical tool deployment is most advanced.

**The most jarring field-score contradiction: Information Systems scores below nursing, urban horticulture, and journalism.** The Master of Information Systems (18/36, HIGH RISK) is outscored by Master of Nursing Science (26/36), Master of Urban Horticulture (25/36), and Master of Journalism (23/36). A prospective student selecting information systems in 2026 on the basis of perceived technology-sector durability would be making a significantly worse investment than one choosing nursing or horticulture. This is institutionally important information that current programme materials do not convey.

**The strongest program by holistic margin: Master of Business Analytics (31/36).** It achieves RESILIENT without critical dimension collapse: D2=3, D3=3, D4=3, D5=3, D6=3, D9=3, D10=3, B=3. Only D1, D7, and D8 are below 3 (all at 2). This is the only RESILIENT program that also passes all three threshold questions (AI cannot produce 80% of first-two-year output; graduates are better positioned than today; graduates will be better positioned in five years). Crucially, at one year in duration, it is the shortest degree in the RESILIENT tier. The structural story is that analytics training integrates AI tool fluency, statistical technical depth, systems thinking about business problems, and strong employer connection — a combination the current AI-era employment market directly rewards.

**The hidden gems: Urban Horticulture and Environmental Law.** Master of Urban Horticulture (25/36) is almost entirely unknown outside the built-environment sector, yet it scores D1=3, D2=3, D6=3, D9=3, and the answer to Q1 (could AI produce 80% of first-two-year output?) is NO. Physical-layer inspection of urban plant health cannot be automated; urban heat island policy and tree canopy mandate targets provide structural tailwind; the integrated ecology, urban planning, and climate adaptation curriculum is genuinely multidisciplinary. This is the portfolio's most underrated risk/reward profile. Master of Environmental Law (24/36) is the only law or humanities programme in the cohort to achieve D5=2/3 — its 'AI and Justice' elective gives it the highest AI literacy score of any law or arts-adjacent program — and it scores D6=3, D9=3, D10=3, reflecting the structural tailwind from mandatory Australian climate disclosure legislation generating documented demand.

**The four identical Science masters profiles.** Master of Science (BioSciences), Master of Science (Physics), Master of Science (Chemistry), and Master of Biomedical Science each score exactly the same dimension vector: D1=2, D2=2, D3=3, D4=3, D5=1, D6=3, D7=3, D8=2, D9=2, D10=1, Bonus=3 — all 25/36, all MODERATE RISK. This is not coincidence; it is a Faculty of Science governance structure that has not incorporated AI literacy as a curriculum requirement. A single cross-Science AI literacy module shared across these four programs, alongside a shared graduate destination tracking system, would move all four from 25/36 to an estimated 27-28/36 without structural redesign.

**The professional accreditation illusion in Actuarial Science.** The Master of Actuarial Science's most important strategic insight is that professional credential protection does not shield graduates when accreditation examinations test the same techniques being automated by AI actuarial platforms. The fix is already available — making two existing optional subjects compulsory requires no structural redesign. The inaction cost is graduates entering a profession whose entry-level tier is compressing while carrying a credential designed for a different automation environment.

**The Applied Business Analytics AI paradox.** Master of Applied Business Analytics scores D5=3 and D3=3 — maximum AI literacy and maximum technical depth — yet totals only 20/36 MODERATE RISK, equivalent to programs that teach no AI at all. The paradox is resolved by D9 Curriculum Currency=1 (the program is teaching yesterday's AI tools without foundational adaptability), D8 Human and Relational=1, and D1 Automation Exposure=1 (the work these graduates are trained to do is itself automatable). AI literacy without judgment scaffolding (D4=2, D7=2) produces graduates fluent in current-generation tools but without runway when those tools evolve. Compare the Master of Business Analytics (31/36) which achieves the same D5=3 but additionally scores D2=3, D4=3, D9=3, D10=3 — the full judgment scaffolding that makes AI literacy durable rather than brittle.

## 8. Strategic Recommendations

**Recommendation 1: Establish a University-Wide AI Literacy Framework as a Graduate Attribute Requirement**

Mandate that all postgraduate programs include a minimum of one core subject embedding domain-specific AI literacy (D5 >= 2) by the end of 2027. Build a shared modular AI literacy unit centrally — a governance core (NIST AI RMF, AI ethics, tool evaluation methodology) with discipline-specific case modules for health, science, law, business, and design. Make this available as a licensed module that any faculty can contextualise, rather than requiring each program to develop its own.

This matters because 34 of 41 programs currently score D5=1 or below, creating a portfolio-wide gap between what graduates are trained to do and what employers are asking for. The AI literacy gap is not a technical problem or a resource problem — the four programs already at D5=3 demonstrate that integration is achievable with existing institutional capacity. A central module replaces 23+ separate program-level curriculum redesigns.

Success looks like: D5 mean across the portfolio rising from 1.22 to >= 2.0 within four years; all programs meeting D5 >= 2 by 2028-29; the portfolio achieving a minimum of five RESILIENT programs.

**Recommendation 2: Invest Immediately in the Master of Actuarial Science Before the Market Window Closes**

Convene an emergency curriculum review of MC-ACTSC with a single mandate: identify which existing optional units in Python, statistical modelling, and data analytics should become compulsory, and introduce one climate risk and sustainability unit responsive to ASRS mandatory disclosure requirements. Target implementation within one curriculum cycle (2027 intake).

This matters because the actuarial market has already bifurcated: traditional pricing and reserving roles are declining 12% year-on-year in ANZ while data scientist in insurance roles are growing 28% year-on-year. The Actuaries Institute is already reviewing its own curriculum for AI content — if UniMelb lags this review, MC-ACTSC loses accreditation-adjacent differentiation in the strongest structural demand signal the profession has produced in a decade. The fix requires no structural redesign; it requires making existing electives compulsory.

Success looks like: MC-ACTSC score rising from 20/36 to 26+/36; the program explicitly cited in Actuaries Institute materials as responsive to the AI curriculum review; measurable increase in graduates entering data science in insurance roles within three years.

**Recommendation 3: Establish a Centralised Graduate Outcome Intelligence System**

Commission the Institutional Research Office to build a centralised graduate tracking system providing program-level, specialisation-level destination data at role-title, industry, salary, and time-to-employment granularity. Integrate this data into public programme pages and annual curriculum review cycles for all 41 programs.

This matters because 16 programs score D10 Outcome Evidence=1/3, and the absence of role-title graduate destination data is the single most common evidence gap identified across all 41 market intelligence reports. The university cannot currently benchmark its own employment performance at program level; market assessments for programs like Computer Science, Climate Science, and Data Science are conservatively classified as Q3 (Curriculum Without Market) rather than Q1 (Stars) primarily because graduate destination data does not exist at the required granularity. Beyond viability assessment, this data is the input that curriculum advisory panels need to make evidence-based update decisions — without it, advisory panels operate on employer anecdote rather than systematic signal. A substantial head-start already exists: the University's Job Insights Reports (Careers Online / LiveAlumni) publish role-title-level destinations for 141 programmes (29,859 alumni, 2015–2025). These can seed the central system immediately for roughly 78% of the portfolio, and on their own provide a documented basis to lift D10 for 12 programmes — including two band reclassifications (see §3 and the companion [DFVA Graduate-Outcomes Evidence Report](dfva-graduate-outcomes-report.md)). The build task is therefore less "collect new data" than "operationalise and extend data the University already publishes".

Success looks like: D10 mean rising from 2.00 to >= 2.5 across the portfolio within three years; program-level graduate destination dashboards publicly available on all 41 programme pages; annual DFVA re-assessment using updated D10 scores producing measurable band reclassifications.

**Recommendation 4: Launch an Emergency Curriculum Review of the Faculty of Business and Economics**

Commission a faculty-level curriculum review of all six Business and Economics programs with explicit reference to the 14-point intra-faculty score spread and three HIGH RISK programs. The review should focus on: bringing Master of Information Systems, Master of Business Administration Marketing, and Master of Advanced Social Enterprise out of HIGH RISK within two curriculum cycles; identifying why the same faculty's Master of Business Analytics scores 31/36 while MBA Marketing scores 17/36; and establishing faculty-wide curriculum currency standards that prevent a 50% HIGH RISK rate from persisting in the most internationally competitive program category on campus.

This matters because Business and Economics is the single most strategically exposed faculty in the portfolio. It occupies the domain (management, marketing, information systems) where AI displacement pressure is advancing fastest, it has the widest internal score spread of any multi-program faculty, and 50% of its programs are HIGH RISK. International fee-paying students in MBA and information systems programs are exactly the market where competitor institutions are most aggressively modernising. The window for curriculum overhaul that preserves brand value is measured in years.

Success looks like: Zero HIGH RISK programs in Business and Economics within three curriculum cycles; the intra-faculty score spread reduced from 14 points to under 8 points; MBA and IS programs explicitly competitive with UNSW and Monash equivalents on AI literacy and outcome evidence dimensions.

**Recommendation 5: Commission a Strategic Review of the Master of Screenwriting with Explicit Viability Assessment**

Convene a review panel with Dean of Arts authority to determine whether the Master of Screenwriting should be (a) comprehensively redesigned as a transmedia narrative and interactive media degree with dual-skill pathways, (b) consolidated with another creative arts degree to achieve critical mass for redesign investment, or (c) suspended pending redesign. This review should be completed and a decision made by end of 2026.

This matters because the Master of Screenwriting is the only CRITICAL-rated program in a 41-program portfolio, scoring 11/36 with three zero-dimension scores (D1, D5, D7) and answering YES to Q1 (AI can produce 80% of first-two-year graduate output) and NO to Q3 (graduates will not be more employable in five years than today). Unlike every other program in the portfolio where incremental improvement is viable, Screenwriting requires structural redesign. The degree's current curriculum identity is centred on exactly what large language models now produce at scale. The dual-skill pathway recommendation — a screenwriting graduate with a genuine second technical domain such as gaming narrative, interactive media, or digital production — is the most ambitious but potentially the only viable path to genuine future-viability. Continued enrolment under the current structure without a redesign commitment serves neither prospective students nor the institution.

Success looks like: A decision made and communicated by end of 2026; if redesign proceeds, a new curriculum structure achieving a projected DFVA score of >= 22/36 before implementation; if consolidation or suspension proceeds, an academically and reputationally managed transition with affected students supported.
