# Evidura — Failure Story: Well-Funded Competitor Free Entry

**Deep-dive scenario:** Lightcast (KKR-backed) enters Australia in 2027 with a free product, killing Evidura's $49K/yr pricing model.

**Prepared by:** David Mulholland · **Date:** 2026-06-30
**Builds on:** [Business model](evidura-business-model.md) · [Competitive intelligence](compass-competitive-strategic-intelligence.md) · [Brand strategy](compass-brand-strategy.md) · [Market sizing](compass-market-sizing.md)

## 1. The Failure Story

### January 2027 — The email that broke the model

It's a Sydney summer morning. The Evidura team is preparing for a Q1 sales push — two Go8 universities and four mid-tiers are in late-stage procurement, all hovering around $32K–$49K annual commitments. The pipeline is real. The benchmark dataset now covers 87 programs across 11 institutions. The methodology white paper has been accepted at an academic conference. The MCP tools have been cited by three AI agents in curriculum governance contexts. Everything is tracking to the business-model plan.

Then the DVC-Academic at a Go8 university forwards an email from Lightcast's new APAC Managing Director, hired out of Blackboard's Sydney office in November 2026. The subject line reads: *"Introducing Lightcast Skillabi — free for all Australian universities, now through 2029."*

The body is short. Lightcast has launched Skillabi in Australia as a **no-cost institutional licence** for all 41 universities. No per-seat fee. No annual subscription. No pilot charge. The offer includes: full course-description and syllabus analysis, the Skills Extractor AI engine, the Program-to-Occupation Alignment Score, curriculum modelling (simulating score impact of proposed changes), and a dedicated APAC customer success team — all free for three years.

The cover note from the DVC is two lines: *"This looks like what Evidura does, but free. Can you help me understand the difference?"*

### February 2027 — Procurement freezes

Within four weeks, every active Evidura deal freezes. The pattern is identical across institutions: procurement offices flag Evidura against a free Lightcast alternative, budget-conscious DVCs pause decisions, and — critically — the conversation shifts from *"should we buy a durability rating?"* to *"why would we pay $49K for something we can get free?"*

The Evidura sales team's counter-arguments are all true but none of them land in a procurement memo:

- *"Lightcast doesn't produce a durability rating — it's a skills-alignment tool, not a rating agency."* True but irrelevant to a budget officer comparing two line items. Both analyse programs. Both produce scores. One costs $49K. One costs $0. The procurement logic is brutal and binary.
- *"Lightcast's ANZ data is US-trained — it doesn't understand TEQSA, AQF, or QILT."* True but Lightcast's Skills Extractor doesn't need to. It extracts skills from curriculum text using the same NLP pipeline it uses globally. Labelling the output "US-centric" sounds like an excuse, not a differentiator, to people who haven't lived the methodology.
- *"Free means Lightcast is burning KKR cash to kill us — they'll raise prices once we're gone."* True but the statement is self-defeating. It admits Evidura can't survive a price war, which makes the argument for switching *stronger*, not weaker. No one wants to be the last customer of a dead platform.
- *"Our independence structure means we're not captured by any institution — Lightcast answers to KKR."* True but independence is a premium feature, not a survival feature. A university with 5.3% margins and course eliminations underway does not pay a 100% premium for governance architecture.

### March 2027 — The pilot institutions reconsider

The worst damage hits the reference network. Three of the five Go8 design partners who received free pilot ratings in 2026 begin piloting Lightcast Skillabi alongside Evidura — and Lightcast deploys two full-time APAC customer success representatives to support them. These are the same institutions Evidura counted on for paid conversion in Year 2. The pilots were free. Now the competitor is free *and* has more support staff on the ground than Evidura's entire team.

One dean tells the Evidura team privately: *"I believe in what you're building. But my CFO is cutting 27 programs this year. I can't take a $49K line item to the budget committee when there's a free alternative the other Go8s are trialling. I'm sorry."*

### June 2027 — The accreditation play collapses

Evidura's secondary revenue strategy — accreditation body partnerships (AACSB, AMC) — was meant to provide the revenue bridge while the institutional base grew. The Year 2 plan assumed 1–2 accreditation body conversations would be in negotiation. Instead, AACSB invites Lightcast to present at its Asia-Pacific conference. Lightcast proposes embedding Skillabi alignment data directly into AACSB Standard 4.1 evidence submissions — for free — as a sector-development initiative.

The Evidura team's positioning — *"we are the independent durability rating agency for the sector"* — suddenly sounds like a commercial vendor asking for money while a global firm is offering to help the sector for free. The framing is devastating: Lightcast isn't selling to universities; it's *giving them data* while Evidura is *charging them to be rated*.

### September 2027 — Revenue collapses

The Evidura pipeline, which had projected $920K in Year 2 revenue from 12–15 institutions, delivers $87,000 — one mid-tier university that signed before the Lightcast announcement, and five single-program purchases at $4,900 each from institutions using Evidura to triangulate what Lightcast is telling them for free.

Staff costs are $670K. Infrastructure and legal costs are $220K. Total costs: $890K. Revenue: $87K. The burn rate is $67K/month with $400K remaining from the seed round. Runway: six months, down from the projected eighteen.

The business model's Year 2 P&L — projected at +$30K EBITDA — is running at –$803K.

### December 2027 — The exit

The board faces three options:

1. **Drop prices to free and burn remaining capital competing on methodology alone.** This would require raising another $1.5M–$2M to survive 2028, with no clear path to monetisation other than outlasting Lightcast's free period — a bet that KKR's patience runs out before Evidura's money does. KKR has $500B+ AUM. Evidura has $400K.
2. **Pivot to a different product entirely** — sell the benchmark dataset to government, become a consultancy, drop the rating-agency model. This abandons the core value proposition and the team's expertise.
3. **Return capital and wind down.**

The board chooses option three. Evidura Pty Ltd enters voluntary administration in February 2028. The benchmark dataset — 87 programs, 18 months of cross-institutional durability scores — is acquired by Lightcast for a nominal sum as part of the wind-down. The methodology white paper, the MCP tools, the brand — all become assets of a company that gave away its product to eliminate the only domestic competitor in the niche.

The Australian LMI-to-curriculum market now has one player. It's free through 2029. And it answers to KKR.

## 2. The Underlying Assumption That Broke

### The explicit assumption (from multiple strategy documents)

> **"An ~18–36 month window before the global incumbents notice the gap."**
> — Brand strategy, §1 (line 33), echoed in business model risks (§9) and brand strategy conclusion (line 168)

The entire Evidura financial model, moat strategy, and timeline rested on this assumption. The Year 1–3 projection — build the benchmark dataset, establish the reference network, prove the methodology, achieve break-even at Month 18–22, spin out as independent entity, reach $1.64M ARR by Year 3 — assumes Evidura has 18–36 months *before facing competition*.

### Why the assumption was false

The assumption treated the Australian market as a *discovery problem* — the global competitors haven't noticed the gap, and when they do they'll need time to localise. In reality, for KKR-backed Lightcast, Australia is:

1. **A solved distribution problem, not a discovery problem.** Lightcast already serves Australian universities through its Labour Insight product (University of Melbourne has used it since 2017). The sales relationships, procurement channels, and institutional contacts already exist. Launching Skillabi in Australia is not entering a new market — it's adding a new SKU to an existing customer base.

2. **A rounding error, not a revenue decision.** Lightcast's parent (KKR, $500B+ AUM) can allocate a $2M–$5M APAC launch budget — covering staffing, free licences, and conference presence — as a *strategic investment*, not a P&L line. Three years of free Skillabi in Australia costs KKR less than one mid-cap deal team's annual travel budget. The calculus is not "can we make money in Australia?" — it's "can we prevent anyone else from building a defensible position in a market we may want later?"

3. **Visible from the outside.** The competitive intelligence document itself documents five acquisitions by Lightcast in 2025 alone, KKR backing, active consolidation strategy, and a product relaunch positioning it for "higher education's relevance crisis." A company acquiring five businesses in one year is scanning globally for opportunities. The assumption that they would need 18–36 months to "notice" Australia requires believing a KKR-backed consolidator with existing Australian customers, existing ANZ data infrastructure, and an active M&A strategy would miss a market where no domestic competitor exists and universities are publicly restructuring courses.

4. **The 18–36 month window was never about noticing — it was about *caring*.** The real bet was not that Lightcast wouldn't *see* Australia. It was that Lightcast wouldn't *bother* — that Australia (41 universities, ~$1.5M institutional ARR) is too small to matter. That's a pricing-power assumption, not a discovery assumption. And it breaks the moment a competitor decides that $5M over three years is worth paying to kill the only domestic entrant who could build a path-dependent moat (the benchmark dataset) before they arrive.

### The deeper assumption: that price is a feature, not a weapon

The Evidura pricing philosophy (§2 of the business model) rests on the idea that *"the rating must never be free — free signals are worth what you pay for them."* This is correct for a rating agency that has already *won* the standard — Moody's, Morningstar, and NABERS can charge for their ratings because they are the *established* signal. But for a rating agency that is *trying to become the standard*, the competitor's free product doesn't need to be better — it only needs to be *good enough* to prevent Evidura from ever crossing the credibility threshold where anyone *cares* about the difference between a free skills score and a paid durability rating.

The assumption that "price signals credibility" is true only if the market has already agreed you are the arbiter of credibility. In 2027, the market is choosing *who* to trust — and free is a powerful trust-builder when the alternative is an unknown startup asking for $49K.

## 3. Early Warning Signs (What Should Have Been Caught)

These are the signals that were visible *before* the competitor launched, any one of which should have triggered a strategy review. They are drawn directly from the existing competitive intelligence document and business model — no hindsight required.

### WARNING SIGN 1: Lightcast's 5 acquisitions in 2025

**Source:** Competitive intelligence §1 (line 21): *"KKR-backed; made five acquisitions in 2025 alone — active consolidation strategy."*

**What it meant:** A company acquiring five businesses in one year is in *roll-up mode*. Roll-ups don't wait for markets to develop organically — they enter, acquire, or suppress. The fastest way to suppress a nascent competitor is to make the market uneconomic before the competitor reaches scale. Australia is the textbook target: one domestic entrant, no other competitors, small enough to drown with a rounding-error budget.

**What should have happened:** The moment this was documented, the strategy should have included a "Lightcast enters Australia within 12 months" scenario with detailed countermeasures: a pre-built free tier, a government/regulatory partnership that creates a structural barrier (e.g., TEQSA endorsement), or an acquisition conversation with Lightcast itself.

### WARNING SIGN 2: Lightcast's existing Australian presence

**Source:** Competitive intelligence §1 (line 20): *"Geographic availability: US, Canada, UK only as of June 2025. No Australia/APAC launch announced."* AND Competitive intelligence §4 (line 159): *"University of Melbourne has used Lightcast's Labour Insight since 2017."*

**What it meant:** Lightcast wasn't "entering" Australia in the sense of establishing a beachhead. It was already *in* Australia — with paying university customers, data infrastructure, and procurement relationships. The question was not "will they enter?" but "when will they add Skillabi to their existing Australian product portfolio?" The "no APAC launch announced" language in the competitive intelligence document framed this as reassuring when it was actually the opposite: an incumbent with existing distribution that hasn't yet pulled the trigger on the one SKU that competes directly.

**What should have happened:** The "no Australia/APAC launch" line should have been treated as a countdown, not a comfort. The strategy should have assumed a Skillabi Australia launch in 2026–2027 and built the business model around *competing with a free/cheap incumbent*, not around *having the market to yourself*.

### WARNING SIGN 3: The pricing model assumes customers with budget flexibility that doesn't exist

**Source:** Market sizing §2 (line 29): *"profit margins have fallen to 5.3% (down 9.6 percentage points over five years), and institutions are actively eliminating courses and restructuring workforce at scale."* AND Business model §9 (line 253): *"Slow university procurement — 9–18 month sales cycles — MEDIUM risk."*

**What it meant:** The business model simultaneously documents that Australian universities are under extreme financial pressure AND assumes they will pay $19K–$49K/year for a new product category with no established procurement line. These two facts are in tension. When a competitor offers the same product category for free, the already-tenuous willingness-to-pay collapses instantly — not gradually, not negotiably, instantly.

**What should have happened:** The pricing model should have stress-tested against a competitor offering free or near-free (e.g., $4,900 unlimited, matching the single-program price). The Year 1–3 revenue projections should have included a "competitor free entry in Year 2" downside scenario with a survival plan.

### WARNING SIGN 4: The moat depends on time, and time is not guaranteed

**Source:** Business model §1 (line 23): *"The benchmark dataset — path-dependent, first-mover, cannot be retroactively manufactured."* AND Business model §9 (line 256): *"Competitor entry — MEDIUM — 18–36 month first-mover window; ANZ-regulatory nativeness as barrier; build the benchmark dataset (path-dependent moat) before they arrive."*

**What it meant:** The entire moat strategy is a race: build the dataset before competitors enter. But the race has a defined finish line (18–36 months) and a competitor who doesn't need to respect it. If Lightcast enters at Month 12 instead of Month 36, the dataset covers 87 programs at 11 institutions — valuable but not sufficient to create switching costs or network effects. The "path-dependent, cannot be retroactively manufactured" property only becomes a moat at scale (hundreds of programs, dozens of institutions, multi-year trends). At 87 programs, it's a research dataset, not a competitive barrier.

**What should have happened:** The strategy should have included a Plan B for "competitor enters before the dataset reaches critical mass" — a regulatory partnership (TEQSA endorsement), a government procurement mandate, or a minimum-viable-moat threshold (e.g., "200+ programs across 25+ institutions") with a plan to reach it faster or survive if you can't.

### WARNING SIGN 5: The independence argument assumes customers care enough to pay for it

**Source:** Brand strategy §6 (line 137): *"COMPASS's entire moat is independence, yet it is incubated inside one university."* AND Business model §9 (line 253): *"Independence perception — HIGH — universities won't pay a UoM-incubated rater to rate them."*

**What it meant:** The business model identifies independence as both the primary moat AND the primary risk. But the independence argument only matters if the competitor *doesn't offer a credible alternative*. When Lightcast (a third party, not incubated inside any Australian university) offers its product for free, the independence comparison flips: Lightcast looks *more* independent than a UoM spin-out AND costs nothing. The independence premium — the very thing Evidura was charging for — evaporates.

**What should have happened:** The independence question should have been resolved *before* any competitor could enter, not in Year 2. A TEQSA partnership, a Universities Australia endorsement, or independent governance with a published charter — established before the first paid subscription — would have created a structural independence signal that a free competitor couldn't replicate by writing a cheque.

### WARNING SIGN 6: The accreditation-body revenue assumption is untested

**Source:** Business model §3.2 (line 64): *"AACSB/AMC/Engineers Australia embed the Durability Rating as an evidence input... $40K–$120K/yr per body."* AND Business model §10 (line 270): *"Begin AACSB/AMC conversations — Q4 2026."*

**What it meant:** The single largest secondary revenue stream — accreditation body partnerships — is an assumption with no validation. No conversation had occurred at the time the business model was written. When Lightcast proposes the same thing for free, the untested assumption becomes an impossible one.

**What should have happened:** The accreditation-body revenue line should have been treated as *speculative*, not included in the Year 2–3 base case. The business model should have shown a survivable P&L from institutional subscriptions *alone*, with accreditation revenue as upside. At projected institutional revenue of $650K in Year 3 against $1.1M in people costs, the model was never survivable on subscriptions alone.

## 4. The Structural Vulnerability

The failure story reveals a structural vulnerability that no amount of sales execution could fix:

> **Evidura's moat requires time to build, but the competitor's moat (capital) is already built.**

KKR's capital moat doesn't degrade over time — it *compounds*. Lightcast can wait. It can enter late, give away the product, lose money for years, and still win, because the win condition for Lightcast is not "make money in Australia" — it's "prevent anyone else from building a defensible position." The cost of doing so ($5M over three years) is trivial against KKR's $500B+ portfolio.

Evidura's time-based moat (the benchmark dataset) is the opposite: it must be built *before* the competitor arrives, and if it's not sufficiently large at the moment of arrival, it's not a moat at all — it's a starter dataset the competitor can replicate or acquire in the wind-down.

This asymmetry — capital moat vs. time moat — is the fundamental reason the 18–36 month assumption was not just optimistic but structurally wrong. Even if Evidura had the full 36 months, Lightcast could enter at Month 37 with the same free-product strategy. The window was never a window — it was a grace period from a competitor who hadn't yet decided to care.

## 5. What a Survivability Plan Would Have Required

For completeness, the conditions under which Evidura could have survived a free-competitor entry:

| Requirement | What it means | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory moat before competitor entry** | TEQSA or UA formally endorses the Durability Rating as a sector standard — making it non-optional regardless of price | Low — regulatory partnerships take years, not months |
| **Government procurement mandate** | Department of Education requires durability ratings as a condition of CSP funding, similar to Texas HB 8 outcomes-based funding | Very low — Australian government does not move this fast on curriculum policy |
| **Pre-built free tier with premium upsell** | Evidura offers a free public rating tier before Lightcast enters, capturing the market's default position | Medium — requires surrendering the "ratings must be paid" philosophy, but is technically achievable |
| **Acquisition by Lightcast/Coursedog** | Sell Evidura to the competitor before they enter, turning the threat into an exit | Medium-low — requires the competitor to value the dataset and methodology enough to buy rather than build |
| **Survive on non-institutional revenue only** | Accreditation, government, international — no dependence on AU institutional subscriptions | Low — none of these revenue streams were validated at the time of writing |
