## 👀 Vision

You cannot predict the future, but you can predict how the mental models holding a system in place will change — and from that, infer where the system is directionally headed. The vision COMPASS is built toward is:

> **"Within seven years, no university will approve, renew, or market a degree without a continuously updated, evidence-based measure of its economic durability in an AI-augmented labour market — and students, employers, and regulators will expect to see that signal before they commit."**

This is **clear** (a specific, observable end state), **strong** (it challenges the prevailing beliefs that currently insulate curriculum from market reality), and **feasible** (the analytical capability already exists; what is missing is a trusted, comparable signal and the norm of demanding one). The time frame — **seven years, to ~2033** — is chosen deliberately: it is one full long-cycle of curriculum governance, the span over which a new mechanism can move from novel to expected.

### How this vision challenges the prevailing mental models

A good vision is not a forecast of products; it is a forecast of belief change. Mapped against the three mental models documented in the [systems-thinking analysis](compass-systems-thinking.md):

| Prevailing mental model (today) | The belief the vision asserts will replace it |
|---|---|
| Accreditation compliance equals quality | Compliance is a floor, not a signal of durability; a degree can be fully accredited and economically obsolete |
| Academic rigour is the primary measure of a program's value | Rigour and labour-market durability are *distinct* axes, and both must be measured — rigorous-but-irrelevant is a failure mode |
| Employer dissatisfaction is a lagging indicator confirming what we already know | Market signal is a *leading* indicator that should reshape curriculum *at the point of design*, not validate it after graduation |

If these three beliefs shift even partially, the system reorganises around a new question — *"will this degree still be economically valuable?"* — and that question requires an instrument to answer it. COMPASS is that instrument.

### The genuine founder's call: framing and horizon

The statement above is the recommended vision. Two decisions inside it are yours to set, and worth holding consciously:

- **Concrete vs. directional framing.** The version above is *concrete and time-bound* (mirroring the food-packaging benchmark: *"…100% non-toxic and recyclable within 10 years"*). A *directional* alternative, in the Tesla mould (*"accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy"*), would read: **"Make the economic durability of every degree continuously visible — to the institutions that design them, the regulators that approve them, and the students who choose them."** Directional travels better in a pitch; concrete is sharper for an internal compass. Recommend leading with concrete, using directional as the one-line.
- **Time horizon.** *Seven years (~2033)* is the recommendation — long enough to be visionary, short enough to be credible against a sector that reviews on 5–7-year cycles. **Five years (~2031)** is bolder and riskier (it implies regulatory or peak-body adoption, not just early-mover institutions). **Ten years (~2036)** is the "deep-tech/medtech" horizon the framework permits and is the safest, but risks reading as someone-else's-problem. The horizon is not cosmetic: it sets how aggressively the roadmap must pursue regulators and peak bodies versus individual institutions.

## 🧠 Mental Models

The [systems-thinking analysis](compass-systems-thinking.md) surfaced three prevailing **mental models** — the unexamined beliefs at the base of the iceberg that hold the current curriculum system in place. The two that most directly interest us, because they are the ones COMPASS's mechanism can actually move, are:

- **That accreditation compliance equals quality.** A program that satisfies TEQSA's Higher Education Standards Framework and its professional accreditor is treated as "fine." Compliance is necessary, but it is silent on whether the capabilities a degree builds remain economically relevant. A fully compliant degree can be quietly obsolescing.
- **That employer dissatisfaction is a lagging indicator** — something that *confirms* what committees already suspect, rather than a *leading signal* that should reshape curriculum at the point of design. (96% of employers say colleges fall short on workforce preparation; only 30% of 2025 graduates secured full-time roles in their field, down from 41% in 2024 — yet this is read as background noise, not as a design input.)

A third model — *that academic rigour is the primary measure of a program's value, independent of whether the capabilities it builds remain economically relevant* — sits underneath both and is the slowest to shift.

These beliefs were serviceable in a stable environment. In an environment where AI is restructuring job families on 12–18-month cycles, they are structurally dangerous: they license the system to keep doing what it has always done while the ground moves.

**How COMPASS changes them.** We cannot reach the base of the iceberg directly — mental models are the hardest level to shift. What we *can* do is make structural misalignment so visible, so fast, and so evidence-based that the beliefs sustaining it become harder to hold. By putting a trusted, comparable durability signal in the hands of the people making decisions — curriculum designers at the point of design, regulators at the point of approval, students at the point of enrolment — COMPASS lets each of them act on a *leading* signal and, in effect, "vote" with their decisions. That makes proactive curriculum durability a competitive and compliance necessity for institutions, rather than a reputational problem they discover at graduation. The mechanism is the same one the food-packaging team identified: change the system not by arguing with the belief, but by making the hidden information visible at the moment of choice.

## 🏗️ System Structure

The mental models above enable a **systemic tension** we keep returning to:

> Universities, students, employers, and regulators increasingly care whether a degree will hold its economic value in an AI-augmented labour market — but **none of them has a fast, evidence-based, repeatable way of knowing, at the point that matters**: design, approval, renewal, or enrolment.

This is the curriculum-sector analogue of the packaging team's insight that *consumers fear BPA and PFAS but have no way to know, at the point of purchase, whether a given item is harmful.* The fear (or, here, the institutional anxiety) is real and widespread; the information needed to act on it is absent at the decisive moment.

The structure producing this tension is a **broken feedback loop**. The loop that should connect labour-market change to curriculum design runs through graduate-outcomes data — Australia's Graduate Outcomes Survey samples graduates 4–6 months out, with longitudinal data at three years — and then through committee cycles that act only once a pattern is undeniable. Curriculum governance runs on **18–84-month cycles**; the market it serves now changes on **12–18-month cycles** — a structural mismatch of roughly **5–10×**. By the time a signal clears the loop, it is five to seven years old. Misalignment is no longer an exception in this system; it is the system's equilibrium. Universities are not passive — but their responses (surveillance-based exams, bolt-on AI-literacy modules) operate at the *event* level and leave the structure untouched.

**Why a "scoring / intelligence" venture, and not a material or a brand.** Just as the packaging team judged that *creating a new material* was beyond their expertise and *creating a new packaging brand* meant competing on scale in a brutal market — and so chose to be a **certifier** — we have judged the equivalent for COMPASS:

- **Not a Curriculum Management System (CMS).** Building the system-of-record for curriculum workflow is a crowded, switching-cost-heavy fight against entrenched incumbents (Coursedog, CourseLeaf, Modern Campus). It also misses the point: the gap is not workflow, it is *signal*.
- **Not a labour-market data vendor.** Becoming a Lightcast-class data provider is a capital- and data-moat-heavy game, and the raw signal is already commoditising.
- **The wedge is the assessment and intelligence *layer*** that sits on top of public curriculum data and live market signal and turns them into a trusted, comparable durability score and redesign roadmap — the layer no incumbent currently owns. This is the strategically defensible, expertise-aligned choice (service design + CX + AI, applied in HE), and it is the layer from which the broader vision can be reached.

## 💡 Opportunity

We frame the opportunity as:

> *Universities, students, employers, and regulators lack a trusted, evidence-based, independent measure of whether a degree program will retain its economic value in an AI-augmented labour market. This absence makes curriculum review anecdotal and brand-driven, leaves students committing $40,000–$200,000+ on backward-looking signals, forces employers to absorb the cost of underprepared graduates, and lets misalignment compound undetected until graduation. A trusted, comparable durability signal — available at the point of design, approval, and enrolment — would close the broken feedback loop faster than the market moves, and turn curriculum relevance from a lagging reputational risk into a leading, managed one.*

This opportunity is large and live: the curriculum-management software peer category alone is **$2.1–3.0B globally (2024), growing 12.5–15.5%**, sitting within a **~$132.8B (2025)** higher-education EdTech segment — and Australia is an unusually favourable launch market, with 41 universities under acute structural pressure (sector profit margins down to 5.3%, courses being cut at scale) and a regulator already naming AI as a curriculum-integrity problem. The durability-signal layer COMPASS targets has **no dominant global incumbent**.

### Precedents that give the thesis legs

The vision rests on a repeatable pattern: an **independent, comparable signal made visible at the point of decision** reshapes a whole market's behaviour without the signal-provider ever making the underlying product. Precedents:

- **Health Star Rating** — an on-pack, comparable, government-backed nutrition signal that shifted both consumer choice and manufacturer reformulation. *(The packaging team's own precedent — and a near-exact structural match.)*
- **B Corp certification** — an independent standard that changed corporate behaviour by making "good" legible and marketable.
- **Credit ratings (Moody's / S&P) and Morningstar** — independent *durability* ratings that markets came to demand before committing capital; the closest analogue to rating a degree's economic durability.
- **AACSB / EQUIS accreditation** — proof that institutions will actively *pursue and market* an external quality signal when it carries reputational and enrolment weight.
- **QILT / Graduate Outcomes Survey** — proof the sector already collects and publishes outcome signals; COMPASS's move is to make that signal *forward-looking and available at design time* rather than retrospective.

Each shows the same mechanism the vision depends on: once a trusted comparable signal exists and the people making decisions start expecting it, the producers reorganise to score well on it — proactively, to avoid losing ground to those who already do.

## Where this leaves us (the loop continues)

This vision is a defensible bet, not a finished answer. The highest-value next move is to take it into **conversations with industry experts** — curriculum deans, TEQSA/peak-body figures, institutional-research leads, and labour-market analysts — and pressure-test the two load-bearing assumptions:

1. **Demand for the signal.** Do decision-makers actually *want* a durability score they can be held to — or will the mental models resist it harder than the evidence suggests?
2. **Trust and standing.** Can an independent layer earn enough authority for its signal to be *expected* — the way the precedents above earned theirs?

If those hold, the seven-year vision is the compass heading. If they don't, we loop back — to a different stakeholder, a different framing of the opportunity, or a different aspect of this same market — carrying everything we have learned.
