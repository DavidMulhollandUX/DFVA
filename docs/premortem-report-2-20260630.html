Second Premortem — Revised Model

We Fixed the Business Model.
Here's How It Still Dies.

6 new failure scenarios investigated against the confidential benchmarking pivot
June 30, 2026 · 6-month horizon · December 2026 failure date

What changed since the first premortem: Pivoted from public rating agency → confidential benchmarking. Staged independence (12–24 months). P&L re-baselined to $1.5M Year 5. Accreditation-body revenue killed. Reliability study commissioned. Confidentiality guarantee added everywhere. Question: did we fix the right problems, or just change which problems kill us?

Most Likely Failure

UoM Commercialisation blocks the spin-out. The staged independence plan assumed UoM would accept 10–20% equity with no board seat — but Commercialisation's standard term sheet demands 30–50% equity, board representation, and first-refusal rights. The IP was developed on university time with university resources. Commercialisation is a policy-driven administrative unit, not a venture investor. When the founder pushes back citing independence requirements, Commercialisation sees it as a beneficiary asking for a special deal. The negotiation stalls. The 12–24 month timeline stretches to 36–48. Go8 design partners withdraw. Lightcast enters Australia free. The window closes. This is the most likely because it's outside the founder's control, the incentives are structurally misaligned, and there's no executive sponsor with authority to overrule Commercialisation.

Most Dangerous Failure

The reliability study produces κ <0.6, and the methodology cannot be sold. The study you commissioned to validate the methodology ends up invalidating it. Three human raters disagree substantially with the LLM and with each other. The results are published — because you committed to transparency. Now every sales conversation includes "I read your reliability study — your own raters couldn't agree." The study that was supposed to be your #1 defense against the original premortem's most dangerous failure mode becomes prosecution exhibit A. The target κ >0.8 is aspirational to the point of fantasy for a novel 11-dimension rubric — educational assessment literature consistently finds κ of 0.3–0.5 for expert judgment of complex constructs. You commissioned a public validation of an internally-unvalidated product with a success threshold that may be mathematically unreachable.

The Revised Revised Plan — What Must Change

1. Pre-negotiate with UoM Commercialisation before incorporating anything. Do not incorporate Evidura Pty Ltd until you have a term sheet. The independence structure document assumed Commercialisation would play ball — verify this before spending money on legal entities. If they demand 30%+ and a board seat, the staged spin-out model is dead. Pivot to Option C (academic research only) or find an executive sponsor (Provost-level) who can override standard terms. This is the #1 blocking risk. Do it this week.

2. Run the reliability study privately before committing to publish. The current protocol commits to publication regardless of outcome. Change it: run the study. If κ >0.7, publish with limitations. If κ <0.6, revise the methodology and re-test. Do not commit to publishing a bad result — that's scientific transparency applied to a commercial context where it becomes a suicide pact. Add a publication off-ramp: "results will be published after methodology refinement if κ is below threshold."

3. Reconsider the clean break vs. staged transition. Agent 2 found that staged independence may be worse than either extreme. The pipeline freezes during the transition because no procurement officer will contract with an entity whose CEO still works at a competitor. Either: (a) stay internal until you're ready for a clean break, or (b) break clean now and raise the seed funding to bridge. The middle path — "kinda independent, kinda not" — satisfies no one.

4. Build the confidentiality breach scenario into the product & contracts. Don't just promise confidentiality — design for its breach. Add: a breach-response protocol in every client contract, a "what happens if your results leak" FAQ on the website, contractual liquidated damages for Evidura-caused leaks, and anonymous benchmarking that makes it impossible to identify any single institution's data. Make the product breach-resistant, not just breach-promising.

5. Model the $1.5M ceiling honestly — it's a job, not a company. At $1.5M ARR with 60% gross margin, you have ~$900K to cover salary, ops, and everything else. That's you + 2–3 contractors. Accept this. Do not plan for a CTO hire or a sales team. The model works as a consultancy with a SaaS delivery mechanism. It doesn't work as a company. Either raise capital to fund the team, or accept the solo-practice ceiling.

6. Test whether confidentiality actually solves demand resistance. The timeout on Agent 1 is a signal — this question is genuinely hard. Before committing to the confidential benchmarking model, interview 3 DVCs and ask: "If you received a confidential assessment showing one of your flagship programs at HIGH RISK, would you commission more assessments or suppress the finding?" The answer determines whether confidentiality solves the demand problem or just changes the venue.

The 6 New Ways It Dies — Agent Investigations

Critical

1. UoM Commercialisation Blocks the Spin-Out

July 2026: first meeting with Commercialisation. They present standard terms: 30-50% equity, board seat, first-refusal rights, non-compete, royalty. Founder counters with 14-page proposal: 10% equity, no board seat, no first-refusal — citing independence requirements. Eight weeks of silence. Then: rejection. "Significant departure from established policy." Revised offer: 25% equity, board seat maintained. Still above viability threshold. 18 months of negotiation produces a poison-pill deal: 20% equity, observer seat that auto-converts to voting director, first-refusal with a $2M carve-out too small for any real raise. VC funding blocked. UK partnership blocked. Go8 partners gone. December 2029: wound down. Total revenue: $38,000.
Underlying Assumption
UoM Commercialisation will behave like a venture investor, optimising for the venture's success. Reality: they're a policy-driven administrative unit optimising for asset protection and policy compliance.
Critical

2. Reliability Study Produces κ <0.6

The 6-week study runs. Results: κ = 0.52 overall. LLM-human agreement is 0.41. Dimension-level κ ranges from 0.31 (D4: Decision-Making Under Uncertainty) to 0.68 (D6: Domain Depth). The study is published — because transparency was promised. Within weeks, a prospect quotes the study in a procurement meeting: "Your own raters couldn't agree on 9 of 11 dimensions." The study designed as the #1 defense against methodology challenges becomes the most effective weapon against Evidura's credibility. The LLM-scoring-challenge failure mode from the original premortem — the most dangerous one — was not prevented. It was accelerated.
Underlying Assumption
The reliability study will produce κ >0.8, validating the methodology. Educational assessment literature consistently finds expert judgment of complex constructs produces κ of 0.3–0.5. The target was aspirational to the point of fantasy.
High

3. Staged Independence Kills the Pipeline

A DVC at a prospective institution asks: "Who exactly am I contracting with?" The answer — "Evidura Pty Ltd, an independent entity. The founder is transitioning out of UoM over 12-24 months and currently holds a 0.4 FTE position there" — satisfies no one. In a relationship-driven market of 41 universities where every DVC knows every other DVC, "mostly independent" reads as "not independent." The pipeline doesn't slow — it freezes. Every conversation ends with "let's revisit when the transition is complete." In university procurement, "come back later" means 24–36 months. The staged approach — designed to de-risk the transition — concentrated risk on the one entity that couldn't bear it: the venture itself.
Underlying Assumption
A staged transition de-risks spin-out via governance firewalls. Reality: governance firewalls solve legal problems but not trust problems. Independence is binary in relationship-driven markets.
High

4. Confidentiality Guarantee Breached

A program director at a paying institution shares her confidential HIGH RISK assessment with a trusted colleague at another university. That colleague mentions it in passing at a Universities Australia forum. Within weeks, multiple institutions "independently" raise concerns about the same program — concerns that align precisely with the confidential assessment's findings. Nobody can prove it was a leak vs. coincidence. It doesn't matter. The next institution considering Evidura asks: "How do we know our results won't leak too?" The confidentiality moat — the product's entire trust signal — becomes a liability. The rumour is structurally unprovable. The ambiguity is fatal regardless of Evidura's actual culpability.
Underlying Assumption
Confidentiality is a deliverable that can be guaranteed by organisational policy and contractual obligation. Reality: a judgment in someone's head cannot be regulated. In a market of 41 universities, the social graph guarantees information flow.
High

5. $1.5M Ceiling Cannot Attract a Team

Year 3: CTO candidates do the equity maths. At a 5x multiple, 3% equity = ~$225K over 4 years — less than the annual comp gap against a senior IC role at Atlassian or Canva. Total comp of $200–220K with upside doesn't beat $180K base + RSUs + super at a real company. Year 3.5: the one sales hire leaves within a week of a recruiter offering $150K base / $250K OTE at a Series A. Year 4: scoring analysts leave for university jobs with 17% super and defined-benefit pensions. Year 5: the founder is working harder than any job they ever had — product, sales, support, admin. "The company" is really them plus five contractors. The lifestyle business never materialised. It's just a job.
Underlying Assumption
A profitable $1.5M ARR SaaS business can attract and retain the talent needed to build a real company. Reality: the talent you need has lifestyle expectations $1.5M can't fund.
High

6. Confidentiality Doesn't Solve Demand Resistance

The premortem assumed universities resist public ratings. The revised model assumes they'll accept confidential assessments. But a CRITICAL finding is still a CRITICAL finding. It still lands on the Dean's desk. It still goes into committee papers. It's still potentially discoverable under FOI. The demand problem may not have been the publication channel — it may have been the verdict itself. A university that commissions an assessment and receives bad news has two choices: act on it (politically costly) or suppress it (the report goes in a drawer). Neither outcome leads to renewal. The confidentiality pivot changed the wrapper, not the contents.
Underlying Assumption
The demand resistance was about publicity, not about the verdict itself. Reality: a CRITICAL finding is institutionally dangerous whether it's public or private. The problem may be the score, not who sees it.

Verdict: Did the Revised Model Fix the Right Problems?

Partially. The original premortem's #1 most likely failure — independence perception + procurement velocity — is not fixed by the staged spin-out. It may be worse because the transition period creates ambiguity that freezes the pipeline. The #1 most dangerous failure — methodology credibility — is not fixed by the reliability study; it may be accelerated if the study produces a bad result and publication was pre-committed. The two genuinely fixed problems are: (a) the public-rating demand resistance is now a confidential-assessment demand resistance — better but not solved; and (b) the self-funded-international-expansion fantasy is now acknowledged as impossible.

The model is more survivable than the original — but it's still fragile at three points: UoM Commercialisation (outside founder control), reliability study outcome (unknown until tested), and the confidentiality guarantee (one leak away from collapse). These are the gates. If all three hold, the model works — as a $1.5M consultancy, not a $10M company. If any one breaks, the venture dies.
