---

> **The discipline of this step.** Everything to here has been desk research and our own reasoning — and the risk now is confirmation bias. We have spent weeks on COMPASS; it is human nature to go looking for evidence that we were right. Step 5 inverts that instinct. The job is to **aim to be proven wrong**: to take the venture to people who know the system from the inside and ask them to break it. The hypothesis that survives expert attempts to kill it is the one worth building. The hypotheses that don't survive have saved us years. This document is the operating manual for that gauntlet — what we're testing, how strong the signals already look, who to speak to, what to send them, and what to ask.

## 🎯 The hypotheses we are testing

Validation is only meaningful against claims that can be **falsified**. The Vision narrowed COMPASS to a single primary opportunity and two load-bearing assumptions. We restate them here as testable claims, each with the evidence that *would change our minds*.

**Primary opportunity hypothesis (H0).**
> *Universities, students, employers and regulators lack a trusted, independent, evidence-based measure of whether a degree will retain its economic value in an AI-augmented labour market — and a comparable "durability signal", available at the point of design, approval and enrolment, is valuable enough that institutions will adopt and pay for it.*

This decomposes into four falsifiable sub-hypotheses. The first two are the **load-bearing** ones the Vision flagged; the second two are the commercial and structural conditions that must also hold.

| # | Hypothesis | We are **wrong** if… | Tested by FQ* | Signal it loads |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **H1 — Demand** *(load-bearing)* | Decision-makers genuinely *want* a durability signal they can be held to — not just a board-table talking point | Experts say the appetite is rhetorical: the mental models (accreditation = quality; rigour ≠ relevance) resist a hard, external score harder than the evidence suggests; "we'd never let an outside number grade our degrees" | 1, 8 | Defined customers; Systemic disruption |
| **H2 — Trust & standing** *(load-bearing)* | An *independent* layer can earn enough authority for its signal to become *expected* — the way Health Star, B Corp, Moody's, AACSB did | Experts say only a regulator or peak body can confer that standing, and a startup score would be ignored or actively distrusted | 7 | Systemic disruption; Entry & scale |
| **H3 — Buyer & willingness-to-pay** | A DVC (Education/Academic) holds a recognised budget line and the delegated authority (typ. A$100–250k) to buy curriculum intelligence *distinct from* existing careers/IR LMI subscriptions | There is no budget line, no clear single buyer, and the sale requires inventing a new category in every institution from scratch | 3, 4 | Defined customers; Tailwinds |
| **H4 — Unoccupied adjacency** | The Stage-3→Stage-4 gap (LMI data never reaches the curriculum decision) is genuinely *unoccupied* — TechnologyOne/CourseLoop has no LMI roadmap closing it | TechnologyOne has internally scoped or contracted LMI integration into CourseLoop's 2026 roadmap — in which case the incumbent closes the gap and the wedge disappears | 2 | Overlooked; Entry & scale |

\* *FQ = the eight priority falsifiable questions in [research-plan.md](research-plan.md#priority-falsifiable-questions-for-primary-research). Every interview should be designed to confirm or refute at least one.*

**The convergence test.** COMPASS survives Step 5 if H1 and H2 hold under expert pressure *and* at least one of H3/H4 holds (a buyer exists, or the incumbent leaves the gap open long enough to establish standing). If H1 or H2 fails, the Vision's central bet fails and we loop back — to a different stakeholder, framing, or aspect of this same market.

## 📊 Market Signals Scorecard

The MEC framework offers eight market signals as criteria. They need not *all* be true — but if any is *clearly false*, that is a sign the opportunity isn't there. Below is the honest read on COMPASS from the research to date. The final column — **"the hole to ask experts to poke"** — is the single most useful output of this exercise: it converts each signal into an interview probe.

|---|---|:--:|---|---|

"Which of these kills it first?" |
| **Systemic disruption potential** | This is the Vision's core bet | 🟢 | The mechanism — a trusted comparable signal at the point of decision reorganises a whole market — has 5 precedents (Health Star, B Corp, Moody's/Morningstar, AACSB/EQUIS, QILT) | "Are the mental models (accreditation = quality) simply too entrenched to move — and can a *new* signal ever become *expected*?" |

Which is the faster path, and is either open to us?" |

**What the scorecard says.** Four signals are **green** (new/emerging, solvable, overlooked, systemic-disruption); four are **amber** (sizeable, defined-customers, tailwinds-vs-headwinds, entry-&-scale); **none is red.** That clears the MEC bar — *"ideally none are clearly false."* Crucially, the two ambers that matter most map **exactly** onto the Vision's two load-bearing assumptions:

- **Defined-customers + tailwinds** ⇒ **H1 (demand)** and **H3 (willingness-to-pay)** — *will they actually want it and pay for it?*
- **Systemic-disruption + entry-&-scale** ⇒ **H2 (trust & standing)** — *can an independent signal earn the authority to be expected?*

The scorecard and the hypotheses agree on where the risk concentrates. The interview programme below is built to attack precisely those four amber cells.

## 🧭 Who to speak to — building the 20+ list

**Strategy: warm-first, and use the unfair advantage.** The MEC guidance is unambiguous — *your own networks are the best channel*, and *start with people you know* to practise in a lower-stakes setting. COMPASS has two advantages most teams don't:

1. **You are inside the buyer institution.** As Associate Director, Service Experience & Design at the University of Melbourne, you have credible internal standing and warm pathways to T&L, careers, IR, and the HE-research centres — without a single cold email.
2. **Council has already raised the questions.** Per [council-alignment.md](compass-council-alignment.md), University Council has independently articulated COMPASS's Q2 and Q3. Council members *are* the DVC-level stakeholders the research plan set out to interview — the warmest possible introduction to the most important "yes" in the model.

Work outward in three waves. Aim to *line up* 20+ so that the ~30–50% who say yes still gives you a dozen conversations.

## 🎤 Interview guides (Learn → Challenge → Probe)

Write questions in advance; **don't follow them religiously.** Open the conversation by telling them, explicitly, that *you want them to poke holes* — you're trying to be proven wrong, not flattered. Listen far more than you talk. Ask "why" and "how." Embrace silence. Ask for concrete examples. Two people per interview where possible (one asks, one notes).

### Universal must-asks (every interview)

- **Open:** "I want to be upfront — I'm not here to sell anything. I'm trying to find the reasons this *shouldn't* exist. Treat it as a deliberate attempt to talk me out of it."
- **Close 1:** "**What didn't I ask that I should have?**"
- **Close 2:** "**Who else should I be speaking to?**" *(This is what makes outreach self-driving — every yes should yield 1–2 more names.)*
- **Close 3:** "If you were betting, would you bet *against* us? Why?"

### Guide A — DVC (Education/Academic) / Director of T&L *(the buyer — tests H1, H3)*

- **Learn:** Walk me through a curriculum review here — what triggers it, who's in the room, what information gets consulted? · What's actually changing in how you think about program relevance right now?
- **Challenge:** When a program is reviewed, does *current labour-market data* ever reach the table — and if not, why not? · The mental model that "accredited = fine" — is that fair, or would a hard external durability score be unwelcome? · Would you let an *independent* number grade your degrees' economic durability? What would make that intolerable?
- **Probe:** Is there a *budget line* for curriculum intelligence distinct from what careers/IR already spend on Lightcast? Who signs? · What evidence would convince you this is worth solving? · What does TEQSA's 2026 shift mean *practically* for your team — urgency, or compliance theatre?

### Guide B — Program Director / academic who's been through a review *(the user — tests H1, FQ1/FQ8)*

- **Learn:** What triggered the review you were in, and what did the process feel like day-to-day? · What information did you consult — and what did you *wish* you'd had?
- **Challenge:** Was there *any* external signal about graduate employment outcomes in the room, or was it internal (peer benchmarking, accreditation)? · If someone had handed you commercial skills-gap data, would you have trusted it, reframed it, or rejected it — and what decides that?
- **Probe:** Who actually had authority to change the program, and what did they need to be convinced by? · Where was the friction that a tool could never fix?

### Guide C — IR/IE & Careers/Employability *(the data holders — tests H4, FQ6)*

- **Learn:** What labour-market data does the institution subscribe to, and who uses it for what? · Has a curriculum team *ever* come to you for labour-market evidence?
- **Challenge:** Why doesn't that data cross into curriculum decisions today — is it organisational, technical, cultural, or just nobody's job? · If you gave a curriculum team a skills-gap report tomorrow, what would actually happen to it?
- **Probe:** What would have to be true for LMI to become a *routine* input to curriculum review? · What's the most useful thing you currently *can't* tell curriculum teams?

### Guide D — CMS / LMI vendor *(the gatekeeper & adjacency — tests H4, FQ2)*

- **Learn:** How does a typical Australian university engagement start — who reaches out, from which department? · What's the most common objection you hear from AU HE buyers?
- **Challenge:** Is there a product *specifically* aimed at curriculum-design decisions, or is that use case marginal? · *(TechnologyOne)* Is LMI integration into CourseLoop on the 2026 roadmap — and if not, what's the stated position on it as a feature? · Why hasn't anyone closed the LMI-to-curriculum gap already?
- **Probe:** If a third party built the durability layer on top of your platform, would that be a partner or a threat? · What would make you build it yourselves?

### Guide E — Regulator-adjacent / peak body / accreditation *(standing — tests H2, FQ5/FQ7)*

- **Learn:** How do you see the line between assessment integrity (TEQSA's focus) and curriculum-market durability (nobody's)? · What's changing in what accreditation panels expect on emerging-technology agility?
- **Challenge:** Could an *independent* durability signal ever carry enough authority to be *expected* — or does that standing only come from a regulator or peak body? · *(NTEU)* Where's the line between a decision-*support* tool you'd accept and a decision-*replacement* tool you'd dispute?
- **Probe:** In recent AACSB CIR / EA self-assessments, has quantitative labour-market alignment data been used as evidence — and did panels accept it? · What would it take for a body like yours to endorse, or co-own, a signal like this?

## 🗂️ Capturing insight (or the interview never happened)

Take notes live; write up **immediately** after — memory decays fast. Use one capture block per interview, stored alongside this workbook (or in [research-plan.md](research-plan.md)'s tracker). *If you don't record the insight, the interview may as well not have occurred.*

```
Interview — [Name, role, org]            Date: [   ]   Channel: [   ]   Warm/Cold: [   ]
Hypotheses tested: [H1 / H2 / H3 / H4]   FQs tested: [   ]

VALIDATED signals:        [what they said that strengthens a hypothesis/market signal]
INVALIDATED / challenged: [what they said that weakens one — the most valuable column]
Net effect on hypotheses: [H1 ↑/↓/—  H2 ↑/↓/—  H3 ↑/↓/—  H4 ↑/↓/—]
Surprises / new angles:   [tangents worth chasing]
"What didn't I ask?":     [their answer]
Referrals given:          [names → add to the 20+ list]
Scorecard cell to revisit:[which amber/green cell moved, and which way]
```

**The decision rule (from the MEC framework).** After ~4–5 interviews, reassess:

- **Confidence building** → one hypothesis survives every attempt to break it and the amber signals are turning green. *Action:* complete the MEC end-of-course form and book the MEC team to progress the venture.
- **Confidence collapsing across *all* hypotheses** → this is a *good* outcome, not a failure: it has saved years. *Action:* loop back — a new stakeholder, a new framing, or a different aspect of this same market — carrying everything learned.

Either way, the network you build here *is* career capital in the sector, regardless of whether COMPASS proceeds.
