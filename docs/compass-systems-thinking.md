# COMPASS: A Systems Thinking Perspective

**Why curriculum misalignment is a structural problem — and why reactive fixes make it worse**

---

## The Iceberg: What We See, and What We Don't

When a graduate enters the workforce underprepared for AI-augmented work, that is an *event* — the visible tip of an iceberg. Declining employment rates, employer dissatisfaction surveys, and skills gap reports are all events. They feel urgent. They trigger responses. But events are the least leveraged place to intervene. They are symptoms, not causes.

One level deeper lie the *patterns*: recurring trends observable across time. The pattern for curriculum is consistent and damning. Each generation of university programs lags three to five years behind the labour market. This was manageable when market change was gradual. It is not manageable now. AI has compressed the half-life of curriculum relevance to less than one degree cycle. A student enrolled today will graduate into a labour market that did not exist when their program was designed.

Deeper still is the *systemic structure* — the institutional wiring generating those patterns. Curriculum review cycles operate on five-to-seven-year rhythms. The feedback loop that should connect employer signals to curriculum reform runs through graduate outcomes data, which takes one to two years post-graduation to collect, then through committee processes that require the pattern to be undeniable before action is taken. No real-time mechanism connects market signals to curriculum design. The structure itself produces misalignment, reliably and repeatedly.

At the base lie the *mental models*: the unexamined beliefs holding the structure in place. Three are particularly powerful. First, that accreditation compliance equals quality. Second, that academic rigour is the primary measure of program value, independent of whether the capabilities it builds remain economically relevant. Third, that employer dissatisfaction is a lagging indicator that confirms what we already know, rather than a leading signal that should reshape what we do. In a stable environment, these beliefs are serviceable. In an environment where AI is restructuring job families in eighteen-month cycles, they are structurally dangerous.

Mental models are the highest-leverage level of the iceberg — the hardest to shift and the most consequential when they do shift. COMPASS cannot reach that level directly. What it can do is make structural misalignment so visible, so rapid, and so evidence-based that the mental models sustaining it become harder to hold.

---

## The Broken Feedback Loop

The natural balancing loop governing curriculum quality should work like this: strong curriculum produces capable graduates; capable graduates satisfy employers; employer satisfaction sustains enrolments and funding; funding enables investment; investment maintains curriculum strength. This loop seeks equilibrium between actual and desired state.

It has broken down under the weight of compounding delays. Physical delays mean two-to-three-year degrees take time to deliver graduates to market. Informational delays mean employment data takes one to two years post-graduation to collect. Perceptual delays — the most treacherous — mean that even when data exists, committees do not act until the pattern is undeniable. By that point, the signal is five to seven years old. AI has compressed the market-change cycle to twelve to eighteen months. Misalignment is no longer an exception; it is the system's equilibrium.

---

## Fixes That Backfire

Universities are not passive. They respond. But the responses tend to operate at the event level — addressing symptoms without touching the structure.

Retreating to surveillance-based examinations removes the assessment-integrity problem without examining whether what is being assessed remains relevant. Adding a standalone AI literacy module adjusts within the existing structure rather than redesigning it. These are "fixes that fail": interventions that reduce the visible symptom in the short term while leaving the underlying structure intact. Surveillance-heavy assessment environments actively erode the collaborative and metacognitive capabilities that AI-resilient graduates most need to develop.

---

## Working On the System

There is a meaningful distinction between working *in* a system and working *on* it. Responding to graduate complaints, adjusting individual subjects, and reacting to accreditation findings is working in the curriculum system. It is necessary, but it cannot resolve a structural problem.

COMPASS is a tool for working on the system. It addresses the delays that have broken the feedback loop. Where informational and perceptual delays have historically stretched the signal from market change to curriculum response across five to seven years, COMPASS compresses that signal to minutes. Any program, assessed against any handbook URL, receives a structured, evidence-based score across ten dimensions — from automation exposure to human-relational capability — along with a risk classification and a prioritised redesign roadmap.

This creates a faster corrective mechanism: a real-time evidence signal enables curriculum redesign; redesign shortens the misalignment lag; a shorter lag means graduates arrive better matched to the labour market that actually exists.

---

## Where COMPASS Intervenes on the Iceberg

COMPASS's three capabilities target the levels where change is achievable and consequential:

- **Viability Assessment** — scoring a program across ten dimensions and assigning a risk band — operates at the *systemic structures* level. It makes the broken wiring visible, moving institutional response from reactive to deliberate.
- **Market Intelligence** operates at the *patterns* level, surfacing real-time labour market signals by job family before they consolidate into a crisis.
- **Curriculum Redesign Roadmap** operates at the *systemic structures* level too: it names what needs to change and sequences how. Together, the Assessment and the Roadmap bracket the problem — one diagnosing the structure, the other redesigning it.

And over time, as structural evidence accumulates and programs demonstrably improve, the mental models that have insulated the system from change become harder to sustain. That is the slower, higher-leverage work — and it begins with making the invisible visible.

---

For university leaders, the question is not whether your programs are accredited. It is whether your institution is designed to detect and respond to structural misalignment before it compounds. COMPASS provides what the current system cannot: a repeatable, evidence-based signal that closes the loop faster than the market moves.
