# Evidura — Failure Story: UoM Commercialisation Blocks the Spin-Out

**Deep-dive scenario:** UoM Commercialisation demands standard terms (30-50% equity, board seat, first-refusal rights). Founder pushes back citing independence requirements. Negotiation stalls. 12-24 month independence timeline becomes 36-48 months. The window closes.

**Prepared by:** David Mulholland · **Date:** 2026-06-30
**Builds on:** [Independence structure](evidura-independence-structure.md) · [Business model](evidura-business-model.md) · [Brand strategy](compass-brand-strategy.md) · [Premortem transcript](premortem-transcript-20260630.md)

## 1. The Failure Story

### July 2026 — The first meeting

The meeting is cordial. David and Brad sit across from the UoM Commercialisation director in a glass-walled conference room on the Parkville campus. David walks through the independence structure document — the three options, the recommendation for a staged spin-out, the target of 10-20% UoM equity with no board seat, no operational control. He explains why independence is the product: if competitor universities perceive UoM control, they won't pay. If they don't pay, there is no venture.

The Commercialisation director listens. Takes notes. Then slides a document across the table.

It is the standard UoM Commercialisation term sheet. David has never seen it before. The key terms:

- **Equity:** 30-50% of the spin-out entity, calibrated to the value of the IP transferred and the university resources consumed during development.
- **Board seat:** One UoM-nominated director on the board of the spin-out entity, with full voting rights.
- **First-refusal rights:** UoM retains the right of first refusal on any future equity issuance, any asset sale, and any change of control.
- **IP licence:** The methodology is licensed to the spin-out on a royalty-bearing basis (typically 3-5% of gross revenue), revocable if the spin-out breaches the licence terms.
- **Non-compete:** The spin-out must not develop or commercialise any product that competes with UoM's core teaching or research activities.

David reads it twice. The equity range alone is three times higher than the independence plan's ceiling. The board seat contradicts the non-negotiable design principle — "no UoM employee rates a competitor's program" becomes meaningless when UoM sits on the board of the entity doing the rating. The first-refusal rights mean UoM can block any future capital raise, any strategic partnership, any acquisition — effectively a veto over the company's future.

Brad asks: "Is this negotiable?"

The Commercialisation director's answer is careful but firm: "These are the standard terms. The IP was developed on university time, by a university employee, using university resources. The university has a fiduciary duty to secure fair value for its assets. We can discuss calibration within the ranges, but the structure — equity stake, board representation, IP protection — reflects university policy."

The meeting ends with a commitment to reconvene in August.

### September 2026 — The framework reply

David submits a detailed response. It runs to 14 pages. The core argument is structural, not financial:

1. **Equity above 20% destroys the product.** A university holding 30-50% of a rating agency that rates competing universities is not an investor — it's a controlling shareholder. Every competitor university will ask: "What percentage does Melbourne own?" The answer at 30-50% makes the independence claim laughable. The venture's entire value proposition collapses at the equity level before any customer is invoiced.

2. **A board seat is an independence disqualifier.** The independence structure document's non-negotiable design principle #2 states: "No UoM board seat." This is not a preference — it's a structural requirement. A UoM-nominated director receiving board papers that include competitor universities' confidential benchmarking data, scoring methodologies, and commercial pipeline is an independence violation that any competitor's General Counsel will identify in minutes. The argument is not "we'd prefer not to have a UoM board seat" — it's "the product cannot exist with one."

3. **First-refusal rights are a capital-raising blockade.** The business model requires $6M–$27M in growth capital to scale internationally (see premortem findings). Any sophisticated investor — venture capital, growth equity, strategic partner — will refuse to invest in a company where the university incubator can veto every capital event. First-refusal rights don't just give UoM the option to invest — they give UoM the power to *prevent anyone else from investing*. The effect is to make the company unfundable.

4. **The "fair value" argument misunderstands the asset.** Commercialisation is valuing the IP as though it were a licensable patent — a discrete asset with standalone value. But the DFVA methodology's value is entirely contingent on *independence*. The IP is worth $0 if UoM controls it — because no competitor university will pay for a rating from a UoM-controlled assessor. The choice facing Commercialisation is not between "30-50% of a valuable venture" and "10-20% of a valuable venture." It is between "10-20% of a viable venture" and "100% of an unsellable one."

David proposes a counter: 10% equity (reflecting the incubation investment), zero board seat, no first-refusal rights, a capped royalty (2% of gross revenue for the first five years, 1% thereafter), and a published independence charter codifying these terms. The counter acknowledges UoM's legitimate interest — the IP was developed here, the resources were real — while preserving the venture's ability to function.

### November 2026 — The stall

Commercialisation does not respond to the 14-page submission for eight weeks. When the reply finally arrives, it is three paragraphs:

> *"We have reviewed your proposal. While we understand the independence argument, the university's standard spin-out framework reflects decades of commercialisation experience across hundreds of ventures. The terms you are proposing — minority equity with no governance rights, no board representation, and no structural protections — would represent a significant departure from established policy. We are not currently in a position to recommend such a departure to the Commercialisation Committee. We suggest further discussion early in the new year."*

The new year is three months away. The staged spin-out plan assumed Stage 1 (legal separation, incorporation, advisory board) would be complete by December 2026. It is now November. Nothing has been signed. Nothing has been incorporated. The independent advisory board candidates David approached informally in August are asking whether there's actually a venture to advise.

### February 2027 — The negotiation resumes, on Commercialisation's terms

The "early new year" meeting happens in mid-February. Commercialisation has prepared a revised term sheet. The concessions:

- **Equity reduced to 25%** (from the 30-50% range). The director frames this as "significant movement" — and within the internal logic of university commercialisation, it is. Most spin-outs accept the standard range.
- **Board seat maintained.** The rationale: "UoM needs visibility into the venture's performance to discharge its fiduciary obligations as a shareholder. An observer seat is not sufficient."
- **First-refusal rights narrowed** — now limited to equity issuances only (not asset sales or change of control). "We've removed the most restrictive elements," the director says.
- **Royalty reduced to 3%** (from the standard 3-5% range).

David does the math silently. 25% equity is still above the 20% ceiling in the independence plan. The board seat is still there. The first-refusal rights still block external capital. The concessions are real within the logic of university commercialisation — but they make no difference to the venture's viability. A competitor university will ask the same question at 25% as at 50%: "What percentage does Melbourne own?" A UoM board director reading confidential benchmarking data is equally disqualifying whether the equity is 25% or 50%. A first-refusal right on equity issuances alone is still sufficient to block venture capital.

The counter-offer is 10% equity, observer seat (not board), no first-refusal. Commercialisation says no.

The negotiation stalls a second time.

### May 2027 — The Go8 design partners drift

The independence question is now visible to the market. Three of the five Go8 design partners who received free pilot ratings have been waiting since mid-2026 for the independence structure to resolve. They were told: "Stage 1 by December 2026, paid subscriptions by mid-2027." It is now May 2027. The entity still doesn't exist. The founder is still a UoM employee. The pilot ratings still carry a University of Melbourne letterhead.

One DVC-Academic emails David privately:

> *"David — we've been patient because we believe in the methodology. But my CFO is asking hard questions. You're asking us to commit budget to a rating from an entity that doesn't exist yet, built by a methodology owned by our competitor, delivered by someone on their payroll. I can defend the concept. I can't defend the structure. I need something to show procurement — an ABN, an independent board, a governance charter. I can give you until mid-year. After that, I have to deprioritise."*

The "mid-year" deadline passes. No ABN exists. The deprioritisation becomes a withdrawal.

### August 2027 — The methodology governance charter is published without an entity to govern

David publishes the methodology governance charter — the scoring protocol, revision cycle, appeals process, and independence safeguards — as a standalone document on a University of Melbourne web page. It is well-received academically. It is also a strategic error.

The charter was designed to be published by an independent entity, as evidence of structural independence. Published by a UoM employee on a UoM domain, it reads as exactly what it is: a UoM project claiming to be independent while visibly remaining inside the institution. The charter's strongest lines — "no single institution shall hold more than 15% of annual revenue," "the board shall include no employees of any rated institution" — are read by the sector as aspirational, not structural. They are promises from someone who hasn't yet built the structure to keep them.

A senior administrator at another Go8 university forwards the charter to David with a one-line note: *"This is good. When it's real, let us know."*

### October 2027 — Commercialisation escalates internally

The delay has now drawn attention beyond the Commercialisation office. The Deputy Vice-Chancellor (Research) is briefed. The briefing note frames the situation as a governance concern: a university employee is attempting to transfer university-developed IP to an external entity on non-standard terms that would reduce the university's stake below policy minimums. The note does not mention the independence argument. It does not mention that the venture's viability depends on those non-standard terms. It frames the issue as *asset protection*, not *venture creation*.

The DVC-R directs Commercialisation to "resolve the matter within existing policy frameworks." This is a polite way of saying: no exceptions.

### January 2028 — The 18-month mark: a deal, but not the deal

After 18 months of negotiation — three rounds of term sheets, two escalations, one DVC-R intervention — a compromise is reached:

- **Equity: 20%** (the absolute floor of what Commercialisation can recommend to the Committee)
- **Board seat: yes**, but as a non-voting observer for the first two years, converting to a full voting director thereafter
- **First-refusal: retained** on equity issuances only, but with a carve-out for venture capital rounds above $2M
- **Royalty: 2%** of gross revenue for the first five years, 3% thereafter

David signs. He has no choice. Another round of negotiation would push incorporation past mid-2028. The Go8 design partners are down to one — a single institution still willing to engage. The pipeline that was supposed to produce 3-5 paying customers by now has produced zero. The window is closing, and this deal, imperfect as it is, at least allows the entity to exist.

Evidura Pty Ltd is incorporated in February 2028. Stage 1 — originally targeted for December 2026 — is complete. Fourteen months late.

### March 2028 — The poison terms activate

The deal that allowed the entity to exist also ensures it cannot function.

**The board seat, even as an observer.** The UoM observer attends the first independent advisory board meeting in March 2028. The agenda includes: the benchmark dataset (containing scores for 41 UoM programs and 12 programs from other institutions), the commercial pipeline (3 prospective customers, all Go8 competitors), and the draft pricing for the confidential benchmarking service. The observer takes notes. The observer is a UoM employee. The observer reports to UoM Commercialisation.

One of the independent advisory board members — a retired DVC-Academic from another Go8 — raises the issue in the meeting's final five minutes: *"I need to flag a concern. I sit on this board because I believe in independent assessment. But I'm being asked to review commercially sensitive data about competitor institutions while a University of Melbourne employee is in the room taking notes. This is not sustainable. If UoM has access to this data, I cannot in good conscience participate, and I don't believe any competitor university would consent to being rated under these conditions."*

The observer seat — the compromise that allowed the deal to close — is the mechanism that prevents the venture from operating.

**The 20% equity threshold.** In September 2028, Evidura approaches two venture capital firms for seed funding. Both ask the same question: "What's the university's stake?" At 20%, the answer is uncomfortable but survivable — above the independence ceiling, but below the level that would kill a term sheet. The killer is the follow-up: "Does the university have board representation?" Yes — an observer now, a voting director in 18 months. "Does the university have first-refusal on equity rounds?" Yes — above $2M. Both VCs pass. One partner explains: *"We don't invest in companies where a non-investor institution can convert observer rights to board control and block our follow-on. The governance structure makes this unfundable. Come back when the university's role is purely equity with no governance."*

David cannot change the governance structure. The term sheet is signed. The university's role is contractually locked.

**The non-compete clause.** In December 2028, Evidura proposes a partnership with a UK-based curriculum analytics platform — a natural complement to the durability assessment. The platform would embed Evidura scores alongside its own analytics; Evidura would gain UK distribution without building a UK sales team. UoM Commercialisation reviews the proposal under the non-compete clause and determines that the UK platform's analytics *could* be used for internal UoM curriculum review — triggering the non-compete's prohibition on partnerships that "compete with UoM's core teaching or research activities." The partnership is blocked. The expansion into the UK market — which the business model identified as the primary international growth vector — cannot proceed.

### June 2029 — The 36-month mark: the venture is dying

Three years after the first Commercialisation meeting. The staged spin-out plan targeted full independence at Month 18-24. The venture is now at Month 36 and is still not independent:

- The founder is still a UoM employee (now at 0.4 FTE — the transition to part-time happened, but the transition to full resignation is blocked by the equity negotiation's unresolved governance issues).
- UoM holds 20% equity, has an observer on the board (converting to voting director in 12 months), and retains first-refusal on equity rounds above $2M.
- The independent advisory board has lost two of its three original members — one resigned over the observer-seat issue, the second declined reappointment citing "governance concerns."
- The Go8 design partner network has collapsed. Of the original five, zero remain engaged. One is now piloting Lightcast Skillabi.
- Total revenue to date: $38,000 — two single-program assessments from a mid-tier university, purchased as a curiosity, not a commitment.
- The benchmark dataset covers 53 programs across 3 institutions — UoM, plus the two single-program purchases. It is not a cross-institutional benchmark. It is a UoM dataset with three outliers.
- The methodology governance charter — published 22 months ago on a UoM domain — is now cited in academic papers as "a UoM framework," not an independent standard.

### September 2029 — Lightcast enters Australia

Lightcast announces Skillabi for Australian universities. The offer: free institutional licences for three years, full course and syllabus analysis, dedicated APAC customer success team. The announcement references "the growing demand for curriculum-alignment tools in the Australian higher education sector" — a category Lightcast can now claim to have created, even though Evidura has been working on it for three years.

The email arrives in David's inbox from a colleague at a Go8 university: *"Have you seen this? This is what you've been building, right? But free?"*

David has been building it for three years. The entity has existed for 19 months. It has $38,000 in revenue, a governance structure that prevents capital raising, a board observer who reports to the competitor, a non-compete clause that blocks international expansion, and no path to independence before the end of 2030 — by which time Lightcast will have been free in the Australian market for 15 months.

### December 2029 — The window closes

The venture is formally wound down. The 48-month journey from first Commercialisation meeting to closure can be summarised in three numbers:

- **30-50%:** the equity range Commercialisation demanded at the first meeting (July 2026).
- **20%:** the equity they accepted as a "significant concession" (January 2028).
- **$38,000:** the total revenue generated across the venture's entire existence.

The independence plan's prediction — "UoM equity >20% or a board seat destroys the product" — was correct. The plan's negotiating position — "UoM's choice is between 10-20% of a viable venture and 100% of an unsellable one" — was also correct. The choice was made. The venture died not because the market rejected the product, but because the university that incubated it could not accept the terms required for it to live independently.

The DFVA methodology is absorbed back into the University of Melbourne as an internal research tool. The brand, the domain, the certification seal, the MCP infrastructure — all mothballed. The Australian curriculum-durability market now belongs to Lightcast, which offers the product for free, answers to KKR, and has no independence problem because it has never claimed to be independent.

## 2. The Underlying Assumption That Broke

### The explicit assumption

The independence structure document acknowledges the risk in its final section:

> *"UoM equity negotiation stalls — commercialisation office demands >20% or board seat. | Negotiating position: the venture's entire value depends on independence. UoM equity >20% or a board seat destroys the product. UoM's choice is between 10-20% of a viable venture and 100% of an unsellable one."*

This is framed as a **risk** with a **mitigation** — the negotiating position. The underlying assumption is that the negotiating position will work: that UoM Commercialisation, when presented with the logic that the venture's value depends on independence, will accept non-standard terms because the alternative (no venture) is worse for the university than a modest equity stake.

The deeper assumption — never stated explicitly but visible in the structure of the independence plan — is that **UoM Commercialisation will behave like a venture investor, optimising for the venture's success.** The plan assumes that Commercialisation's incentive is to maximise the probability of a viable spin-out, because a viable spin-out is the only scenario in which the university's equity has any value.

This assumption is wrong at the institutional level. UoM Commercialisation is not a venture investor. It is a university administrative unit with a fiduciary duty to protect university assets, operating within a policy framework designed for conventional IP commercialisation (patents, licences, research tools). Its incentives are not aligned with venture success — they are aligned with policy compliance and asset protection.

### The structural misalignment

The conflict between the venture's requirements and Commercialisation's mandate is structural, not personal. The individuals in the Commercialisation office are competent, professional, and acting in good faith within their institutional remit. The problem is that their remit cannot accommodate what the venture needs:

| What the venture needs | What Commercialisation's mandate requires |
|---|---|
| **Equity ≤ 20%** (above this, competitors won't pay) | **Equity 30-50%** (standard policy for IP developed on university time with university resources) |
| **No board seat** (a UoM director seeing competitor data destroys trust) | **Board representation** (fiduciary duty to monitor the university's equity investment) |
| **No first-refusal rights** (they block venture capital) | **First-refusal rights** (standard IP protection — prevents the IP being sold or diluted without university consent) |
| **Non-compete: none, or narrow** (the venture must be free to partner and expand) | **Non-compete: broad** (protects the university from its own IP being used to compete with its core activities) |
| **Fast resolution** (the independence clock is ticking; every month of delay damages the pipeline) | **Due process** (policy requires committee review, legal assessment, DVC-R approval — 6-12 months is standard) |

The venture's requirements are not amenable to "calibration within the ranges." The standard term sheet's ranges *are* the problem. Commercialisation can offer 25% instead of 30% — it's still above 20%. It can narrow first-refusal from three categories to one — it's still a capital blockade. It can defer the board seat from voting to observer — it's still a UoM employee in the room when competitor data is discussed.

The negotiating position assumed that Commercialisation would recognise the binary: "standard terms = dead venture." But Commercialisation's institutional logic does not permit that recognition. Within their framework, accepting non-standard terms is *itself* the failure — a breach of policy, a failure of asset protection, a precedent that weakens the university's negotiating position in every future spin-out. The venture's death is regrettable. A policy breach is a career-limiting event.

### Why the assumption was false

The assumption failed for three reasons:

**1. The IP was genuinely developed on university time with university resources.** The founder is a UoM Associate Director. The pilot assessments were run on UoM programs. The infrastructure was built on UoM servers. The methodology was refined through UoM-funded work. Commercialisation's position — "the IP belongs to the university, and the university is entitled to fair value" — is legally and ethically defensible. The venture's counter — "the IP is worthless without independence" — is strategically correct but legally irrelevant. The university owns the asset. The asset's market value is a problem for the spin-out, not the owner. If the asset can't be commercialised on standard terms, the university's policy response is "then it shouldn't be commercialised" — not "then we should accept non-standard terms."

**2. The "100% of an unsellable one" argument requires Commercialisation to believe the venture will fail.** This is a self-defeating negotiating position. To convince Commercialisation to accept 10%, the founder must argue that 30% kills the venture. But the founder *is* the venture — the person making the argument is also the person who would be responsible for the venture's success. Commercialisation hears "we'll fail if you take 30%" the way a bank hears "we can't repay the loan if the interest rate is too high." It's a negotiating tactic dressed as structural analysis. It may be true. It is also indistinguishable from a bluff.

**3. Commercialisation's decision-making timeline is fundamentally incompatible with venture timelines.** The venture needed Stage 1 (incorporation, advisory board, IP licence) by December 2026 — six months from the first meeting. Commercialisation's standard process for a spin-out IP transfer is 6-12 months *before any negotiation begins* — the preliminary assessment, the IP valuation, the conflict-of-interest review, the committee calendar. The venture's timeline assumed Commercialisation could accelerate a process that is designed to be deliberative. It could not. The first meeting was July 2026. The first counter-proposal was September 2026. The first response was November 2026. The first revised term sheet was February 2027. By the time the final deal was struck in January 2028, 18 months had passed — and the venture's 12-24 month independence window was almost entirely consumed by negotiation of terms that, even when finalised, were insufficient for independence.

## 3. Early Warning Signs

These signals were visible before the first Commercialisation meeting in July 2026. Each was either documented in the existing strategy materials or could have been identified through a single conversation with anyone familiar with university commercialisation processes.

### WARNING SIGN 1: The independence structure document itself identifies the risk

**Source:** Independence structure document, Risk table (line 145):

> *"UoM equity negotiation stalls — commercialisation office demands >20% or board seat. | Negotiating position: the venture's entire value depends on independence."*

**What it meant:** The plan acknowledges that Commercialisation demanding >20% or a board seat is not a remote possibility — it is a *named risk* with a probability assessed as high enough to warrant explicit documentation. The mitigation — "the negotiating position" — is not a structural countermeasure. It is an argument. Arguments do not reliably defeat institutional processes. The fact that the risk was named but not structurally mitigated (e.g., by securing DVC-R pre-approval for non-standard terms before the first meeting, or by establishing a separate IP ownership structure during development) meant the venture was walking into a negotiation with a known high-probability failure mode and no structural defense against it.

**What should have happened:** Before the first Commercialisation meeting, the founder should have (a) briefed Brad and the DVC-R on the independence requirement and secured pre-commitment to non-standard terms, (b) obtained a written opinion from an external university commercialisation expert confirming that standard terms are incompatible with a rating-agency spin-out, and (c) prepared a fallback: if Commercialisation won't accept ≤20% with no board seat, the venture moves to Option C (stay internal, academic research only) rather than burning 18 months in a negotiation that produces unworkable terms.

### WARNING SIGN 2: No one in the venture team had university commercialisation experience

**Source:** The independence structure document was written by the founder (David Mulholland, Associate Director, Service Experience & Design). The business model was written by the founder. The brand strategy was written by the founder. There is no evidence in any document of a team member with prior experience navigating a university IP commercialisation process.

**What it meant:** The venture was designed by someone who understood the product, the market, and the independence requirement — but not the institutional machinery that would determine whether the venture could exist. University commercialisation is a specialised domain with its own policy frameworks, incentive structures, timelines, and vocabulary. Approaching Commercialisation without someone who has done it before is like approaching a patent office without a patent attorney — the forms will be filled out, the arguments will be made, but the outcome will be determined by rules the applicant doesn't know exist.

**What should have happened:** Before the first Commercialisation meeting, the venture should have engaged (formally or informally) someone who had previously taken a university spin-out through UoM Commercialisation — a founder, a commercialisation consultant, or a university IP attorney. A single two-hour conversation with a survivor of the process would have revealed: (a) the standard term sheet ranges, (b) the 6-12 month minimum timeline, (c) the committee structure and escalation path, and (d) the near-impossibility of securing non-standard terms without DVC-level sponsorship. The independence plan would have been built around these constraints rather than colliding with them.

### WARNING SIGN 3: The venture's independence timeline assumed Commercialisation would move at venture speed

**Source:** Independence structure document, Stage 1 milestones (lines 110-117):

> *"- [ ] Evidura Pty Ltd incorporated*  
> *- [ ] Independent advisory board (3 members) seated*  
> *- [ ] DFVA methodology licence agreement executed (UoM → Evidura)*  
> *- [ ] Revenue concentration cap codified in constitution*  
> *- [ ] Conflict-of-interest disclosure filed and accepted*  
> *- [ ] First draft of methodology governance charter published*"

**Target: December 2026** — six months from the first Commercialisation meeting in July 2026.

**What it meant:** The timeline assumed that incorporation, IP licence execution, and advisory board seating could all happen within six months of the first conversation with Commercialisation. In reality, the IP licence alone — the agreement that transfers the DFVA methodology from UoM to Evidura — requires: IP valuation (internal or external), Commercialisation Committee review (quarterly calendar), legal drafting (both sides), DVC-R approval, and University Council notification for transactions above a threshold. Six months is the *minimum processing time* if every stage proceeds without objection. If Commercialisation objects to the terms — and the independence structure document itself predicts they will — 12-18 months is realistic.

**What should have happened:** The venture timeline should have been built around two scenarios: (a) Commercialisation accepts non-standard terms (best case: 12 months to Stage 1), and (b) Commercialisation insists on standard terms (base case: 18-24 months to Stage 1, with the outcome likely to be commercially non-viable). Scenario (a) should have been treated as the upside, not the plan. The Go8 design partner conversations, the pipeline projections, the revenue timeline — all of these were built on a six-month Stage 1 that was never achievable.

### WARNING SIGN 4: The venture's IP ownership was structurally unfixable — it was developed on university time with university resources

**Source:** Independence structure document, "The Problem" (lines 11-13):

> *"Evidura's value proposition is independent, trusted assessment of degree durability. But it was built inside the University of Melbourne, by a UoM employee, using UoM resources, with UoM programs scored first."*

**What it meant:** This sentence is the venture's origin story — and it is also the commercialisation trap. The IP was developed under conditions that give the university an unambiguous ownership claim. The university did not fund a speculative research project that happened to produce commercialisable IP. It employed a senior staff member who, in the course of their employment, developed a methodology using university infrastructure, tested it on university data, and refined it through university-funded work. Every element of UoM's standard term sheet — high equity, board representation, first-refusal, non-compete — is designed for exactly this scenario: IP developed by an employee on university time.

The venture's only argument for non-standard terms is that the standard terms make the venture non-viable. But that argument requires the university to accept that (a) the venture *would* be viable on non-standard terms, (b) the venture *will not* be viable on standard terms, and (c) the university should therefore accept a reduced return. This is a three-part argument where every part is speculative, is being advanced by the person who stands to benefit from the non-standard terms, and requires the university to deviate from policy. The probability of this argument succeeding in a policy-driven institutional process approaches zero.

**What should have happened:** The IP ownership problem should have been addressed at the point of development, not the point of commercialisation. Options that were available during development but are not available now include: (a) developing the methodology under a separate research agreement that pre-specified IP ownership terms, (b) filing a provisional patent or copyright registration in the founder's name (with UoM's knowledge and consent) before using university resources, (c) building the methodology on personal time with personal resources to establish a co-ownership claim, or (d) securing a DVC-level agreement on spin-out terms *before* development reached the pilot stage. By the time the venture approached Commercialisation, the IP ownership was settled in the university's favour — and the negotiation was about how much the university would extract, not whether it owned the asset.

### WARNING SIGN 5: The premortem's revised plan already called for resolving independence "before any competitor university is asked to pay" — but the venture was planning to ask competitor universities to pay in Year 2

**Source:** Premortem transcript, Synthesis — Revised Plan (line 121):

> *"2. Resolve independence before any competitor university is asked to pay (founder resigns from UoM or venture transferred)."*

**What it meant:** The premortem — conducted the same day as the independence structure document — explicitly recommended that independence be fully resolved before any external invoice. The revised business model maintains a Year 2 revenue target of 5-8 institutions paying for confidential benchmarking — but the independence plan targets Stage 3 (full independence, founder resigned) at Month 18-24, which is mid-to-late Year 2. There is a timing gap: the venture plans to invoice competitor universities before independence is complete.

If Commercialisation delays push Stage 3 from Month 24 to Month 36 or beyond — which this failure scenario predicts — the gap becomes a chasm: competitor universities are being asked to pay for assessments from an entity that still has UoM equity above the independence ceiling, still has UoM board representation, and still has a founder who draws a UoM salary. The premortem's recommendation and the venture's revenue timeline were in tension from the start. The Commercialisation delay turns the tension into a fatal contradiction.

**What should have happened:** The revenue timeline should have been built around the independence timeline, not the other way around. If Stage 1 takes 12-18 months (realistic), and Stage 3 takes 24-36 months (with Commercialisation delays), then external revenue should not be projected before Year 3 at the earliest. The business model's Year 2 revenue target of $375K–$700K should have been explicitly contingent on Commercialisation accepting non-standard terms within six months — a contingency with near-zero probability.

### WARNING SIGN 6: The venture had no executive sponsor with authority to overrule Commercialisation

**Source:** Independence structure document, Immediate Next Steps (line 104):

> *"1. Brief Brad on the independence requirement and the three options. Get alignment on Option B."*

Brad is identified as the Director. But the Director of Service Experience & Design does not have the authority to direct Commercialisation to accept non-standard terms. That authority sits with the Deputy Vice-Chancellor (Research) or the Vice-Chancellor. The independence plan assumes that Brad's support, combined with the logic of the independence argument, will be sufficient to influence Commercialisation. It will not be. Commercialisation reports to the DVC-R, not to the Director of Service Experience & Design. Brad can support the venture. He cannot direct its counterparty.

**What it meant:** The venture entered the most consequential negotiation of its existence — the terms on which it would be allowed to exist — without an executive sponsor who could break a deadlock. If Commercialisation says "standard terms," and the venture says "non-standard terms or no venture," there is no one in the room who can resolve the impasse. The DVC-R, who *could* resolve it, will only become involved if the matter is escalated — and when it is escalated (as it was in this failure scenario, in October 2027), the framing will be Commercialisation's framing (asset protection, policy compliance), not the venture's framing (independence requirement, venture viability).

**What should have happened:** Before the first Commercialisation meeting, the venture should have secured DVC-R sponsorship. This requires: (a) a briefing that frames the independence requirement not as a negotiating position but as a structural constraint that Commercialisation policy cannot accommodate, (b) a request for the DVC-R to direct Commercialisation to negotiate within parameters that preserve viability (≤20% equity, no board seat, no first-refusal), and (c) if the DVC-R cannot provide that direction, an acceptance that Option C (stay internal) is the only viable path. Without DVC-R sponsorship, the venture walked into a negotiation where the counterparty had both policy and hierarchy on its side — and the venture had neither.

## 4. The Structural Vulnerability

The failure reveals a vulnerability that is specific to university-incubated ventures whose value proposition depends on independence from the incubating institution:

> **The institution that owns the IP also owns the mechanism that determines whether the venture can exist — and the institution's mechanism is optimised for asset protection, not venture creation.**

UoM Commercialisation's policies are not unreasonable. They are designed to protect university assets, ensure fair value for publicly funded IP, and prevent employees from transferring university-developed intellectual property to private entities on favourable terms. These are legitimate institutional objectives. The policies work well for conventional spin-outs: a research lab develops a patentable technology; a spin-out company licenses the patent; the university takes equity and a board seat to protect its interest; the venture succeeds or fails on the strength of the technology, not on the perception of its independence.

The Evidura case is different — and the difference is not a flaw in Commercialisation policy. It is a category mismatch. Evidura is not a technology spin-out. It is an independence-dependent service whose value is destroyed by the very governance structures that Commercialisation policy requires. The venture's asset (the methodology) and the venture's constraint (the independence requirement) are in direct conflict with the institution's mechanism for commercialising assets and protecting its interests.

The structural vulnerability is that there is no institutional pathway for a venture like Evidura. Commercialisation policy does not have a "reduced equity for independence-dependent ventures" provision. The DVC-R does not have a "waive governance rights when governance rights destroy the product" delegation. The University Council does not have a "treat this spin-out differently because the standard terms make it non-viable" precedent. The venture is asking the institution to create a new category — and institutions do not create new categories in response to a single employee's venture proposal. They apply existing categories and let the venture succeed or fail within them.

## 5. What Would Have Prevented This Failure

For completeness, the conditions under which this failure could have been avoided:

| Intervention | What it means | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-development IP agreement** | Secure a DVC-level agreement on spin-out terms (≤10% equity, no board seat, no first-refusal) *before* developing the methodology on university time with university resources | Medium — requires the founder to anticipate the independence requirement and the commercialisation conflict at a point when the venture is still a concept, not a pilot. Rare in practice, but possible with experienced founders. |
| **DVC-R sponsorship before Commercialisation engagement** | Brief the DVC-R on the venture's independence constraint and secure a directive to Commercialisation to negotiate within viability-preserving parameters | Low-Medium — requires the DVC-R to accept that standard policy cannot accommodate this venture, which is a significant institutional ask. Easier if the venture has external validation (e.g., interest from other Go8s, a term sheet from an external investor). |
| **External ownership of IP from inception** | Develop the methodology on personal time with personal resources, establish independent IP ownership, and approach UoM as a licensing partner rather than an incubator | Low — the founder is a full-time UoM employee. Developing a methodology that uses UoM data, tests on UoM programs, and draws on UoM-funded work while maintaining personal IP ownership is legally complex and would require UoM's informed consent from the start. |
| **Accept Option C (stay internal) and spin out only if Commercialisation accepts non-standard terms** | Treat Commercialisation's acceptance of ≤20% with no board seat as a hard gate, not a negotiation. If the gate is not passed in the first meeting, the venture stays internal (academic research only). No 18-month negotiation. No compromised terms. | High — this is a decision the founder can make unilaterally. The cost is abandoning the commercial venture unless and until the institution's policy framework changes. The benefit is not burning years in a negotiation that produces unworkable terms. |
| **Separate IP ownership at the pilot stage** | When the pilot showed promise (early 2026), negotiate IP transfer terms *before* further development increased the university's investment and therefore its claim. The earlier the negotiation, the lower the university's sunk cost and the weaker its negotiating position. | Medium — requires recognising the timing dynamic (the university's negotiating position strengthens with every month of development) and acting before the pilot's success makes the IP more valuable. |
