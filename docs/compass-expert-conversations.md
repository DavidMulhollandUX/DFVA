## 1. The discipline: how to run these so the data is real (The Mom Test)

The single biggest risk in these conversations is not that experts mislead us — it's that they are *polite*. People reflexively encourage ideas they think we're attached to. Encouragement feels like validation and is worthless as evidence. We are not here to be told the idea is good; we are here to try to **kill it** and see if it survives. The conversations that move the venture forward are the ones that hurt.

Five rules (adapted from Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test*):

1. **Talk about their world, not our idea.** Do not describe COMPASS. The moment we pitch, the data is contaminated — they start reacting to us instead of describing their reality. Frame every conversation as "I'm trying to understand how curriculum review is changing," not "I'm building a tool that…".
2. **Ask about the past, not the future.** "Would you use a durability score?" invites a free, meaningless yes. "Walk me through the last program review you sat in — what evidence was actually in the room?" yields fact. Hypotheticals are how people tell you what they *imagine* they'd do; past behaviour is what they *actually* did.
3. **Anchor on commitment already made — money, time, reputation.** What they've already paid for, fought for, or built a workaround around is real. What they say they'd *like* is not. "Have you ever spent budget to get labour-market evidence into a review?" beats any expression of interest.
4. **Treat compliments and hypotheticals as noise, not signal.** "That sounds really useful" is a red flag, not a milestone. Politely steer back to specifics: "When did you last hit that problem?"
5. **Go in trying to falsify.** The goal of every interview is to find the strongest *disconfirming* evidence. If five conversations all "validate" the idea, suspect we asked leading questions — not that we've found gold.

Apply these to **every** question in Validate's interview guides (A–E): if a question can be answered with an opinion about the future, rewrite it on the spot to ask about the last time the thing actually happened.

## 3. De-bias the questions already in the research plan

The [interview skeleton in Track 2](research-plan.md) is solid, but two of its questions are hypothetical/leading and will produce polite noise. Recommended swaps (keep everything else):

- **Replace** *"What would it mean for a curriculum team to have real-time intelligence on whether their program is producing graduates with the skills employers are hiring for?"* (invites imagined enthusiasm) → **with** *"In your most recent review, what did the team do when they needed labour-market evidence and didn't have it?"* (forces past behaviour). Keep the follow-up — *"What would stop that from being useful — trust, format, workflow, culture?"* — it's well-formed.
- **Replace** *"What would need to be true for labour market intelligence to be a routine input into curriculum review?"* (IR/Strategy) → **with** *"Has a curriculum team ever come to you for labour-market evidence? Walk me through what happened."* If they have, the routine-input question answers itself from fact; if they never have, that absence *is* the finding.

Everything else in the skeleton — especially the past-behaviour questions about what data was consulted and where it came from — should stay.
